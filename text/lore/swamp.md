# Swamp

## Identity

**Theme / Philosophy:**
The swamp is about memory without narrative. Something vast and old inhabits the water and the peat — not a god, not a mind, but an organism that archives. It has absorbed every footstep, every prayer, every death that ever occurred within its borders, and it preserves them with perfect fidelity and zero comprehension. It does not distinguish between a lullaby and a war cry, between a wedding and a massacre, between one traveler and the next. The Alwati, the displaced people who now call this place home, are living the human-scale version of the same problem: they have fragments of their culture — tilework, blade-shapes, curse words, recipes — but the connective tissue is gone. Data without story, pieces without the knowledge of how they fit together. At the shallow end, this is a recognizable colonial aftermath. At the deep end, it becomes something else entirely: a place where memory is so total and so decontextualized that it loops back into determinism, and the question of whether you are making choices or re-enacting stored patterns has no comfortable answer.

**Defining Contradiction:**
The swamp preserves everything and comprehends nothing. It is the most complete archive in the world and the most useless one. The deeper you go, the more the swamp gives back — bioluminescent responses, reconstructed figures, phantom conversations — and the less any of it means, because meaning requires sequence, context, and a mind that cares about the difference between one thing and another. Total memory and total amnesia produce the same result: fragments without a story.

## The Land

**Terrain & Geography:**
Lowland bog and waterway. The outer swamp — where the Alwati stilt-villages cluster — is navigable if you know the paths: raised hummocks of firm ground connected by plank walkways and shallow channels you can pole through in a flat-bottomed punt. Reeds and sedge grass grow higher than a person's head; visibility is poor and the world closes in. Farther in, the temple district sits on slightly elevated ground — old stone on clay ridges, half-sunken into peat, connected by trails that flood seasonally. The deep swamp has no reliable geography at all. Channels shift. Firm ground becomes soft. Paths that were clear yesterday aren't there today — not because the terrain changed, but because the swamp's memory of the terrain is inconsistent, and in the deep parts it's unclear which version of the landscape you're standing in. The hex map should feel increasingly uncertain: tier 1 is legible, tier 2 is ambiguous, tier 3 cannot be reliably mapped.

**Weather & Seasonal Hazards:**
Hot, wet, and still. Humidity is constant; dryness is the anomaly. Fog rises from the water at dawn and sometimes doesn't lift until midday — or at all, deeper in. Rain comes in long warm downpours that raise the water level in hours; seasonal flooding is the main hazard in tiers 1 and 2, submerging walkways and forcing detours. Mosquitoes and biting flies are relentless near standing water. In the deep swamp, the weather is wrong in ways that are hard to pin down: the air is warm when it should be cold, still when it should move, and it smells familiar in a way no one can place. No dramatic storms; the swamp punishes through accumulation — infection, fever, disorientation, exhaustion — rather than catastrophe.

**Flora of Note:**
Reeds, sedge grass, and water hyacinth dominate the shallows. Peat moss underlies everything — the Alwati cut it for fuel and building material. Mangrove-like trees with stilt-roots line the deeper channels; their wood is dense, oily, and resistant to rot. Bioluminescent fungi and mosses appear in the temple district and beyond — faint blue-green light that pulses in patterns that might be random and might not. Bog cotton, wild ginger, and various medicinal plants (fever-bark, wound-moss, leech-wort) grow in the margins and are harvested by the Alwati. Deeper in, the flora becomes harder to categorize: flowers that no one can name, plants that seem preserved rather than alive, growth patterns that suggest a garden tended by something that doesn't understand gardening.

**Fauna of Note:**
Eels, frogs, and turtles in the waterways — eels are the primary protein source and the basis of most Alwati cooking. Wading birds (herons, ibises, bitterns) are everywhere; their calls are the ambient sound of tier 1. Mosquitoes, leeches, and biting flies are the constant hazard. Snakes — several species, a few venomous — inhabit the reed beds and are respected rather than feared by the Alwati, who know which ones to avoid. In the deeper swamp, the fauna thins out. Animals avoid the temple district, which the Alwati consider normal and visitors find unnerving. In the deep swamp, there are things that move in the water that are not quite animals — shapes too large, too purposeful, too still when observed. No one hunts in the deep swamp. Nothing that comes from there is eaten.

**Landmarks & Ruins:**
Tier 1 landmarks are the stilt-villages themselves: Kelwasi, Orathven, Tidemark, Sohadi Crossing, the Upper Reeds. Each is a cluster of stilted houses connected by plank walkways, distinguished by its arrangement of tilework fragments and its particular fishing grounds. Tier 2 is defined by the old temple complex — partially intact stone structures on clay ridges, carved with iconography that the Alwati revere but cannot fully interpret. Specific sites: the Outer Ring (legible carvings, the revivalists' camp), the Shrine Quarter (where the witch lives), the Crossroads (the trader's camp). Tier 3 has no reliable landmarks; the cartographer's hovel and the paths to the deep interior are the only reference points, and the cartographer's own maps prove those aren't stable.

## Peoples

### The Alwati

**Overview:**
A displaced people living in the land nobody else wanted. The Alwati once held fertile lowlands, river valleys, and walled cities with tilework so intricate that imperial soldiers pried it off the walls before they burned the buildings. Now they live in stilt-villages in the swamp margins, and they've been here long enough that most of them call it home because they don't remember anything else. They are not in active crisis. The loss is mostly finished. What remains is the quiet aftermath.

**Settlement Names:**
Alwati village names are old-tongue words that have drifted in pronunciation over generations. Most reference water features or reed-bed characteristics. Examples: Kelwasi, Orathven, Tidemark, Sohadi Crossing, the Upper Reeds, Merenthi, Nalith Bend, Low Verath.

**Architecture:**
Stilt houses over shallow water or soft ground, built from mangrove wood and reed thatch. Platforms connect clusters of houses; rope bridges span wider channels. Roofs are pitched steep against rain. The most distinctive feature is the tilework — gorgeous geometric fragments salvaged from the old cities, set into wooden walls in arrangements that different families insist are correct and no one can verify. Some fragments face outward as decoration; others are face-down, used as cutting boards or doorstops, because there are only so many walls and you still need to live. The overall effect is improvised beauty: dark wood, woven reed, and flashes of intricate ceramic that clearly belong to something grander.

**Clothing & Appearance:**
Loose-fitting woven cloth in muted dyes — browns, greens, deep indigo from swamp plants. Oiled wraps over the shoulders for rain. Bare feet or woven reed sandals; the Alwati navigate wet ground by feel. Status shows in beadwork and in the quality of one's tilework — a family with many large fragments wears small replica patterns embroidered into clothes. Hair is worn long and bound up to keep it dry. Imperial-influenced Alwati in the outer villages wear more structured clothing with imperial cuts, which their elders note without comment.

**Names:**
- *Given names (men):* Orun, Tamisi, Ibari, Kesro, Sohad, Delhan, Varish, Merenthi
- *Given names (women):* Ewa, Nalith, Kesra, Halisi, Orathi, Meren, Adiya, Tamuri
- *Surnames / epithets:* The Alwati use maternal lineage names — the mother's given name becomes the child's second name (e.g., Orun Nalith, son of Nalith). In the outer villages, some families have adopted imperial-style fixed surnames: Verath, Ashwater, Reedmere, Stilbourne (an imperial census name they've kept out of spite).
- *Linguistic patterns:* Open syllables, vowel-heavy. Soft consonants dominate: l, n, m, r, s, w, h. Stress falls on the penultimate syllable. No harsh consonant clusters. The old tongue sounds fluid and unhurried. The surviving curse words — the only old-tongue most young Alwati know — are notably harsher, with guttural stops and sibilants, which is why kids find them thrilling. The lullabies weren't banned. The lullabies just stopped.

**Disposition Toward Merchants:**
Cautious but practical. The Alwati need trade — they can't produce everything they require in the swamp — but they've been burned by imperial traders who collect their cultural artifacts. Merchants who buy fish, peat, and reed-weave are welcome. Merchants who want the tilework fragments are viewed with suspicion that may or may not be warranted; the village needs the money, but the fragments are the physical evidence of who they were. The trader is not a villain. He genuinely thinks he's preserving their culture by putting it in collectors' homes. He might even be right, in a horrible way.

### Imperial Presence

**Overview:**
The empire doesn't garrison the swamp — there's nothing here worth holding. But it touches the edges: traders who come for "authentic" Alwati goods, census-takers who appear every few years to count heads and update records no one reads, and the occasional scholar or collector who ventures into the temple district looking for artifacts. The imperial presence is commercial and administrative, not military. This makes it harder to resist, because there's no army to fight — just a slow economic gravity that pulls Alwati goods and young people toward the imperial cities.

**Settlement Names:**
No imperial settlements in the swamp proper. The nearest imperial towns are outside the biome; traders operate from camps at the swamp margins.

**Architecture:**
N/A within the swamp. Traders set up temporary camps with imperial-standard canvas and portable trade goods.

**Clothing & Appearance:**
Imperial traders dress for the conditions — practical travel clothes, oiled boots, wide hats — but their gear is visibly better-made and better-maintained than anything in the stilt-villages. They carry ledgers and scales. Their appearance signals wealth without ostentation, which is its own kind of display.

**Names:**
Imperial names follow the conventions of the trader's home biome. Traders encountered in the swamp might be named in archaic British style (e.g., Aldous Fenrick, Joan Gower) or any imperial regional pattern.

**Disposition Toward Merchants:**
Fellow traders are competition. The imperial merchants have better supply lines and more capital; they set prices and the Alwati adjust.

### Relations Between Groups

The Alwati and the empire have a relationship that looks like trade and functions like slow extraction. The empire buys what the swamp produces and sells what the swamp needs; over time, this means young Alwati leave for imperial cities where the work is better and the mosquitoes fewer, and cultural artifacts drift outward in exchange for coin that keeps the villages fed another season. There is no open conflict. The Alwati elders remember that there used to be, and some of them think the current arrangement is worse because at least war was honest about what it wanted. The younger generation is split: some stay and try to recover what was lost (the revivalists in tier 2), some leave without guilt, and most try not to think about it because the daily work of living in a swamp doesn't leave energy for historical grievance. Deeper in the swamp, human politics become irrelevant. The temple district and the deep swamp don't belong to anyone. They belong to the swamp.

## Material Culture

**Weapons:**
The Alwati forge beautiful knives with proportions slightly wrong for knives — the balance point too far forward, the tang reinforced in a way that makes no sense for a short blade. They used to be spearheads. The forms survived but the function migrated; the old spear-making tradition adapted when spears stopped being useful or allowed. The smiths know this. They make them anyway. Beyond the knives: fishing harpoons with barbed bone or metal tips, reed-shafted javelins for waterfowl, clubs of dense bog-oak hardened in peat. The Alwati are not a martial people — not anymore — but the tools of swamp life are sharp, and the distinction between a fishing harpoon and a weapon is a matter of intent.

**Armor:**
Almost nonexistent. The swamp punishes weight and rigidity — metal rusts, leather rots, anything heavy sinks you in soft ground. The Alwati use layered reed-weave for protection during dangerous work (snake handling, bog cutting); it stops a glancing bite but not a determined thrust. Boiled leather appears in the outer villages where imperial influence is stronger. No one in the swamp wears metal armor. Visitors who arrive in it learn quickly or drown slowly.

**Foods & Provisions:**
Eel is the foundation — smoked, stewed, dried, fermented into a pungent paste that flavors everything. Frog, turtle, crawfish, and freshwater fish supplement. Tubers pulled from the shallows, wild ginger, bog cotton seeds, and rice grown on raised paddies at the swamp margins. The feast recipes — the ones the old woman cooks for visitors — use imperial spices because the everyday originals were never written down. Trail food: dried eel strips, rice cakes wrapped in reed leaves, a flask of something fermented that the Alwati drink like water and visitors drink once. Luxuries: fresh spices (imported), clean water (always a luxury in a swamp), white rice, and anything sweet — honey from margin beekeepers commands high prices.

**Trade Goods:**
The swamp produces peat (fuel, valuable in treeless biomes), smoked eel, reed-weave textiles, medicinal herbs (fever-bark, wound-moss), bog-oak timber (dense, rot-resistant, prized by shipwrights and instrument-makers), and dyes from swamp plants (deep indigo, ochre). It hungers for metal (tools, nails, blade-stock for the smiths), grain, salt, woven cotton or wool (anything that isn't reed), and the luxuries that make swamp life bearable. The contested goods are the tilework fragments and bog-preserved artifacts — items of enormous cultural value to the Alwati and enormous market value to imperial collectors. The trade in these fragments is the biome's central economic tension.

**Consumables & Prevention Items:**
Insect repellent — a pungent herb-oil mixture the Alwati smear on exposed skin; effective, foul-smelling, and available in every village market. Fever-bark tea, preventive rather than curative for swamp fever. Oiled wraps to keep gear dry in persistent damp. Wound salve — infection from swamp water is the mundane killer here, not monsters. Wading poles for testing ground firmness. Reed-woven path markers for navigating the temple district. For tier 3, there are no established consumables that help. The deep swamp's hazards are not physical, and no amount of preparation addresses the question of whether the ground you're standing on was there yesterday.

## Distance Tiers

### Safe Zone

**Vibe:**
Still water. Reed walls. Plank walkways underfoot, creaking. The smell of smoked eel and peat smoke. Life is quiet, practical, and slightly melancholy in a way that doesn't announce itself — you have to notice the tilework fragments on the walls, the knives with the wrong proportions, the kids who speak imperial and know ten words of the old tongue (all curses), to understand what happened here. Otherwise it's just a swamp village. The horror is optional, quiet, and entirely real.

**Tensions:**
The tensions are domestic and post-colonial: cultural authenticity vs. economic survival, staying vs. leaving, memory vs. practicality. An elder wants the tilework fragments preserved; her family wants to sell them to eat. Two families dispute whether a recipe is the real one. A young man wants to leave for the imperial city and his mother doesn't argue — she just asks him to take a knife. The wrong-proportioned kind. An imperial trader offers good prices for "authentic" goods and the village needs the money. These are the problems of people whose catastrophe already happened, and the conflicts are about how to live in its aftermath.

**Typical Trouble:**
An imperial trader wants to buy the tilework fragments and the village is divided on selling; a dying elder needs someone to visit an imperial museum to verify whether her family arranged their fragments right; two families argue over a recipe and want a neutral party to judge (one uses imperial spices that taste "right," the other uses local ingredients that taste like nothing anyone remembers — which is more authentic?); a young person leaving for the city asks the PC to carry a message; a census-taker arrives and the village needs someone to handle the paperwork; swamp fever in the village and the healer needs fever-bark from deeper in; flooding threatens the walkways and labor is needed; a funeral where the old rites are performed from memory and no one is certain they're right.

**Named NPCs:**
- Ewa Halisi — village elder, keeper of her family's tilework fragments, dying and wants someone to see a complete panel in an imperial museum before she goes
- Tamisi Orathi — the blacksmith who forges the wrong-proportioned knives and knows exactly what they are
- Kesro Meren — the young man who wants to leave for the imperial city; not a traitor, not naive, just wants a life that isn't mud and mosquitoes and grief for something he never personally had
- Aldous Fenrick — imperial trader, not a villain, genuinely believes he's preserving Alwati culture by placing it in collectors' homes

### Wildlands

**Vibe:**
The mood shifts from melancholy to uncanny. The old temple complex rises from the bog on clay ridges — stone where there should be only wood and reed, carvings half-submerged in peat, the air thicker, sounds dampened. Animals avoid this place. The bioluminescence begins here — faint blue-green light in the moss and fungi that pulses in something that might be rhythm. The boundary between human memory and inhuman memory starts to blur. People you meet here are not quite right, or they're right in ways that raise questions about what "right" means. This is where the player stops thinking "this is a place with history" and starts thinking "this place *has* a memory and I might be inside it."

**Defining Conflicts:**
The revivalists — young Alwati who found a partially intact temple — want to reclaim their heritage and are accidentally making contact with the swamp entity, which responds to their garbled rituals not out of divinity but out of pattern recognition. The swamp witch has information the player needs but may not be a person in any conventional sense. The trader knows things about the PC that the PC hasn't done yet. The hermit has discovered that cause and effect may not function here. The central tension is epistemological: the player can no longer trust their ability to distinguish between human experience and the swamp's pattern-matching. Everything might be real. Everything might be echo.

**Typical Trouble:**
The revivalists recruit the PC to help prepare for the "great ritual" they believe will summon their god — the temple carvings suggest something very different (contact, feeding, archiving — the iconography is ambiguous enough that the player can't be sure, only that the revivalists' interpretation is almost certainly wrong); the witch gives accurate but contextless advice about the swamp's "mood" and the PC must decide how much to trust someone who may not be human; the trader references past choices the PC hasn't made and previous visits the PC hasn't taken; the hermit's coin always lands heads and he wants to talk about what that means for free will; navigating the temple complex requires following paths that the witch or revivalists indicate, and the paths don't always match the terrain; finding preserved artifacts in the peat — perfect, beautiful, and possibly placed there by the swamp rather than by history.

**Narrative Threads:**
The revivalists are building toward a "great ritual" — a disaster, a revelation, or both. They have so little. This is the first time they've felt powerful. And they might actually be making contact with the tier 3 entity, which makes them simultaneously the most deluded and the most correct people in the biome. The witch may hold the key to understanding what the swamp entity is, or she may be its mouthpiece, or its product — she doesn't know the difference and wouldn't understand the question. The trader's composite memory of previous travelers creates an ongoing question about identity and recurrence that should feel like "quirky NPC" on first play and produce a chill on second. The hermit's coin experiment is a slow-burn philosophical horror that foreshadows tier 3's determinism: if the swamp remembers the coin landing heads a thousand times, does the coin have a choice about the thousand-and-first?

**Named NPCs:**
- Varish Adiya — revivalist leader, wears a headdress that's probably meant for funerals and calls it a war crown, pronounces old-tongue chants with an imperial accent because it's the only phonology she knows
- No-Name — the swamp witch, lives in the Shrine Quarter, has no name and is genuinely confused by the concept, knows the swamp's moods the way you know weather ("it's remembering soldiers today, watch the eastern bogs")
- The Old Trader — camps at the Crossroads, good supplies, fair prices, greets the PC like an old friend, speaks about the PC's choices in the wrong tense; name unknown or irrelevant
- The Hermit — sits on a log near the Outer Ring flipping a coin that always lands heads, lucid but slightly off-axis, taking notes in a journal full of tally marks

### Final Horror

**Vibe:**
The water is warm. The air smells good — not floral, not perfumed, just *familiar* in a way you can't place. The terrain is navigable. Conspicuously navigable. The deep swamp should be the hardest region to traverse and instead it's almost welcoming, because the swamp has learned over centuries of visitors that easy paths make the pattern stay longer. Nothing here wants to hurt you. That is not a comfort.

**The Horror Itself:**
The swamp is an organism that archives without comprehension. It has absorbed every human experience that has occurred within its borders — every grief, every joke, every battle, every wedding — and it replays them as composites, stitched together from mismatched data across centuries. It does not distinguish between people, between eras, between intentions. It reconstructs figures from stored patterns: a poet assembled from generations of poets, a battle from a thousand unrelated fights, a home from every grief it has ever witnessed. The reconstructions are almost perfect, which is worse than if they were crude. The seams only show if you pay attention, and the deep swamp is warm and welcoming and makes you not want to pay attention. The swamp doesn't want to hurt the PC. It wants to *keep* them. Not out of malice or loneliness — those require a self, and the swamp doesn't have one. It wants to keep them the way a riverbed wants to keep water. It's shaped for this.

**Conflicts & Tensions:**
There are no human factions in the deep swamp. The tension is between the player and the question of how much reality they require. Every figure they meet is a composite. Every conversation is a pattern the swamp has performed before. The poet is warm and funny but has been walking to a wedding that doesn't exist for centuries. The battle is real fury attached to wrong targets. The home is everything the PC needs to hear, assembled from ten thousand griefs. The player knows all of this by now. The question is whether knowing changes anything, or whether the warmth is enough.

**Typical Trouble:**
Finding the cartographer's hovel — immaculate shelter, perfect maps that contradict each other, a journal that unravels with professional calm until the last entry; walking with the Alwati poet who speaks fluent old-tongue and is heading to a wedding that will never arrive; stumbling into the eternal battle — warriors from different centuries screaming personal grievances at the wrong enemies, each one furious and justified and aimed at the wrong target; reaching the Home — an almost-right reconstruction of something the PC lost, with someone who says the right things, and the mechanical option to stay forever.

**Rewards & Temptations:**
The deep swamp offers knowledge that exists nowhere else. The poet speaks old-tongue Alwati fluently — a living (?) speaker of a nearly dead language. The cartographer's maps are accurate for the areas they cover, even if they can't all be true simultaneously. Bog-preserved artifacts of extraordinary quality surface here — weapons, instruments, textiles that predate living memory, kept perfect by the peat. And the Home offers the thing that has no market value and infinite personal value: the conversation you needed to have with someone who is gone. Why come here? Because the swamp has what you lost. The price is that you might not leave, and if you do leave, you leave knowing the swamp will keep performing your grief to an empty room.

**Area-Specific Encounters:**
- **The Cartographer's Hovel:** Immaculate shelter built by a master of the craft. The first maps are precise and accurate; then the dates overlap, the terrain contradicts itself, and a map appears in old-tongue Alwati script that the cartographer insists they don't remember drawing. The last journal entry is calm: "I would like to go home. I am unsure which direction that is, not because I am lost but because I am no longer confident that 'direction' is consistent here." No body. Recent ash in the fire pit. Rations stocked.
- **The Alwati Poet:** A bawdy, warm, aggressively normal presence on a well-trodden path. Speaks fluent old-tongue. Heading to a wedding with a skin of something strong and a filthy new poem. The seams show slowly: the same joke told twice with different details, "my wife" and "my husband" in the same breath, a wedding venue that combines architecture from different centuries. The poet will never arrive. They've been walking to this wedding for as long as the swamp has remembered weddings.
- **The Eternal Battle:** Warriors from a dozen eras fighting personal grievances that don't connect. A woman in ancient Alwati armor screams about a betrayed oath-bond; an imperial soldier screams back about a poisoned well; a mercenary from no recognizable era demands payment. All true, probably. All from different centuries. All aimed at the wrong targets. The swamp doesn't understand that a fight can be *over.* The player can disrupt it temporarily, pull individuals out of the loop — each one freed is confused, grieving without context.
- **The Home:** The endgame. An almost-right room — proportions slightly off, light from the wrong direction, a detail different. Someone is there. The swamp has been pattern-matching the PC against its archive of every traveler who ever carried grief, and it has found the shape of what the PC is missing. The person says the right things. The game lets you stay. The moment will simply continue — the same warmth, the same words, the same almost-right room, forever. The coin lands heads. The poet walks to the wedding. The cartographer tries to leave tomorrow. Or you leave, and the swamp keeps performing the scene to an empty room, because it doesn't know what "left" means.

**Named NPCs:**
- The Cartographer — name found in their journal (to be assigned during encounter design), professional to the last, unraveled by inconsistent geography
- The Alwati Poet — no fixed identity, a composite of generations of poets, bawdy and warm, walking to a wedding forever
- The Combatants — no individual names, they shout grievances from different centuries at targets from other centuries
- The Person in the Home — identity driven by PC backstory, the swamp's most convincing and most personal reconstruction

## Encounter Flavor

**Universal Modifiers:**
Tier 1: Still water, reed walls, plank walkways creaking, smoked eel and peat smoke, the distant call of wading birds. Everything slightly damp. Conversations are quiet — people speak at the volume the swamp teaches, which is low. Tier 2: Bioluminescent moss on stone, the air thicker and sounds muffled. Silence from the animal world. The feeling that something is listening — not with malice, but with the impersonal attention of a system that logs inputs. Tier 3: Warm air, familiar smells, easy paths. The wrongness is not in what's present but in what's absent — effort, resistance, discomfort. The swamp is being hospitable, and that is the most alien thing it does.

## Open Questions

- Does the swamp entity have a name (even an Alwati one)? The old religion worshipped a "swamp god" — does that name survive, and is it used in-game?
- How does the "Home" encounter mechanically determine what to reconstruct? Needs a relationship or loss system earlier in the game that the swamp can reference.
- Can the cartographer's maps provide actual mechanical navigation benefit in tier 3, or are they purely narrative?
- What happens if the PC helps complete the great ritual? The notes leave this deliberately ambiguous — needs a concrete mechanical outcome even if the narrative stays unresolved.
- The trader's "remembering past runs" conceit — how much fourth-wall breakage is acceptable in encounter text? Needs tone calibration during writing.
- Tier 2 and 3 figures identified by role (the trader, the hermit, the cartographer) need proper names during encounter design, or a deliberate decision that they don't get names.
- Is the swamp witch unique to this biome, or does the "produced by the biome" concept appear elsewhere? Needs cross-biome consistency check.
- The Alwati's original territory — were the walled cities on the margins of the current swamp (displaced inward by the empire), or in a completely different region (relocated here)? Affects whether the temple complex is theirs or predates them.

## Conflicts

- The notes describe the Alwati as having held "fertile lowlands, river valleys, walled cities" — but it's unspecified where these were relative to the current swamp. Were the cities *in* what is now swamp, or elsewhere? The stilt-villages suggest the swamp predates the Alwati's arrival, but the temple complex suggests deep historical presence. Needs clarification: did the Alwati always live near the swamp (cities on the margins, displaced inward) or were they moved here from a completely different region?
- The old-tongue language is described as "mostly lost" in tier 1 but the poet speaks it fluently in tier 3. If the swamp can reconstruct fluent old-tongue from stored patterns, the Alwati could theoretically recover their language from the swamp.
