## The People: The Revathi (self-name)

**Endonym:** *Revathi* (singular *revas*), roughly meaning "those who carry." The root *rev-* encodes a concept that doesn't cleanly split into "remember" and "carry" — it's both. You carry memory the way you carry weight. The implication is that identity is a *labor*, not a state.

The colonizers likely call them something else entirely — a flattened exonym, maybe derived from a place name they misheard. The Revathi name for themselves wouldn't appear in imperial records.

---

## The Ancient Tongue: Revathikh

The "deep speech," now surviving only in fragments — ritual phrases, place-names, lullabies half-understood by the people who still sing them. This is where the memory theme lives at the structural level.

**Phonology.** Built around a contrast between *aspirated* and *plain* consonants (not voiced/voiceless), with a vowel harmony system that groups sounds into "near" and "far" registers. The near register is used for lived/witnessed experience; the far register for inherited or dreamed knowledge. So the *sound* of a sentence tells you the speaker's relationship to the truth of it.

Core consonants: p, t, k, s, sh, m, n, r, l, v, th (dental fricative), kh (velar fricative), plus aspirated variants ph, th, kh. No voiced stops — nothing that "punches." The language sounds breathy, layered, a little evasive to outside ears.

Vowels: a, e, i, o, u in both near (plain) and far (shifted: ä, ë, î, ö, û — realized as centralized or raised variants). So *reva* ("I carry/remember [because I was there]") vs. *revä* ("I carry/remember [because it was given to me]").

**Grammar.** Polysynthetic and head-marking. Verbs are the spine of everything — you can build a complete sentence inside a single verb by stacking affixes. Critically, Revathikh has a **four-way evidentiality system** baked into every predicate:

- *-al* — witnessed (I saw/felt this)
- *-ën* — inherited (this was placed in me by someone who witnessed it)
- *-oth* — reconstructed (I've reasoned this from evidence)
- *-uri* — dreamed/revealed (this came to me unbidden, from a source I can't name)

There's no way to make a claim in Revathikh without declaring how you know it. And here's the thematic gut-punch: **there is no evidentiality marker for "I read this" or "I was told this by an authority."** Knowledge transmitted through institutional channels literally cannot be expressed in the deep grammar. It's not that they were primitive — it's that their epistemology didn't recognize that *category* as real knowledge.

**Word order** is free but pragmatically driven — topic-first. What you're talking *about* comes before what you're *saying* about it, which means the thing you consider important is always foregrounded.

**Sample glosses:**

- *Thîkërevänosh* — "The river carries-for-us (inherited knowledge) the name-of-the-dead." A single word. A prayer, essentially.
- *Sulal kethîmoth* — "I have seen the water; [therefore] the path is [reconstructed to be] here."

## The Modern Tongue: Revashu

"Revashu" literally means something like "carrying-talk" or "the speech that remains." It's what the Revathi actually speak now, and it bears the scars.

**Phonology.** The aspirated/plain contrast has collapsed in most dialects — younger speakers just use plain consonants. The vowel harmony is *dying*: most speakers use near-register for everything, switching to far-register only in fixed expressions they learned from elders. The phonemic inventory has shrunk. Some speakers have picked up voiced stops (b, d, g) from contact with the imperial tongue, and those sounds feel foreign in the mouth, used mostly for loanwords.

The language sounds "smaller" than Revathikh. Faster, more clipped. The breathiness is mostly gone.

**Grammar.** Still verb-heavy, but the polysynthesis is eroding. Complex verb forms have broken into analytic constructions — separate words for what used to be affixes. Crucially:

**The evidentiality system has collapsed to two markers:** *-al* (I know this from experience) and a generalized *-en* (I know this some other way). The inherited/dreamed/reconstructed distinctions have merged into a single "not-witnessed" category. This is the central linguistic tragedy: *the grammar that encoded the Revathi's entire relationship to memory has simplified to a binary.* Either you were there, or you weren't. The nuance of how knowledge travels between people — the thing that made their epistemology unique — is becoming inexpressible in their own language.

Some elders still use *-uri* (dreamed/revealed) and are understood, but younger speakers parse it as poetic or archaic, like "thee" in English.

**Loanwords and gaps.** Revashu has borrowed heavily from the imperial language for concepts related to technology, administration, and military force. But it has *no native word* for "own" in the property sense — possession is expressed as a kind of caretaking relationship (*veshkal*, "I hold-for-now"). Imperial loan-words for ownership exist but feel obscene, like a slur you use about yourself.

There are also **lexical ghosts** — words that survive but whose meanings have drifted because the cultural context evaporated. *Thîkë* once meant a specific kind of sacred river-memory. Now it just means "river." The sacred freight is gone but the sound remains, and some speakers feel an unease they can't explain when they say it.

**Sample glosses:**

- *Rev en ko shatal* — "I carry something-I-didn't-witness about this place." (Analytic where Revathikh would be synthetic.)
- *Keshî danel, vo keshî* — "The empire named it, so it is named." (A resigned proverb. *Keshî* is borrowed from the imperial tongue.)

## Design Notes for You

A few things I was trying to thread:

The linguistic erosion isn't presented as the Revathi being "degraded." Revashu is a living language — it's adapted, it's functional, people fall in love and tell jokes in it. The loss is real but it's not a narrative of pure decline. That matters for avoiding the "noble savage elegy" trap.

The evidentiality system gives you a concrete game-mechanical hook if you want one: NPCs who speak in the old forms are making epistemological claims the player can parse. An elder who uses *-uri* is telling you she dreamed something. That's *different* from *-ën* (someone placed this memory in me). Quest information delivered in different evidential frames could mean different things about its reliability.

The lack of a property-ownership concept isn't "they were so pure and communal" — it's that their ontology categorized the relationship between people and things differently. The imperial language isn't "corrupting" them; it's *imposing categories that don't fit*, and the friction is where the story lives.

Want me to go deeper on any piece of this — phonological tables, a fuller morphology sketch, naming conventions for people and places?

This is the right instinct. Morrowind's secret is that it trusts the player to do the work — you hear *n'wah* three times and you *know* what it means and it hits harder than any tooltip could. So let's build a tight kit.

## Living Names (Revashu)

Modern Revathi names follow a **two-part pattern**: a *personal name* (given at birth, short, usually two syllables) and a *carry-name* that indicates whose memory you bear. Not a surname — more like "I am the continuation of."

Structure: **[Name] vo [Carry-name]**

*Vo* means roughly "from-within," implying the memory lives inside you rather than that you descended from someone. It's not patronymic — you might carry the name of a grandmother, a dead friend of your mother's, or someone no one alive actually remembers anymore.

Some working names:

- *Telen voAsha* — a woman. Telen is a common modern name. Asha was someone.
- *Duri vo Kethlan* — a man. Blunt, short sounds — feels working-class in Revashu.
- *Sevan vo Mîrath* — notice the far-register vowel in the carry-name. Whoever Mîrath was, no one living witnessed them. This person carries a *very old* name and other Revathi would notice that.
- *Orel vo Thîkë* — this would raise eyebrows. Thîkë is the word for river, but it *used* to be a sacred concept. Carrying it as a name is either very traditional or slightly provocative, depending on who you ask.

**For game use:** NPCs introduce themselves with both parts. If they trust the player, they use just the personal name. If they're being formal or cold, full name. If an NPC drops the *vo* and just says their personal name to the player early, that means something.

## Active Settlements (Revashu)

Swamp settlements are named **descriptively and provisionally** — these were never supposed to be permanent, and the names reflect that. They use Revashu compound words, often blunt and a little grim.

- *Sulvesh* — "held water," i.e. a place where you can hold your ground against the wet. The closest thing to a capital.
- *Revashol* — "the carrying-place." Where elders gather to transmit memory. Has a ritual function. *(The -ol suffix implies witnessed/established.)*
- *Nakhem* — "still breathing." A settlement that has survived multiple attempts to dislodge it. The name is gallows humor that stuck.
- *Thalken* — "root-sit." Practical, almost dismissive. A newer camp. People live in the root structures of massive swamp trees.

**For game use:** The mundane practicality of these names tells the player everything. These people didn't *choose* this land. They're making do. Nobody names their forever-home "still breathing."

## Lost Cities and Holy Places (Revathikh)

These names should sound *different* — longer, more breath in them, aspirated consonants and far-register vowels that modern speakers don't naturally use anymore. When the player sees these on the map, they should feel older just from the phonology.

- *Thîkëravänoth* — the great river-city. A compound that encodes "the place where the river carries inherited memory." No modern speaker can say this without stumbling slightly. When Revathi mention it, they usually just say *Thîkë* and leave the rest implied.
- *Kephîralûn* — a mountain holy site, now in imperial territory. The *-ûn* suffix indicates dreamed/revealed knowledge — this place was founded because someone dreamed it. The name itself is a theological claim.
- *Ashëmîr* — a lost city whose name means something like "the-quiet-that-holds." A place of contemplation. Now an imperial administrative center with a different name, and the Revathi name for it appears on no official map.
- *Sothëvani* — a place of the dead. The word has no modern equivalent. Even the elders aren't sure if it was a physical cemetery or a concept. The name survives because it appears in lullabies.

**For game use:** If the player stumbles across these names carved into stone in the swamp, or hears an elder whisper one, the sheer *sonic difference* from the modern names signals deep time. You don't need a lore dump. The sound does the work.

## Invective (The Spice Rack)

This is where you get the most flavor per syllable. The goal is four to six words max that the player absorbs through context, Morrowind-style.

**Kesh** — (borrowed from *keshî*, the imperial language's word for itself). Used as an insult meaning roughly "empire-talker" or "one who speaks like a colonizer." Devastating when aimed at another Revathi. If an NPC calls the player *kesh*, it means "you talk like them." If a Revathi calls *another Revathi* kesh, it's close to a slur — you're accusing them of having been hollowed out. Usage: *"Don't bring me kesh coin and expect thanks."*

**Noth** — literally the evidentiality suffix for "reconstructed from evidence," stripped from a verb and thrown like a stone. Calling someone *noth* means "you weren't there, you don't know, you're guessing and presenting it as truth." It's an accusation of intellectual fraud. Short, sharp, easy to spit. Usage: *"You call yourself a guide? Noth."*

**Vesh-ka** — from *veshkal* (to hold-for-now), but the *-ka* suffix is diminutive/contemptuous. It means a person who clutches, who hoards, who holds things that shouldn't be held. It's the closest thing to calling someone "greedy" but it hits different — it implies a *spiritual* failing, a refusal to let things pass through you the way memory and goods are supposed to. Usage: *"Imperial vesh-ka built a wall around Ashëmîr like you can own grief."*

**Vo-sul** — literally "from-within water," meaning someone whose inner life has gone murky, unreadable. A lighter insult — more like "you're being shady" or "I can't read your intentions and I don't trust that." Casual enough to throw around. Usage: *"Vo-sul, that one. Watch your pack."*
