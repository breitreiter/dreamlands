The Spires

This was supposed to be paradise.

Past the scavenger fields and the last abandoned forts, the plains flatten into something else. The grass thins and gives up. The soil turns smooth and fused, like the land was fired in a kiln. In every direction, the domed city is visible—pale, clean-lined, and intact on the horizon, close enough to read as a destination and far enough to feel like it's watching. The area is small. There is nowhere here that the city is not part of the view.

This was someone's utopia. An advanced civilization built the domed city as a mercantile outpost on the edge of the plains—a clean, bright place where everyone lived inside the dome and the surrounding land was cultivated. Orchards, grain fields, vegetable gardens, all fed by engineered irrigation. Families walked the orchards in the evening. Children played in the fields and no one worried, because the Iron kept everyone safe. The Iron were protectors. Friendly, visible, dependable. They patrolled the perimeter so the farmers didn't have to think about danger. The city thrived for a time, and then it didn't—the outpost wasn't financially viable, too remote to sustain. The residents packed up and left. They left the Grid running. Why not? What if someone decided to come back someday? Better to keep the place secure than let it be overrun.

Nobody came back. The irrigation systems shut down. The orchards died. The fields dried out and the soil fused into glass and ceramic hardpan under decades of unfiltered sun. The land that was once lush and managed is now a flat, sterile expanse. Scattered across it are squat concrete and metal buildings—pump houses, junction boxes, switching stations, equipment sheds. Some are intact enough to shelter in. Others are just low walls and a slab. The pipes are dry. The channels are silted over or fused shut. These are the bones of the irrigation network, and they tell you what this place was: a garden. The craters came later, when the empire arrived. The ground is glass in places. In others it's pitted and spongy, like it was reorganized at a molecular level by someone who didn't quite understand molecules. There are craters so old they've become shallow bowls of dead earth, and at their centers, things that hum.

Red pylons mark the perimeter. They are tall, angular, and impossible to miss—a deep oxide red against the pale fused ground, visible from a considerable distance. They are warnings. The name "the Spires" comes from these—the locals mean the pylons, and the meaning is clear: you don't go near them. Beyond the pylons, the Grid is active: the city's automated defense network, still fully operational, still enforcing boundaries that once kept residents safe and now keep everyone else out. The Grid does not chase. It does not negotiate. It activates based on density thresholds, weapon signatures, heat signatures, and boundary crossing. It was built to protect civilians from external threats. The civilians are gone. The Grid doesn't know that. The pylons are the last courtesy—the last point where the system tells you to turn back before it stops asking.

The Iron are the worst part. They were built as guardians—protectors of orchards and children and evening walks—and they are still doing exactly what they were built to do. They just no longer have anyone to protect. Massive, loud, and mechanical, they clank across the fused ground on heavy limbs, servos whirring, joints grinding, pistons cycling with a rhythm that carries across the silent flats. They are not sleek. They are not elegant. They are industrial—thick-plated, riveted, built to function and maintain function indefinitely. Soviet futurism by way of an engineering culture that valued endurance over aesthetics. The sound of their movement—the steady clank-whir-clank of metal on glass—announces them long before they're visible. They don't attack. They don't acknowledge you. They execute routines written in a language that no longer exists, and if you're in the path of the routine, that's your problem, not theirs. They are not hostile. They are incompatible with your existence.

At the edges of the Spires, where the fused ground gives way to dead grass and packed earth, the remnants of the imperial advance litter the landscape. Sixty years ago, an imperial expeditionary unit stumbled into the Grid's perimeter and was destroyed. The empire interpreted this as an act of war and sent two legions—the 7th and 12th—to take the city. The Iron met them in the fields. Friendly protective robots, designed to keep children safe, annihilated two legions in weeks. The same machines that once waved to farmers walking the orchards burned through imperial battle lines without registering the difference. What the legions left behind has had six decades to decay: supply wagons with collapsed wheels and rusted axles, half-buried in windblown dust. Canvas long rotted away, leaving skeletal frames. Abandoned encampments marked by fire-rings, corroded tent stakes, and the occasional collapsed lean-to built from salvaged timber. Battered siege equipment—a scorpion with a warped torsion frame, a ram dragged halfway to the pylons and abandoned where it stopped. No forts. No formal defenses. This was the furthest edge of the advance, and the advance broke before anyone could dig in. The bodies that couldn't be recovered are still here—desiccated, leather-skinned, half-buried, some still in the remnants of imperial kit. Sixty years of wind and sun have reduced them to husks. There is nothing usable left. No supplies, no gear, no weapons worth carrying. Just pockets of rusted remnants and the quiet evidence that an army came here, met something that wasn't even fighting back, and ran.

There is nothing to trade here. Nothing to harvest. Nothing to bring back except the knowledge that this is not a place of honor.

## Encounters

There are no NPCs. There is no one here. Anything the player meets is a remnant—autonomous, still executing its original purpose, and operating on logic that includes the concept of a living observer only as something to be kept safe or kept out.

Encounters should feel vast, loud (near the Iron), and fundamentally alien. The danger is not aggression—it's interference. Walking through a machine that's still running without understanding what any part of it does. The player is not threatened. They are irrelevant, and irrelevance near active machinery is its own kind of danger.

The Iron are not subtle. You hear them before you see them—the grind of metal, the hiss of hydraulics, the clank of heavy feet on fused ground. They are large and they do not care about stealth. When one of the Iron passes nearby, the ground vibrates. When several converge on a patrol intersection, the noise is considerable. This is not the quiet menace of a ghost; it is the indifference of heavy machinery in motion.

Environmental hazards are primary: zones of altered physics, emanations from structures that affect the body or mind, terrain that is not what it appears to be, the Grid activating in response to a threshold the player didn't know they'd crossed, and the slow dawning realization that the scale of what happened here exceeds the player's ability to comprehend it.

## Scene Palette

Characters: none. One of the Iron walking a perimeter around a city whose residents left generations ago, clanking steadily, pistons cycling. Another, identical, walking the same perimeter in the opposite direction. Something that might be one of the Iron, motionless, in a position that might be standing guard or might be broken mid-stride—but the joints still whir faintly. In the far distance, movement too large and too slow to be anything the player has a name for.

Objects: a red pylon humming with a low electrical drone, the oxide paint still vivid against the pale ground. A squat concrete building with a rusted metal door hanging open—inside, dry pipes and dead gauges. The stumps of an orchard in neat rows, dead wood bleached white, the irrigation channels between them cracked and dry. A dolmen humming at a frequency that vibrates the player's gear. A crater with a smooth glass floor reflecting a sky that doesn't look quite right. A door in a freestanding frame that opens onto the same plain but the light is different on the other side. An overturned imperial supply wagon, its iron fittings orange with rust, canvas long gone. A scorpion bolt-thrower with a warped frame, half-sunk in dust. A desiccated corpse in faded imperial kit, leather-skinned, undisturbed for sixty years. A pile of Iron parts arranged neatly by someone or something. A column of air that shimmers and is warm to approach. A trench cut into the glass plain by something that moved in a straight line to the horizon.

Sensory: a hum felt in the jaw and sternum before the ears register it. The grinding clank of one of the Iron on patrol, audible from a quarter-mile out. The click of boots on ground that rings like ceramic. Wind that crosses the plain without resistance and sounds like a sustained note. The absence of smell—no rot, no growth, no dust, just nothing. A headache that starts at the temples when facing certain structures. Light that falls flat and shadowless near the craters. The unnerving mechanical precision of an Iron patrol—exactly even, exactly timed, never varying, servos whining on each step. A taste like copper when the air shimmers. The domed city, always visible, pale and clean-lined against the horizon in every direction. The visual wrongness of a horizon that's too sharp, too defined, like a rendered image with no atmospheric haze. Silence so total that your own heartbeat becomes the loudest thing—until the Iron break it.

Activities: approaching a red pylon and deciding whether to cross the line it marks. Watching one of the Iron's patrol route to find the gap, timing the clank-whir-clank of its stride. Sheltering in a concrete pump house and examining the dead gauges and dry pipes. Stepping over a trench cut into the earth and choosing not to follow it to the horizon. Standing near a dolmen long enough to notice the pattern and then stepping away because the pattern is starting to make sense and that feels dangerous. Picking through the remains of an imperial encampment—fire-ring, rusted stakes, a leather satchel with nothing inside. Mapping a route between active zones. Looking at the domed city on the horizon and calculating whether it's getting closer or you're just seeing it more clearly. Resisting the urge to touch a surface that looks like it wants to be touched. Counting your supplies and calculating whether you can afford to go further or if further is a concept that still applies here.

Tone reference (do not reuse these scenarios): one of the Iron walks directly through the player's camp at night along a path that has not varied in decades, the ground shaking with each step. A dolmen sequence completes and something changes at the edge of perception—the light shifts, or the hum drops a register, or a door that was closed is now open. The player finds bootprints in the fused glass that are not their own and are not old. A red pylon begins to pulse, and somewhere in the distance, something large starts moving.
