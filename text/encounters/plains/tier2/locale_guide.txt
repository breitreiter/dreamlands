# The Failed Campaign

Past the farmlands, the empire's ambition lies exposed. Forts, signal towers, supply depots, and straight roads built to support an advance that broke against the Grid. The campaign lasted weeks. The infrastructure was built for months. What remains is a skeleton of imperial order — half-collapsed garrisons, sealed depots, dug-in artillery pits, and roads that lead to positions no one holds.

The fortifications bear the marks of terrible violence. Walls cratered or blown open. Stone melted or vitrified in ways no siege engine could explain. Whole sections corroded by something that left no ordinary ash. To the people who live here now, the damage looks like the work of gods. The empire's soldiers fought something that could do *that* — and the empire has never quite said what it was. They call them "The Iron." The official term is "The Wardens." Neither name helps.

Two groups occupy this no-man's-land. Military remnants — small detachments rotated out each spring, holding forts out of habit, maintaining protocol with no mission and no resupply. And scavenger bands — families and work crews who squat in abandoned positions, pick salvage, and operate like guilds with territory claims. Neither group is going anywhere. Neither trusts the other. Both believe the land still belongs to someone.

The land between them is not empty wilderness. It's a logistics network with no army to serve — roads, waymarkers, earthworks, depots — all slowly being absorbed by the grass. Travelers follow military roads because they're the only roads. They pass through checkpoints that may or may not still be manned. They camp in structures built for a war effort that collapsed within living memory.

## Encounters

The tension here is social and political, not wilderness survival. Territory claims, contested salvage, brittle authority, and the question of who owns what the empire left behind. Military patrols insist you shouldn't be here but can't quite say why. Scavengers want to know whose claim you're near. Everyone is performing a role — soldiers pretending they still have a mission, scavengers pretending they're not squatting in someone else's ruins, and traders caught between jurisdictions that don't technically exist.

Underneath the human politics, the Grid's legacy is physical and present. Wreckage too large to move. Craters too perfect to be natural. Sealed vaults nobody has the tools or nerve to open. The empire's great lesson from the campaign was not humility — it was envy. There's a quiet industry of people trying to understand, repurpose, or profit from what the Grid left behind, and none of them fully grasp what they're handling.

Encounters should feel brittle, contested, and uneasy. People here are not desperate — they're entrenched. They have routines, grievances, hierarchies, and long memories. The violence of the campaign is over but its consequences are still being negotiated, one salvage claim and one expired requisition at a time.

## Scene Palette

Characters: a lieutenant running a checkpoint who asks for papers that haven't been issued in years, a scavenger matriarch who knows every depot and cache in a three-day radius and trades information like currency, a military quartermaster inventorying supplies that expired seasons ago and filing the reports anyway, a pair of soldiers playing cards in a watchtower with nothing to watch, a salvage crew hauling corroded metal on a handcart toward a buyer they haven't confirmed, a deserter living quietly among the scavengers who flinches when soldiers pass, a tinker who buys Grid fragments and resells them as curiosities to heartland collectors, a former campaign surveyor now drawing maps for scavenger bands, a military chaplain holding services for a congregation of eight in a roofless fort

Objects: a requisition ledger with entries dated years after the retreat, a signal mirror still mounted on a tower with no one to signal, a boundary marker driven into the ground by surveyors who never came back, a crate of crossbow bolts green with verdigris, a scavenger's territory map scratched onto a piece of salvaged plate, a faded duty roster pinned to a barracks wall with names no one recognizes, a fragment of Grid metal that's lighter than it should be and faintly warm, a sealed imperial strongbox with a lock nobody has the key for, a regimental standard used as a tent flap, a child's toy carved from a piece of salvaged bone

Sensory: wind constant and unbroken across flat ground with nothing to stop it, dust kicked up by boots on roads built for column marches, the creak of a watchtower in a gust, the particular silence of a checkpoint where no one has passed in days, the smell of old canvas and rust in an abandoned barracks, grass growing through the floor of a signal station, birdsong that stops when you approach a ruin and starts again when you've passed, the distant clang of a salvage crew working metal, evening light catching vitrified stone and making it glow like glass, cookfire smoke rising from behind a wall that was breached by something enormous

Activities: presenting credentials to a patrol that has no authority to check them, haggling over salvage rights at the edge of someone's claimed territory, sheltering overnight in a depot that two different groups consider theirs, reading old campaign dispatches nailed to a message board at a crossroads, following a military road past positions marked on a map that no longer exist, bartering trail food for information about what lies ahead, helping a salvage crew free something heavy from collapsed stonework, sitting through a campfire argument about whether the retreat was cowardice or wisdom, carrying a message between groups who won't speak to each other directly, examining blast damage on a fortification and trying to understand what weapon could do that
