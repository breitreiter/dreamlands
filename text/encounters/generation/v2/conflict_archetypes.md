**Broken reciprocity** — Someone received something (hospitality, a favor, a loan, protection) and failed to return in kind. The injured party wants restitution or acknowledgment. The PC is asked to weigh what was owed against what was given. Examples:
- Two things of incomensurable value were exchanged and the parties cannot agree on an exchange rate
- An equitable arrangement was made in the past, but now things have changed and one party believes the deal unfair
- One party recieved something they did not want, the other is demanding payment
- One party asserts they are owed a fee, gift, or tithe while the other denies this claim
- One party was injured or mistreated and demands recompense. The other asserts they are not responsible.

Possible conflict participants:
- neutral vs neutral
- neutral vs villain
- villain vs villain

**Contested claim** — Two parties assert rights over the same thing: land, water, salvage, an inheritance, a trade route, a corpse. Neither is obviously wrong. The PC isn't restoring balance so much as *drawing a line* — and someone will end up on the wrong side of it.

Possible conflict participants:
- neutral vs neutral
- neutral vs villain
- villain vs villain

**Status transgression** — Someone acted above or below their station, used a title they weren't entitled to, crossed a boundary that's socially legible but not contractual. The offense is real but the harm is abstract.

Possible conflict participants:
- neutral vs neutral
- neutral vs villain
- villain vs villain
- pc vs neutral
- pc vs villain

**Oath violation** — A vow, betrothal, sworn service, or ritual promise was broken. Distinct from broken reciprocity because oaths have witnesses, sacred weight, or institutional backing. The question isn't "what's fair" but "what does the oath demand," and the PC has to decide whether the oath's terms should hold or whether circumstances justify release.

Possible conflict participants:
- neutral vs neutral
- neutral vs villain
- villain vs villain

**Hospitality breach** — Refusal of shelter, abuse of guest-right, betrayal under a shared roof. This includes refusal to render aid to someone in distress.

Possible conflict participants:
- neutral vs neutral
- neutral vs villain
- villain vs villain

**Ritual or sacred violation** — Desecration, improper burial, broken taboo, trespass on holy ground. The injured party might be a community, a priesthood, or something nonhuman. Resolution often requires sacrifice or penance rather than compensation.

Possible conflict participants:
- neutral vs neutral
- neutral vs villain
- villain vs villain
- pc vs neutral
- pc vs villain

**Failure of kinship duty** — Abandonment of dependents, refusal to pay blood-price, denial of kin-right, failure to shelter or avenge family.

Possible conflict participants:
- neutral vs neutral
- neutral vs villain
- villain vs villain

**Fraud or false witness** — Someone lied about identity, quality of goods, testimony, or provenance. The structural difference from other categories is that *the truth is discoverable* — so the encounter can involve investigation, not just judgment.

Possible conflict participants:
- neutral vs neutral
- neutral vs villain
- villain vs villain
- pc vs neutral
- pc vs villain
