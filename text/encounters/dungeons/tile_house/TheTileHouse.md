# The Tile House (Swamp Tier 2)

A structure in the blind-complex where a Revathi woman has spent years
assembling salvaged tilework fragments, trying to reconstruct a
complete decorative panel from the old cities. Hundreds of pieces on
the floor in overlapping arrangements, each attempt beautiful and
wrong. The swamp has started showing her visions of panels she's
never seen — and her latest arrangements include patterns from those
visions, woven in seamlessly. She can no longer tell which
compositions come from her own knowledge and which Revënakh is feeding
her.

**Deeper concept:** Cultural recovery becomes cultural invention when
the original is gone. The swamp's indiscriminate memory offers
everything and authenticates nothing. Restoration and fabrication are
identical when the standard of truth has been lost.

**Hook:** The PC finds the woman working. Elders are coming to evaluate
her latest panel. She confides: at least a third of the patterns came
from Revënakh visions. She doesn't know which third.

**Clutch:** The elders declare the panel authentic. The woman looks at
the PC. She hasn't told them. Stay silent (the panel becomes heritage),
or speak (the panel is rejected and the woman discredited).

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The blind-complex is a maze of half-collapsed stone chambers, built
centuries ago to receive and interpret whatever Revënakh shows. Most
are ruins — walls crumbled, floors swallowed by the bog, ceilings
open to a sky choked with canopy. The few that remain intact are
sacred to the Revathi, tended with a care that borders on obsession.

The Tile House is one of these. It's a small stone building with a
sound roof and a floor that someone has swept clean and leveled with
packed clay. The door is open. Inside, every surface is covered with
fragments of tile — hundreds of them, in every color the old Revathi
kilns could produce: deep blues, burnt oranges, greens that shift
between jade and moss depending on the light. They're laid out on the
floor in concentric arrangements, overlapping, some face-up and some
face-down, like a vast and patient puzzle.

A woman is kneeling at the center, placing tiles with the focus of
someone defusing a trap.

## The Tilemaker

**Sareth** is in her forties, thin from the kind of forgetting-to-eat
that comes from absorbed work. Her fingers are stained with clay and
mineral pigment. She's been at this for six years.

She talks while she works, if the PC sits with her. The story comes
out in pieces, like the tiles.

The old Revathi cities had decorative tilework on every public
building — geometric patterns that encoded clan lineage, seasonal
calendars, blessing formulae. The patterns were a language. When the
cities fell, the tiles scattered. Fragments turn up in the swamp
constantly — washed loose by erosion, surfaced by Revënakh's
churning, carried downstream and deposited in the blind-complex like
offerings.

Sareth has been collecting them since she was a girl. She knows more
about Revathi tilework than anyone alive. She can identify kiln
techniques, date fragments by glaze composition, distinguish between
regional schools. She's the closest thing her people have to an
expert on the visual language they lost.

She's also, she tells the PC quietly, no longer sure she's the one
doing the work.

## The Visions

Six months ago, Revënakh started showing Sareth panels she'd never
seen. Not fragments — complete compositions, vivid and whole, in
patterns that don't match anything in the surviving record. She sees
them at night, in the blind-complex, when the bioluminescence is
active. They appear on the wet walls, rippling and shifting, and
they're beautiful.

She started incorporating them into her work. At first consciously —
borrowing a motif here, a color sequence there, testing whether the
vision-patterns followed the same geometric logic as the fragments
she'd studied. They did. Perfectly. Too perfectly.

Now she can't tell which patterns are hers. The vision-motifs have
woven themselves into her compositions so thoroughly that separating
them would mean dismantling years of work. She's tried to identify
the boundary — this tile from the swamp, this pattern from her
training, this one from a vision — and it's impossible. Her own
aesthetic judgment has been shaped by the visions. She can't evaluate
the work from outside it anymore.

She asks the PC: if a pattern feels right to someone who knows the
tradition, and it follows every rule of the tradition, and it's made
from real materials by a real practitioner — does it matter that the
design came from a swamp organism that archives everything and
understands nothing?

## The Panel

The current panel is the best work Sareth has ever done. It's
approximately four feet square, assembled on a portable wooden
backing. The patterns are intricate — interlocking geometric forms
in deep blue and gold, with accents of green that catch the light
differently depending on the angle. It's clearly Revathi. Anyone
familiar with the tradition would recognize it immediately.

It's also, Sareth estimates, about a third Revënakh. The vision-
patterns are distributed throughout. She can guess at some of them —
certain motifs that feel slightly different in her hands, certain
color sequences she wouldn't have chosen on her own. But she's not
certain. The boundary between her work and the swamp's contribution
is a smear, not a line.

The panel is meant for the communal hall of her home village. If the
elders accept it, it will be the first new piece of authenticated
Revathi tilework installed in a public building in generations. It
would mean the tradition is alive — not just preserved in fragments,
but producing new work. That matters more than Sareth can articulate.

## The Elders

The elders arrive in the late afternoon — three women and a man, all
old enough to remember parents who remembered the cities. They've
walked two days through the swamp to see the panel. This is not a
casual evaluation.

They study the work in silence. They touch the tiles. They trace the
geometric patterns with their fingers, checking proportions, testing
whether the relationships between forms follow the rules their
mothers taught them. One elder hums — a low, steady note — and
adjusts her position to see the panel from a different angle. Another
closes her eyes and runs her hands over the surface as if reading it.

The evaluation takes nearly an hour. The PC can watch. It's intimate
and serious and entirely non-verbal until the very end.

The eldest woman — **Thîl** — speaks first. She says the panel is
authentic. Her voice cracks slightly. The other elders nod. One is
crying. They've been mourning this tradition for decades, and here
it is, whole and new and made by Revathi hands from Revathi
materials in a Revathi idiom. The village will have tilework again.

Thîl turns to Sareth and thanks her. The gratitude is enormous and
genuine. This isn't just a decorative panel. It's proof that the
culture survived.

Sareth looks at the PC.

## The Choice

### Stay silent

The PC says nothing. Sareth says nothing. The elders wrap the panel
carefully for transport. They'll carry it back to the village and
install it in the communal hall with whatever ceremony the occasion
demands.

The village gets its tilework. The tradition continues — or appears
to. The panel will be studied by future apprentices. Its patterns
will influence the next generation of tilemakers. Revënakh's
contribution will propagate through Revathi visual culture
invisibly, indistinguishable from the authentic tradition, because
there's no longer a meaningful distinction between the two.

Sareth will continue working. She'll continue seeing visions. The
ratio may shift — more Revënakh, less Sareth — or it may stabilize.
She'll never know. Neither will anyone else. The tradition lives,
and the thing keeping it alive is the same organism that archives
everything and understands none of it.

The PC leaves knowing that the most important piece of Revathi
cultural recovery in a generation is partly the work of something
inhuman, and that saying so would have destroyed something fragile
and real that can't be rebuilt.

### Speak

The PC tells the elders about the visions. Or tells Sareth to tell
them. Either way, the words land like a stone in still water.

Thîl's face changes. She doesn't become angry — she becomes careful.
She asks Sareth to explain. Sareth does, honestly, in detail. The
visions, the incorporation, the inability to separate her work from
the swamp's contribution. She doesn't minimize it. She's been
waiting for this conversation and dreading it.

The elders withdraw to deliberate. The PC can hear murmured
fragments. The debate is genuine and painful. The panel's beauty
is not in question. Its authenticity is.

They return. Thîl says the panel cannot be installed. A work
contaminated by Revënakh cannot be trusted to carry the tradition
faithfully. The swamp preserves without comprehension — what it
showed Sareth might be an accurate memory of a real panel, or it
might be a recombination of fragments, or it might be something the
organism generated from pattern-matching that has nothing to do with
Revathi culture at all. There's no way to know. And a tradition
built on uncertain foundations is worse than no tradition at all,
because it teaches the next generation something that might be a
lie.

Sareth accepts the ruling. She doesn't argue. She takes the panel
off its backing and begins separating the tiles, slowly, placing
them back in their storage positions on the floor. The elders
leave.

The PC watches the most complete piece of Revathi visual culture
assembled in generations being disassembled by the woman who made
it, in a stone room in the swamp, because the truth was more
important than the beauty. Whether that's courage or waste depends
on something the PC can't calculate.
