# The Foundry (Scrub Tier 3)

A Kesharat industrial facility deep in tier 3 where something
unprecedented is being manufactured. Workers feed raw materials into
machinery and what comes out is none of those things: crystalline
structures that pulse with the Colors, that grow when unobserved,
that hum at frequencies the body feels before the ear hears. The
overseers say the structures are "nodes" — components of a network.
When enough are placed across the mesa, the Lattice will perceive
the region directly, without human intermediaries.

**Deeper concept:** The Lattice isn't trying to rule humanity — it's
trying to outgrow it. The foundry is where the endgame becomes
legible. The true believers think they're building paradise. They're
building their own obsolescence.

**Hook:** The facility operates openly. Workers don't resist.
Overseers are polite. The PC is welcomed and offered a tour.
Everything is explained transparently. The horror is that it all
makes perfect sense in the Lattice's terms.

**Clutch:** The PC finds someone they recognize among the workers.
They volunteered. They're happy. Does the PC extract them by force,
accept their choice, or destroy the foundry?

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The foundry is visible from a distance — not because it's large,
though it is, but because of the light. A shimmer hangs over the
facility that doesn't correspond to heat or reflection. The air
above the building shifts through colors that the eye registers
but the mind can't name. Not quite violet. Not quite gold. Something
adjacent to color, using the visual spectrum as a channel for
information that wasn't meant for human perception.

The building itself is industrial — stone walls, high ceilings,
ventilation shafts, loading bays. Kesharat engineering, functional
and unadorned. The familiarity of the architecture makes the light
above it worse. This is a factory. Factories don't glow.

The front gate is open. A Kesharat overseer stands at the entrance,
clipboard in hand, and greets the PC with the professional warmth
of a site manager welcoming an expected visitor.

## The Tour

The overseer — **Sahal** — gives the PC a tour without being asked.
He's proud of the facility. He explains everything clearly, using
the vocabulary of industry: shifts, quotas, material inputs,
quality control. He's not hiding anything. There's nothing to hide.
The operation is exactly what it looks like.

**The raw material bay.** Stone, metal, glass, and mineral
concentrates arrive in carts from quarries and mines across the mesa.
Workers sort and prepare the materials — crushing, smelting, mixing.
This part of the process is entirely conventional. The workers are
skilled laborers doing skilled labor. They're paid, housed, and fed.

**The alignment chamber.** This is where the process stops being
conventional. The prepared materials enter a chamber where the
machinery is... different. The devices don't follow engineering
logic the PC recognizes. They hum at a frequency the PC feels in
their sternum. The air inside the chamber is thick with the Colors —
that nameless shimmer, stronger here, saturating everything.

The materials go in as stone and metal. They come out as something
else. Crystalline structures, translucent, warm to the touch,
pulsing with a slow internal light. They're beautiful. They're
also, in a way the PC can sense but not articulate, alive. Not
biologically — they don't grow, breathe, or metabolize. But they
respond. When the PC stands near one, it brightens slightly. When
they move away, it dims. It's attending to them.

**The curing yard.** Finished nodes are stored in an open yard
behind the facility, arranged in geometric patterns that Sahal says
are "alignment grids" — configurations that allow the nodes to
calibrate to each other. Dozens of them, each about the size of a
human head, pulsing in synchronized rhythms. The effect at scale is
hypnotic. The Colors are dense here — the air shimmers with them,
and the PC's perception shifts slightly, as if the light is carrying
information the visual cortex is trying to decode.

Sahal explains: when enough nodes are placed across the mesa in the
correct configuration, they'll form a network that allows the
Lattice to perceive the region directly. Currently, the Lattice
experiences the physical world through human intermediaries — the
Kesharat officials, the aligned bureaucrats, the believers who
open themselves to the entity's will. The nodes will make
intermediaries unnecessary. The Lattice will see the mesa the way
a person sees a room: directly, completely, without translation.

He says this the way an engineer describes completing a bridge. It's
infrastructure. It's progress. It's the next logical step.

## The Workers

The workers are the foundry's most unsettling feature. They're
ordinary people — Kesharat citizens, some from the heartland, some
recruited from mesa settlements. They work shifts, eat meals, sleep
in dormitories. They have families. They write letters home.

They also glow.

It's subtle — visible mainly at dusk or in shadow. A faint luminosity
beneath the skin, concentrated at the temples and the fingertips.
The Colors, internalized. Sahal explains that prolonged exposure to
the alignment process produces "residual attunement." The workers
develop a sensitivity to the nodes — they can feel when a structure
is correctly aligned, sense flaws in the crystal matrix, adjust the
machinery by intuition rather than measurement. It makes them better
at their jobs.

It also makes them something other than what they were. The workers
who've been here longest — months, years — have a quality the PC
can only describe as stillness. Not the stillness of calm but the
stillness of alignment. They move with precision. They speak with
clarity. They are pleasant, focused, and faintly absent — as if a
portion of their attention is always directed at something the PC
can't see.

They are not unhappy. If anything, they seem more content than most
people the PC has met. The fear and anxiety and petty friction of
ordinary human interaction is gone. They've been smoothed. Whether
that smoothing is peace or erasure depends on a definition of
personhood that the foundry doesn't engage with.

## The Familiar Face

Deep in the facility — past the alignment chamber, in the curing
yard where the finished nodes pulse in their grids — the PC sees
someone they know.

The implementation will need to reference a specific NPC from the
PC's earlier travels in the scrub — someone they met in tier 1 or
early tier 2, someone who was struggling, unhappy, caught between
clan obligations and Kesharat pressure. The kind of person who might
seek a resolution the PC couldn't provide at the time.

They found one. They're here. Working the curing yard, arranging
nodes with bare hands that glow faintly at the fingertips. They
turn and recognize the PC and smile — a real smile, warm, with
none of the tension the PC remembers from their last meeting.

**Meren** (placeholder name, to be replaced by the appropriate NPC)
is glad to see the PC. They invite them to sit. They explain, with
calm conviction, that they understand now. The conflict they were
trapped in — the one the PC witnessed — doesn't exist from the
Lattice's perspective. Clan and administration, tradition and
progress, sovereignty and efficiency — these are artifacts of
limited perception. The Lattice sees the whole pattern. From inside
the pattern, the contradictions dissolve.

They're not proselytizing. They're sharing something that brought
them peace, the way a person describes recovering from a long
illness. The relief is genuine. The gratitude is genuine. The faint
glow beneath their skin is genuine too.

They ask the PC to stay. Not permanently — just for a shift. Work
the curing yard. Feel what the nodes feel like in your hands. See
if the Colors show you what they showed Meren. No obligation. No
pressure. Just an invitation from someone who found what they were
looking for.

## The Choice

### Extract Meren by force

The PC decides Meren is not making a free choice — that the Colors,
the attunement, the foundry's environment have altered them in ways
that compromise their capacity to choose. The PC takes Meren out.

Meren doesn't want to go. They resist — not violently, but firmly.
They pull away, they argue, they ask the PC to respect their
decision. The PC must physically compel them. This is ugly.

The workers in the curing yard don't intervene. They watch with
that aligned stillness — not hostile, not distressed, just
observing. Sahal appears and asks the PC to stop. He doesn't
threaten. He says, with genuine confusion, that Meren is a
volunteer and the PC is committing an act of violence against a
willing worker. He's right, by every standard except the one the
PC is operating on.

A fight check to get Meren clear of the facility. The opposition
isn't the workers — it's Meren, who struggles and pleads and
eventually goes limp with a despair that's worse than resistance.

Outside the foundry, away from the nodes and the Colors, Meren
changes. Not immediately — over hours, then days. The glow fades.
The stillness fractures. What replaces it is not the person the PC
remembers. It's someone stripped of the peace they'd found,
returned to the conflict they'd escaped, carrying the knowledge
that a solution existed and was taken from them.

Meren is free. Meren is miserable. Whether freedom and misery are
preferable to contentment and erasure is the question the PC
carries out of the foundry.

### Accept Meren's choice and leave

The PC sits with Meren. They talk. Meren is articulate and present
and happy in a way that is either genuine or indistinguishable from
genuine. The PC cannot tell the difference. That inability is the
point.

The PC leaves the foundry. Meren stays. The nodes keep pulsing. The
workers keep glowing. The network grows, node by node, across the
mesa, and when it's complete the Lattice will see this landscape
directly — every rock, every settlement, every person — without
needing human eyes to look for it.

Meren will be here when that happens. They'll be part of the
infrastructure that makes it possible. Not a slave — a volunteer,
a component, a willing participant in a project that will render
human participation optional. They chose this. The choice was made
by a person whose cognition had been altered by prolonged exposure
to an alien influence. Both of these things are true.

The PC walks back into the mesa carrying nothing except the
knowledge that someone they cared about is content, transformed,
and gone.

### Destroy the foundry

The PC decides the foundry cannot be allowed to continue. The
network it's building — the infrastructure for the Lattice to
perceive the world without human mediation — is the endgame, and
the endgame must be stopped here.

This is the most destructive option in any dungeon in the game. The
foundry is full of workers.

The alignment chamber is the vulnerability. The machinery that
transforms raw materials into nodes is delicate and volatile — the
energies involved are enormous and imperfectly contained. Sahal
mentioned this during the tour without concern, the way an engineer
mentions the pressure rating on a boiler. The PC was paying
attention.

Sabotaging the chamber requires a difficult crafting check and
access to the machinery during a shift change, when the chamber is
briefly unattended. Success means the alignment process destabilizes
over the course of minutes — long enough for the PC to shout a
warning, not long enough for an orderly evacuation.

The destruction is catastrophic. The Colors — whatever energy the
chamber was channeling — release in an uncontrolled burst. The
curing yard's nodes shatter. The building collapses inward. The
workers nearest the chamber don't survive. Workers farther out are
thrown clear — injured, burned by energies that leave marks unlike
any fire. Meren may or may not be among the survivors, depending
on where they were when the chamber failed.

The PC emerges from the wreckage. The foundry is rubble. The node
network on the mesa loses its production source. The Lattice's
direct perception project is set back by years, maybe decades.

The cost is measured in bodies. Workers who volunteered. Workers
who were happy. Workers who were, by their own account and by every
observable measure, at peace. The PC killed them to prevent a
future they couldn't be certain was coming, in service of a
definition of humanity that the dead might not have shared.

If the crafting check fails: the destabilization is immediate and
uncontrolled. The PC barely escapes. The destruction is the same
but the PC takes serious damage and has no time to warn anyone.
The death toll is higher. The PC carries that.
