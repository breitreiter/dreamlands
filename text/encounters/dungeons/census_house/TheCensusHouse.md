# The Census House (Scrub Tier 2)

A Kesharat administrative office where a young functionary has compiled
the most thorough record of clan genealogies ever assembled. She's
polite, diligent, and genuinely fascinated by kinship structures. Her
census maps every family, every alliance, every marriage debt, every
vulnerability. It's a masterpiece of ethnography. It's also a targeting
document.

**Deeper concept:** Knowledge gathered in good faith serves whoever
holds it, and the collector doesn't get to choose the use. Good
intentions are perfectly irrelevant when the institution you serve is
parasitic.

**Hook:** A clan raid hits the Census House. The raiders want to burn
the records. The functionary is hiding. The raiders aren't killers —
they're a clan elder and his sons, frightened of what the records
represent.

**Clutch:** The functionary begs the PC to save her life's work. The
elder explains that every name in those books is a throat the Kesharat
can cut. The records are both things at once. Does the PC let them burn?

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The Census House is a modest Kesharat administrative building —
single-story, whitewashed, with the Administration's geometric emblem
above the door. It sits at the edge of clan territory, deliberately
placed where the mesa road meets the first grazing tracks. A neutral
location. A welcoming location. The kind of building designed to say:
we're here to help.

The PC arrives to find the door forced open and voices inside. Not
screaming — controlled, tense, the sound of people trying to be
deliberate about something that frightens them. A young woman's voice,
higher, faster, pleading. An older man's voice, steady and firm.

Four men are inside. Three of them — young, broad, carrying staves —
are pulling books off shelves and stacking them in the center of the
room. The fourth — older, grey-bearded, unarmed — is directing them
with the calm authority of a patriarch managing a household task.

Behind the desk, crouched against the back wall, a young Kesharat
woman in administrative dress is clutching a leather-bound volume to
her chest and talking very fast.

## The Functionary

**Zahra** is twenty-six, originally from the Kesharat heartland, and
this is her first posting. She requested it. She wanted to work with
the plateau clans because she finds kinship structures fascinating —
not as an abstraction but as living mathematics. Who marries whom, and
why, and what obligations flow from the union, and how those
obligations compound across generations.

Her census is extraordinary. The PC can see it, if they look — the
books the raiders are stacking are filled with careful diagrams,
family trees that branch and reconnect across pages, annotations in
a precise hand that cross-reference marriages with alliance networks,
grazing rights with inheritance patterns, feuds with their origin
debts. It's not a list of names. It's a topology of an entire
culture, mapped by someone who genuinely loves the complexity.

Zahra is terrified but not incoherent. She tells the PC — rapidly,
interrupting herself — that these men are going to burn years of
irreplaceable scholarship. She's not wrong. The census represents
the most complete ethnographic record of plateau clan structure ever
compiled. No one else has done this work. No one else could. She
spent three years building trust, learning customs, sitting with
elders who taught her their genealogies because she listened with
real attention and asked the right questions.

She doesn't understand why they want to destroy it. She recorded
their culture. She honored it. She made it visible.

That's the problem.

## The Elder

**Rashîd** is the patriarch of the Ouren clan — one of the three
major clans whose genealogies fill Zahra's census. He's in his
sixties, spare and weathered, with hands that have spent decades
working stone and rope. He's not angry. He's frightened, and he's
old enough to act on fear without letting it make him stupid.

He tells the PC what happened. Last month, a Kesharat tax assessor
arrived at the Ouren compound with a detailed understanding of the
clan's internal structure — who owed what to whom, which families
were in debt, which marriages were strategic and which were
fragile. He knew things that only the clan should know. He used
that knowledge to propose a "revenue arrangement" that targeted the
clan's weakest families — the ones who couldn't refuse without
fracturing alliances that held the whole structure together.

The assessor had read the census. Rashîd is certain of it. The
specific details he cited — a marriage debt between the Ouren and
the Veshar, a grazing dispute from twelve years ago that was
resolved but left a residual obligation — are in Zahra's books and
nowhere else. The census gave the Kesharat a map of every pressure
point in the clan network.

Rashîd doesn't blame Zahra. He says this explicitly. She's a
scholar. She documented what she saw. He liked her. His mother liked
her. His daughters taught her the family songs. She was a guest in
their compound and she honored the guest-right.

But the books are a weapon now. Not because Zahra made them a
weapon — because the institution she serves will use anything it
holds. The Kesharat Administration doesn't distinguish between
scholarship and intelligence. The census went into the archive and
the archive is accessible to every functionary on the mesa. What
Zahra recorded with love, the assessor read with calculation.

Rashîd tells the PC: every name in those books is a throat the
Kesharat can cut whenever they choose. He's not being rhetorical.
He's describing what happened to the Ouren last month. And his
clan isn't the only one in the census.

He wants the books burned. He'll let Zahra go unharmed. He's not
a killer. But the books burn today.

## The Weight

The PC has time to examine the situation. Key details:

**The books are irreplaceable.** Zahra compiled the census from oral
sources — elders who shared genealogies in confidence, assuming the
information would be used for cultural documentation. Many of those
elders are dead. The knowledge in the books exists nowhere else in
written form. If they burn, the most complete record of plateau clan
culture burns with them. Future scholars will have nothing to build
on.

**The books are already copied.** Zahra admits this, if pressed. She
filed quarterly reports with the regional archive in the Kesharat
capital. The reports are summaries, not full transcriptions — they
contain the major family structures and alliance networks but not the
detailed annotations. Burning the originals destroys the depth but not
the skeleton. The Kesharat already has enough to work with. Rashîd
doesn't know this.

**The books will be used again.** The tax assessor's visit wasn't
isolated. The Kesharat Administration is systematically mapping clan
vulnerabilities across the mesa. Zahra's census is the most detailed
source, but it's part of a larger project. Even if the books burn,
the institutional appetite for this information doesn't.

**Zahra isn't innocent.** She's not guilty either, but the
distinction matters. She filed the reports knowing they went into the
administrative archive. She didn't ask what the archive was used for
because the question wasn't part of her training. She was a scholar
doing scholarship within an institution that uses scholarship as
infrastructure. She didn't choose to be a weapon. She also didn't
choose not to be.

## The Choice

### Let the books burn

The PC steps aside. Rashîd's sons stack the volumes in the center
of the room — carefully, without malice, the way you'd handle
something you respected and needed to destroy. Rashîd lights the
pile himself. The fire is hot and fast. Three years of work, the
most complete ethnography of a living culture ever assembled,
becomes ash and smoke in twenty minutes.

Zahra watches. She doesn't cry — she's past that. She holds the
one volume she grabbed — a partial genealogy of the Veshar clan,
maybe eighty pages — and watches the rest go.

Rashîd thanks the PC. Not warmly — there's no warmth in this. He
takes his sons and leaves. The Census House is intact. The shelves
are empty. The Kesharat will send someone to investigate. Zahra will
file a report describing the raid. The quarterly summaries in the
capital archive will remain.

Zahra tells the PC, before they leave: it won't matter. The
Administration will send another functionary. The next one might not
love the work. The next one might understand exactly what they're
building. The census will be rebuilt — not with the depth Zahra
brought, not with the relationships she built, but with the
institutional resources the Kesharat can deploy when they decide
something matters. The clans will be mapped again, by someone who
doesn't care about the culture and only cares about the topology.

### Save the books

The PC intervenes. This means stopping Rashîd and his sons — by
persuasion or by force.

Persuasion requires a difficult check. The PC must make an argument
Rashîd hasn't already considered: that the quarterly summaries mean
burning the originals doesn't protect the clans, that the detailed
annotations actually contain context that makes the bare data harder
to weaponize (an assessor reading Zahra's notes would understand why
a marriage debt exists and why pressuring it would be destructive;
a functionary reading the summary would only see the leverage point).
This argument is true and Rashîd might accept it. He's not stupid.
He's just scared.

If the PC persuades him: Rashîd leaves the books and takes his sons
home. Zahra keeps the census. The books remain in the Census House,
on shelves, accessible to the next assessor who walks in. Zahra
understands now what her work is used for. She can restrict access,
she can annotate the files with context designed to complicate
weaponization, she can refuse to file further quarterly summaries.
All of these choices carry professional consequences. She'll make
them alone, after the PC leaves.

If the PC uses force: a fight check against three young men with
staves. Rashîd doesn't participate — he's too old, and fighting
an outlander over books isn't how he wants to die. The sons are
strong but not trained fighters. If the PC wins, the sons retreat.
Rashîd looks at the PC with an expression that is not anger — it's
the specific grief of a man who asked for help and was refused by
someone who had the power to give it. He leaves. The books remain.
The Census House is intact. The Ouren will remember the PC.

### Help Zahra destroy the summaries

The hardest option, and the one Zahra doesn't suggest — the PC has
to see it.

The originals aren't the real threat. The quarterly summaries in the
capital archive are. The books in this room are detailed,
contextual, human — they contain the *why* alongside the *what*.
The summaries are stripped-down data: names, relationships, leverage
points. That's what the assessor used.

If the PC explains this to both Zahra and Rashîd, a different
possibility opens. Zahra keeps her originals — the full,
contextualized ethnography, stored here, under her control. She
stops filing summaries. She sends a final report to the capital
declaring the census project concluded and the records archived
locally. The capital archive keeps what it has, but receives nothing
new.

This requires Zahra to defy her institution. It requires Rashîd to
trust that Zahra will actually stop filing. Both are difficult asks.
A persuasion check for each.

If both succeed: the books stay. The pipeline closes. Zahra becomes
the sole custodian of a record she now understands is dangerous,
maintaining it not for the Administration but for the clans
themselves — a scholar's archive that serves the people it documents,
not the institution that commissioned it. It's a fragile arrangement.
It depends entirely on Zahra's integrity and the Kesharat's
inattention. Either could fail.

If either check fails: the compromise collapses. Rashîd won't trust
a Kesharat functionary's promises. Or Zahra can't bring herself to
betray her institution. The PC is back to the first two options.
