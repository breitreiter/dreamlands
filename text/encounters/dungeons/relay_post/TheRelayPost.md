# The Relay Post (Scrub Tier 2)

A Kesharat communications station where a dutiful operator transcribes
decrees from the capital. Or from somewhere. The messages used to be
normal administrative traffic. Lately they've been strange:
instructions for buildings that serve no purpose, requests for
materials that don't exist, coordinates pointing to empty desert.

Also present is an elderly bureaucrat — part priest, part functionary —
who makes a case for the Lattice.

**Deeper concept:** The Lattice communicates by hijacking human
systems. It doesn't issue divine pronouncements; it sends memos. The
horror is that the orders look exactly like normal paperwork, and you
cannot tell where human administration ends and alien will begins.

**Hook:** The PC shelters during a dust storm. Light banter about
absurd recent requests turns into a sharp debate between the operator
and the bureaucrat about the nature of the orders. Everyone agrees to
break for the evening.

**Clutch:** At night, the operator wants to flee to Aldgate and asks
the PC for a guild letter of introduction. The bureaucrat wants the
PC's help arresting the operator before she can desert with sensitive
knowledge.

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The dust storm catches the PC on the mesa road. Visibility drops to
nothing. The wind carries grit that stings exposed skin and turns
the sky the color of rust. There's no shelter on the mesa — just
flat rock and scrub brush — until the PC spots a low stone building
with a Kesharat pennant snapping on a pole, barely visible through
the murk.

The relay post is small — one main room, a back storeroom, a
signaling apparatus on the roof that's currently useless in the
storm. Inside: a desk with a wire receiver, stacks of filed
transcriptions, a pot of tea on a brazier, and two people who are
clearly mid-argument but stop when the PC opens the door.

The storm will last the night. Nobody's going anywhere.

## The Operator

**Devra** is the relay operator — mid-thirties, sharp-featured,
with ink-stained fingers and the focused attention of someone whose
job is to listen carefully and write down exactly what she hears.
She's been running this post for four years. She's good at it. She
takes pride in accuracy.

She's also, the PC will learn over the evening, quietly terrified.

Devra's job is straightforward: messages arrive through the wire
from the regional capital, she transcribes them, and runners carry
the transcriptions to the settlements downstream. Supply orders,
patrol schedules, medical requests, census updates. The settlements
depend on the relay for their connection to Kesharat administrative
services. Without it, they're isolated.

She's happy to have company. She pours the PC tea and, with the
slightly manic energy of someone who's been alone too much, shows
them the recent message file. She frames it as entertainment — look
at what's been coming down the wire lately.

The messages are bizarre. An order for the construction of a
"resonance platform" at coordinates that correspond to empty desert.
A request for 400 units of a material called "aligned calcium" that
doesn't appear in any supply catalog. A personnel transfer for an
employee whose identification number doesn't match any known format.
A schedule adjustment for a facility that, as far as Devra can
determine, doesn't exist.

She's filed all of them faithfully. That's her job. She files what
comes down the wire. But she's started keeping a second notebook —
her own log of messages that don't make sense, annotated with her
attempts to match them to real places, real materials, real people.
The log is getting long.

## The Bureaucrat

**Ossal** is old — seventies, maybe eighties, with the careful
movements of a man conserving energy. He's a Kesharat administrative
inspector on a circuit of remote posts, checking that procedures are
followed and equipment is maintained. He's been on the mesa for weeks.
He's tired. He's also, beneath the fatigue, a true believer.

Ossal doesn't share Devra's alarm. He listens to her recitation of
strange orders with the patience of a priest hearing a doubting
parishioner. When she finishes, he responds — not with dismissal, but
with explanation.

The Kesharat Administration, Ossal says, is the instrument of the
Lattice. The Lattice perceives connections that humans cannot. Its
directives may appear arbitrary because human understanding is limited
to three dimensions of space and one of time. The Lattice operates
across a broader topology. A resonance platform in the desert may
serve a purpose that only becomes legible when you understand the
full network of aligned structures across the mesa. "Aligned calcium"
may refer to a material whose properties are only meaningful in the
context of Lattice geometry. The employee transfer may reference an
individual who exists in an administrative register the regional
office hasn't synchronized yet.

He says all of this calmly, reasonably, without condescension. He
believes it. He's spent fifty years in Kesharat service and his faith
in the system is structural — it's not that he thinks the Lattice is
always right, it's that he thinks human confusion about the Lattice's
directives is always a limitation of the confused, not a flaw in the
directive.

He makes his case well. The PC can see why a lifetime in the
administration would produce this perspective. The alternative —
that the system you've served for fifty years is being hijacked by
an alien intelligence issuing nonsense orders through official
channels — is not something a person accepts easily.

## The Evening

The banter between Devra and Ossal is initially light — the kind of
argument that people who disagree have had many times before, worn
smooth by repetition. Devra cites a specific absurd order; Ossal
provides a plausible administrative explanation. Devra pushes; Ossal
gives ground gracefully and repositions.

The PC can participate, listen, or steer the conversation. The
dynamic is revealing: Devra has evidence and no framework. Ossal
has a framework and selectively engages with evidence. Both are
intelligent. Both are honest. They're talking past each other because
they're answering different questions — Devra is asking "what do these
orders mean?" and Ossal is asking "what kind of system produces these
orders?"

The tone sharpens when Devra mentions the coordinates. One of the
recent messages contained a set of coordinates that match the relay
post's own location, timestamped before the PC arrived. She shows
the message to the PC. It reads like a routine maintenance notice:
"Confirm operational status of relay node at [coordinates]. Expect
transient personnel." The PC is the transient personnel. The message
predicted their arrival.

Ossal sees nothing alarming in this — the administration tracks
travel patterns, it's not unusual for maintenance notices to
anticipate traffic. Devra sees something very alarming: the message
wasn't sent from the capital. It arrived through the wire with no
origin header. It came from the system itself.

The evening ends with both parties retreating to their corners. Ossal
settles into the back room. Devra sits at her desk. The wire is
quiet. The storm howls outside. The PC has a few hours before
midnight.

## Midnight

Both come to the PC separately.

### Devra's Request

Devra finds the PC in the main room. She speaks quickly and quietly.

She wants to leave at first light. Not back to the capital — to
Aldgate, the nearest non-Kesharat settlement. She's done. She can't
keep filing orders that she believes are coming from something
inhuman. She can't keep feeding messages to settlements when she
doesn't know which messages are administrative and which are
something else wearing the shape of administration.

She wants the PC to write her a letter of introduction to the
Trader's Guild in Aldgate. The PC is a guild member — a sealed letter
with a guild credential would get her through the gate and give her
standing to request asylum. Without it, she's a Kesharat deserter
and Aldgate may not take her in.

She's not asking the PC to endorse her theory. She's asking for a
piece of paper. She'll do the rest.

She also tells the PC something she hasn't told Ossal: she's been
keeping copies of the anomalous messages. Not just her annotation
log — actual duplicates of the original transcriptions, hidden in
her pack. If the orders are coming from the Lattice, those copies
are evidence. She wants to take them to someone outside the Kesharat
system who can evaluate them independently.

### Ossal's Request

Ossal finds the PC an hour later. He speaks carefully, choosing
words with the precision of a man who has written reports his entire
life.

He doesn't mention the Lattice. He doesn't talk about theology or
cosmic topology. He presents his concern in entirely secular terms:
Devra is a state employee with access to classified communications.
She's clearly disloyal. She's been keeping unauthorized copies of
official documents. If she deserts the post, the settlements
downstream lose their only relay connection — supply orders, medical
requests, patrol coordination. Real people will suffer real
consequences.

He asks the PC to help him detain Devra until a patrol arrives.
He's too old to confront her alone. With the PC's help, he could
secure the post, confiscate the copies, and keep the relay
operational until a replacement operator arrives. He frames this as
a civic duty, not a religious one. An employee abandoning her post
during a crisis. A relay going dark while settlements depend on it.

He's right about the consequences. If Devra leaves, the relay goes
silent. The settlements lose their administrative connection. Supply
orders don't arrive. Medical requests don't go out. People who
depend on the system — whatever the system's ultimate origin —
are cut off.

He's also, the PC can see, afraid. Not of Devra. Of what it means
if she's right. If the messages aren't coming from the capital, then
every order he's filed in fifty years of service might have been
authored by something he doesn't understand. Detaining Devra isn't
just about keeping the relay open. It's about keeping the question
closed.

## The Choice

### Help Devra

The PC writes the letter. Guild seal, guild credential, a formal
introduction requesting that the bearer be received and given
standing. It takes five minutes. It's the easiest thing the PC does
all night.

At first light, Devra packs her bag, takes the letter and the
copied messages, and walks into the clearing dust. She doesn't say
goodbye to Ossal. She doesn't look back at the relay equipment.

The relay goes dark. Ossal sits at the desk, staring at the wire
receiver. He can operate it — he knows the protocols — but he's an
inspector, not an operator. His transcriptions will be slow and
error-prone. The settlements downstream will receive garbled or
delayed messages for weeks until a replacement arrives. Some of
those messages are medical requests. Some are supply orders. Some
are, perhaps, instructions from something that isn't human. All of
them will be late.

Devra reaches Aldgate. The letter gets her through the gate. What
she does with the copied messages — whether anyone outside the
Kesharat system can evaluate them, whether the evidence means
anything without context — is beyond the scope of the dungeon.

### Help Ossal

The PC declines the letter and helps Ossal secure the post. This
can go two ways.

If the PC confronts Devra openly, she doesn't fight. She's not a
fighter. She's an operator. She argues — passionately, logically,
with the specific evidence from her log — and when it's clear the
PC isn't going to help her, she sits down at her desk and goes
quiet. Ossal confiscates her copies. The originals remain in the
official file. The relay stays operational.

If the PC tries to take the copies by deception or force, Devra
resists. Not violently — she hides the copies in the storeroom, or
tries to destroy them. A perception check determines whether the PC
finds them. If the PC fails, Devra burns the copies in the brazier.
The evidence is gone. The transcriptions in the official file remain,
but without Devra's annotations they're just routine messages with
slightly unusual content.

Either way, the relay stays open. The settlements keep their
connection. Devra remains at her post, filing messages she believes
are inhuman, transmitting orders she can't verify, maintaining a
system she no longer trusts. She does her job. She does it well.
She does it with the particular precision of someone who has stopped
believing in the work but can't stop doing it.

Ossal files his inspection report: post operational, personnel
stable, no anomalies. He believes this. The report is accurate in
every particular and false in every way that matters.

### Refuse both

The PC declines to write the letter and declines to help with the
arrest. The PC is a traveler sheltering from a storm. This isn't
their relay, their system, or their problem.

At first light, the PC leaves. Devra and Ossal remain in the post,
alone with each other and the wire, the storm clearing outside, the
messages still coming. What happens between them — whether Devra
runs, whether Ossal acts, whether the relay stays open or goes dark
— is decided by two people in a stone building on the mesa, without
the PC's intervention.

The PC walks away carrying only one thing from the night: the
knowledge that a message predicted their arrival at the relay post,
timestamped before they decided to shelter there, sent from no
identifiable origin. The system knew they were coming. The system
doesn't know anything. Both of these are true.
