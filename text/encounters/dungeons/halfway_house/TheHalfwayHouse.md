# The Halfway House (Mountains Tier 2)

A hostel on the switchback between the mining valleys and the scholar
institutions, run by a woman who was once a miner's wife and is now a
scholar's assistant. She carries petitions uphill and explanations
downhill. Both sides trust her completely. She's been editing the
messages — softening the miners' rage into language the scholars will
read, flattening the scholars' abstractions into advice the miners can
use. She's the only bridge between two kinds of justice, and the bridge
is built entirely on lies.

**Deeper concept:** Translation between incompatible moral frameworks
requires choosing what survives the crossing. Diplomacy is the art of
productive dishonesty, and the woman who understands both sides can only
serve them by betraying each.

**Hook:** The PC stops at the hostel and finds both a miner delegation
and a scholar delegation present. The hostess is running an elaborate
ruse to keep them separate. Both groups describe her differently.

**Clutch:** Does the PC collapse the ruse or allow it to continue?

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The Halfway House sits at the switchback where the mountain path turns
from steep to steeper — the last comfortable stop between the mining
valleys below and the scholar institutions above. It's a solid stone
building with a slate roof, a chimney putting out woodsmoke, and a
porch where travelers can sit and watch the valley floor recede into
haze. The kind of place that exists because the mountain is long and
people need to sleep somewhere.

When the PC arrives, the porch is occupied by three miners in work
clothes, sharing a pipe and speaking in low, tense voices. Through
the window, two scholars are visible at a table inside, papers spread
between them, writing intently.

Neither group acknowledges the other. The miners are waiting. The
scholars are working. The hostess is somewhere between them, managing
the fact that they're in the same building at the same time — which,
the PC will learn, was never supposed to happen.

## The Hostess

**Lenna** is a small, quick woman in her forties with a miner's hands
and a scholar's vocabulary. She greets the PC with the warmth of
someone who needs another body in the room to dilute the tension.

Her story, as she tells it to the PC over tea: she grew up in the
Tarnach valley. Married a miner. He died in a shaft collapse — the
usual story. She climbed the mountain looking for answers, found the
scholars instead, and stayed. She worked as a research assistant for
a few years, learned to read and write properly, and eventually came
back down to the switchback and opened the hostel.

What she doesn't say — but what becomes clear over the course of the
evening — is that the hostel is a front. The real work is the messages.

## The Messages

Lenna carries paper between the valleys and the institutions. Petitions
go up. Responses come down. She's the postal service, the translator,
and the editor. Both sides trust her because she speaks their language
— literally. She code-switches between the miners' dialect and the
scholars' formal register without thinking about it.

She's also rewriting everything.

The miners' petitions arrive at the hostel raw — angry, specific,
sometimes barely literate. A foreman cheated us on tonnage. A shaft
is flooding and the company won't shore it up. My brother died and
they won't release the body. Lenna takes these and rewrites them in
the scholars' idiom: measured, evidenced, framed in terms of systemic
pattern rather than individual grievance. The scholars receive
petitions that read like case studies. They respond with interest.

The scholars' responses arrive at the hostel in academic language —
dense, qualified, full of caveats and theoretical frameworks. We note
the structural parallels to the Kresh Valley incident of 4012 and
recommend a comparative analysis of inspection protocols across
jurisdictions. Lenna takes these and rewrites them as advice: don't
let the foreman weigh your haul alone; bring a witness. The miners
receive responses that sound like someone who understands their
situation.

Both sides think they're communicating directly. Neither side has
ever read the other's actual words.

## The Miner Delegation

The three miners on the porch are here to deliver a petition
in person. Their situation is urgent: a new shaft in the Tarnach
mine has been opened without proper inspection, and the company is
pressuring miners to work it. Two men have already refused and been
docked pay. The delegation wants the scholars to intervene — to send
a formal objection to the guild council, backed by scholarly
authority.

Their leader, **Garren**, is a compact man with a direct manner. He
describes Lenna as "the woman who gets things done." He trusts her
completely. He's never met a scholar in person and assumes they're
sympathetic allies who simply lack information. Every response he's
received — Lenna's rewritten versions — has confirmed this. The
scholars care. They just need to know what's happening.

He asks Lenna when he can meet the scholars upstairs. Lenna tells him
they're occupied with urgent research and will receive his petition
in writing. He accepts this. He's used to it.

## The Scholar Delegation

The two scholars inside are here on a different errand. They've been
compiling a comparative study of mining safety across the range and
need Lenna to distribute a survey to miners in the valley — a
questionnaire about working conditions, foreman behavior, inspection
frequency. It's thorough, well-designed, and written in language no
miner would sit still for.

The lead scholar, **Halle**, is a precise woman who describes Lenna
as "our fieldwork coordinator." She has enormous respect for Lenna's
ability to gather data from the mining communities. She's never spoken
to a miner directly and assumes they're inarticulate but earnest
laborers who need scholarly advocacy. Every petition she's received —
Lenna's rewritten versions — has confirmed this. The miners are
suffering. They just lack the framework to articulate it properly.

She asks Lenna when the survey can be distributed. Lenna says she'll
translate it into accessible language. Halle nods. She thinks "translate"
means simplify. It means rewrite entirely.

## The Crack

The PC can move freely between the porch and the main room. Both groups
are happy to talk to a traveler. The discrepancy becomes apparent
quickly: Garren describes responses from the scholars that Halle has
never written. Halle references petitions from the miners that Garren
has never sent. The words are different. The sentiments are different.
The entire relationship between the two groups exists only in Lenna's
handwriting.

If the PC confronts Lenna, she doesn't deny it. She's been expecting
this — not today specifically, but eventually. She pours the PC tea and
explains.

The miners' actual words would horrify the scholars. Not the content —
the tone. The rage, the profanity, the directness. Halle would read
Garren's real petition and see an uneducated man making demands. She
wouldn't hear the fear underneath. She'd respond with procedure, and
Garren would receive a response that sounds like indifference, and the
relationship would collapse in one exchange.

The scholars' actual words would enrage the miners. Not the content —
the distance. Garren would read Halle's real survey and see a woman who
treats his life as data. He wouldn't understand the genuine concern
underneath the methodology. He'd refuse to cooperate, and Halle would
conclude the miners are hostile to help, and the relationship would
collapse in one exchange.

Lenna is the only person who speaks both languages. She can't let them
speak directly, because their actual languages are mutually
unintelligible — not in vocabulary, but in the things that convey
respect, urgency, and good faith.

She asks the PC: is the truth more important than the outcome? Because
right now, the miners get advice that helps them, and the scholars get
data that motivates them, and both sides think they have an ally. The
alliance is a fiction. But the help is real.

## The Choice

### Collapse the ruse

The PC brings Garren inside. Or brings Halle outside. Or simply tells
both groups what Lenna has been doing.

The meeting is a disaster. Not violent — worse. Polite and bewildered.
Garren speaks and Halle stares at him like he's speaking a foreign
language. She responds and Garren's face closes. The scholars' concern
is real but sounds like condescension. The miners' urgency is real but
sounds like aggression. Within minutes, both sides are looking at Lenna
with betrayal in their eyes.

Garren leaves. He tells Lenna he'll find another way to reach people
who actually care. Halle packs her papers. She tells Lenna the survey
data from the valley is now unreliable — if Lenna was rewriting
petitions, she may have been rewriting survey responses too. She's
right. Lenna was.

The bridge collapses. Both sides lose their only channel of
communication. The miners' urgent petition about the unsafe shaft
goes undelivered. Halle's survey — which would have produced evidence
useful to the miners — is abandoned.

Lenna sits in the empty hostel and tells the PC: now you've seen
what honesty looks like. She's not bitter. She's just tired. She
knew this would happen eventually. She hoped it wouldn't be while
something urgent was on the table.

### Allow the ruse to continue

The PC says nothing. Lenna continues her work. Garren's petition
goes uphill, rewritten into scholarly language. Halle's survey goes
downhill, rewritten into plain speech. Both sides continue to believe
they have a sympathetic ally on the other end of the mountain.

The unsafe shaft gets a formal scholarly objection — not because the
scholars read Garren's words, but because Lenna's rewrite of his
words is compelling enough to move them. The survey gets completed —
not because the miners trust the scholars, but because Lenna's
rewrite of the survey makes it sound like someone who understands
their work.

The system works. The system is a lie. The PC walks away knowing that
every piece of cooperation between the miners and the scholars of
this mountain passes through one woman's judgment about what each
side can bear to hear. If she dies, or leaves, or loses her nerve,
the entire relationship collapses — and neither side will understand
why, because neither side knows what they've actually been saying to
each other.

### Help Lenna prepare the meeting she's afraid to hold

The hardest option. The PC argues that the ruse can't hold forever and
the current crisis — an unsafe shaft, miners being pressured to work
it — is important enough to risk the introduction. But not a raw
introduction. A managed one.

This requires a difficult persuasion check to convince Lenna. She's
terrified of this. She's spent years building a system that works
precisely because the two sides never meet.

If the PC succeeds: Lenna agrees to let Garren present his petition
directly to Halle — but the PC helps her coach both sides first.
Garren is told the scholars communicate formally and it doesn't mean
they don't care. Halle is told the miners communicate directly and
it doesn't mean they're hostile.

The meeting is awkward. Strained. Garren speaks too bluntly and
Halle flinches. Halle asks a clarifying question that sounds like
cross-examination and Garren bristles. But they're in the same room,
talking about the same shaft, and the facts are the facts regardless
of how they're delivered. It's painful and halting and it works —
barely.

The bridge holds, but it's a different bridge now. Thinner, more
fragile, built on real contact instead of comfortable fiction. It
might last. It might not. Lenna watches from the doorway, hands
clenched, and the PC can't tell if she's relieved or devastated that
the thing she built alone now belongs to other people.

If the PC fails the persuasion check: Lenna refuses. She can't risk
it. The ruse continues. Same outcome as "allow the ruse to continue,"
but the PC knows they tried.
