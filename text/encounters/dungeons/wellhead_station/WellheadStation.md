# Wellhead Station (Scrub Tier 2)

A Kesharat-built water distribution facility constructed over a spring
that a local clan controlled for generations. The new infrastructure
is objectively better: pumps, pipes, a schedule that ensures every
settlement in the area receives water. More efficient and more
equitable than the old arrangement. But the clan that held the spring
has lost its primary source of sovereignty and identity. During a
drought, the schedule doesn't change — and the question of who gets
cut first reveals what "equitable" means when the Lattice's priorities
are the ones being served.

**Deeper concept:** Efficiency and justice aren't the same thing. A
system can be objectively better at distributing a resource while
being objectively worse at everything that resource used to mean —
sovereignty, obligation, the binding power of hospitality. The
Kesharat improved the water and destroyed the well.

**Hook:** The PC arrives during a drought. A clan delegation is
demanding the functionary change the schedule. The schedule allocates
a share to a Kesharat construction site nobody knew existed.

**Clutch:** The construction site is a rail extension. The schedule is
"fair" only if the rail project counts as a legitimate recipient.
Sabotage the station, expose the hidden allocation, or broker a
compromise.

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The station is Kesharat engineering at its most persuasive: clean
lines, functional design, everything in its right place. A stone
pump house built over the spring, with copper pipes running outward
in four directions — one to each of the surrounding settlements.
Brass valves with calibrated handles. A posted schedule, neatly
printed, showing allocation times and volumes for each pipe. A
maintenance log, up to date, in a careful hand.

The spring itself is visible through a grated floor in the pump house
— clear water rising from the rock, the same water the Reshîd clan
has drawn from for six generations. Before the station, the spring
was the Reshîd's most important asset. It made them hosts. Every
clan in the area came to the Reshîd for water, and the Reshîd gave
it freely, and the giving created obligation, and the obligation
created a web of alliance and debt that made the Reshîd the center
of the region's social structure.

The station made all of that unnecessary. Now the water goes where
the schedule says it goes. The Reshîd are just another settlement
on the list.

The drought has been going for weeks. The spring is producing less
than usual. The pipes are running at reduced pressure. And a clan
delegation is standing in the pump house, voices raised, demanding
to see the schedule.

## The Functionary

**Aban** runs the station. He's a young Kesharat administrator —
polite, competent, and genuinely committed to the equitable
distribution of water. He's not a villain. He's not even insensitive.
He grew up in a Kesharat settlement where the water infrastructure
worked, and he believes — sincerely — that this is a better system
than the old arrangement.

He shows the delegation the schedule with the patience of someone
who believes in the schedule. Each settlement gets an allocation
proportional to its population, adjusted for agricultural needs,
delivered on a rotating basis. The math is transparent. The system
is fair.

The system also includes a fifth pipe — a line running southeast,
toward a site labeled "KA-7" on the schedule. The allocation for
KA-7 is modest but real: enough water to supply a small work crew.
Nobody in the delegation has heard of KA-7. Nobody in the
surrounding settlements has seen a fifth pipe leave the station
before, because the pipe runs underground and the connection was
made during a maintenance closure three months ago.

Aban explains, if asked, that KA-7 is a sanctioned Kesharat project
and its allocation was approved by the regional administration. He
has the paperwork. He's uncomfortable — not because he's hiding
something, but because he's realizing, as he speaks, that the
paperwork was filed without local consultation. He followed
procedure. The procedure didn't include asking.

## The Reshîd

The clan delegation is led by **Heshma**, an elder of the Reshîd — a
woman in her sixties with the bearing of someone who has spent a
lifetime being the person other people come to. She's not shouting.
She's worse than shouting: she's calm, precise, and visibly
restraining herself from doing something she can't take back.

Her argument isn't about water volume. It's about what the water
means. The Reshîd spring was a gift-giving institution. The clan's
identity was built on generosity — you came to the Reshîd for water,
and they gave it, and the giving was a social act that bound the
region together. The station replaced the gift with a schedule. The
drought is exposing what that replacement costs.

Under the old system, the Reshîd would increase water distribution
during a drought — give more, not less, because drought is when
generosity matters most. The obligation created by drought-giving was
the deepest kind: the kind people remember for generations. The
station's schedule doesn't increase during drought. It reduces
proportionally. Everyone gets less. Nobody owes anyone anything.

And now a fifth of the reduced supply is going to a construction
site the Reshîd didn't know existed, built on land the Reshîd
consider theirs, fed by the spring the Reshîd tended for six
generations.

Heshma tells the PC: this isn't about water. It's about whether the
Reshîd still exist in any way that matters. The station took their
sovereignty. The schedule took their generosity. The hidden pipe
took their dignity. What's left?

## KA-7

If the PC investigates the construction site — a half-day walk
southeast, following the buried pipe — they find a rail extension.
Foundation work: concrete forms, graded earth, surveyor's stakes
marking a line that extends toward the horizon. A small Kesharat
work crew, maybe eight people, mixing concrete with the water from
the spring.

The foreman is cooperative. He shows the PC the project plans: a
rail spur connecting the mesa corridor to the Kesharat trunk line.
When completed, it will allow goods and personnel to move through
clan territory at administrative speed. The foreman says this like
it's good news. More trade. More connection. More services for the
settlements along the route.

The route passes through Reshîd grazing land. And the land beyond.
And the land beyond that. The rail doesn't stop at the mesa. It
connects to a network that extends to the Kesharat capital and
beyond — the Lattice's infrastructure, spreading at the pace of
poured concrete, one approved project at a time.

The water allocation for KA-7 is small. The implications are not.
Every day the concrete cures, the rail gets longer, and the
administrative reach of the Kesharat extends further into clan
territory. The water schedule didn't just subtract from the Reshîd's
allocation. It's funding the infrastructure that will make the
Reshîd irrelevant.

## The Choice

### Sabotage the station

The PC damages the pump mechanism — breaks a valve, clogs a pipe,
disables the distribution system. This requires a crafting check.
The spring keeps flowing, but the pipes stop carrying.

The water returns to the surface. The Reshîd can draw from the
spring directly, the way they always did. The surrounding
settlements lose their piped supply and must come to the Reshîd for
water — which is exactly what Heshma wants. The old system
reasserts itself. The Reshîd are hosts again. The gift economy
restarts.

The cost: the station was the only equitable distribution system in
the region. "Equitable" was compromised by the hidden allocation,
but the infrastructure itself served every settlement, including
ones too small or too distant to walk to the Reshîd spring. Those
settlements now depend on the Reshîd's generosity, which is real but
conditional — the Reshîd give, and the giving creates obligation,
and obligation is a form of power. The old system wasn't fair. It
was gracious, which is a different thing.

Aban reports the sabotage. The Kesharat will send engineers to
repair the station. The repair will take weeks. The drought will be
over or it won't. The rail construction at KA-7 halts — no water,
no concrete. The foreman packs up. He'll be back.

### Expose the hidden allocation

The PC brings the KA-7 information back to the pump house and
presents it to both Heshma and Aban, publicly, in front of the
delegation.

Aban is mortified. He didn't know the pipe was feeding a rail
extension. He knew "KA-7" was a sanctioned project. He didn't ask
what it was. He followed the schedule. The schedule was the system.
The system was supposed to be fair.

Heshma is not surprised. She suspected something like this. She
tells the delegation, and the delegation tells the other clans,
and by nightfall every settlement in the area knows that the
Kesharat has been siphoning drought water to pour concrete on
Reshîd land.

The confrontation at the station turns volatile. The delegation
wants the fifth pipe cut. Aban can't authorize that — it requires
regional approval. The delegation is past asking for authorization.
A fight check determines whether the PC can keep the situation from
turning violent. Success means the delegation cuts the pipe
themselves — controlled, deliberate, symbolic. Failure means the
pump house is damaged in the scuffle, and the station goes offline
for everyone while the mess is sorted out.

Either way, the rail project is exposed. The Kesharat administration
will respond — with diplomacy or with force, depending on factors
beyond the PC's control. The clans now know what the infrastructure
is for. That knowledge doesn't stop the rail, but it changes every
future negotiation.

### Broker a compromise

The PC negotiates between Heshma and Aban. The shape of the deal:
the Kesharat formally acknowledges the Reshîd's historical
relationship to the spring. The water schedule is revised — the
KA-7 allocation is suspended during drought conditions, and the
Reshîd receive a priority allocation that recognizes their role as
the spring's traditional stewards. In exchange, the Reshîd accept
the station's continued operation and don't oppose the rail
extension.

This requires a difficult persuasion check. Heshma is reluctant —
the compromise legitimizes the station and the rail. Aban is
reluctant — the compromise creates a precedent for clan sovereignty
claims over Kesharat infrastructure.

If the PC succeeds: both sides sign. The deal holds for now. The
Reshîd get symbolic recognition and a drought provision. The
Kesharat keep the station and the rail route. Heshma walks away
knowing she traded the spring's future for a concession on the
schedule. Aban walks away knowing he handed a clan legal standing
over infrastructure they'll use as leverage in every future dispute.

The compromise works. The compromise is also the mechanism by which
the Reshîd formally accept Kesharat authority over their own spring.
In a year, the rail will be built, the Kesharat will control the
corridor, and the drought provision will be a footnote in an
administrative file. Heshma knows this. She signs anyway, because
the alternative — violence or sabotage — is worse. She's not wrong.
She's also not winning.

If the PC fails: Heshma refuses. She won't legitimize the station.
The standoff continues. The PC must choose between the other two
options or leave.
