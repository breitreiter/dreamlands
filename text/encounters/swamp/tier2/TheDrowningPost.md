# The Drowning Post (Swamp Tier 2)

A colonial-era punishment post, half-submerged in the bog. The empire
chained prisoners here at low tide. The Revathi avoid the place. But
Revënakh doesn't — the post is ringed with bioluminescent activity,
and at night, composite figures appear around it, re-enacting the
drownings in a continuous loop. A young revivalist has been coming
here to listen. The figures are speaking fluent old Revathikh. The
words are the last words of the dying, preserved and replayed without
context or mercy.

**Deeper concept:** The swamp preserves suffering with the same
fidelity it preserves everything else. You can recover a dead language
from the mouths of the dead, and the grammar will be impeccable, and
the content will be agony. The revivalist wants the language. The
language comes packaged in the worst moments of the people who spoke
it. You can't separate the two.

**Hook:** The PC finds the revivalist transcribing at dusk. She's
reconstructed verb conjugations, possessive forms, hundreds of words.
She's also visibly unwell. An elder is trying to convince her to leave.

**Clutch:** The elder asks the PC to destroy the tablets. The
revivalist asks the PC to protect them. The figures keep speaking.

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The bog thins here. The canopy opens onto a stretch of dark water,
still as glass, edged with reeds that don't move because there's
never wind this deep in the swamp. At the center of the clearing,
a wooden post rises from the water — old, blackened, the grain
swollen and split by centuries of submersion. Iron rings are bolted
to it at intervals: wrist height, throat height.

The water around the post is faintly luminous. Not the diffuse glow
the PC has seen elsewhere in the swamp — this is concentrated, active,
pulsing in slow rhythms that don't correspond to anything biological.
Revënakh is paying attention to this place.

On a dry hummock near the clearing's edge, a young woman sits cross-
legged with a wax tablet on her knees and a stylus in her hand. She's
writing fast, head tilted, listening to something the PC can't hear
yet.

An older man stands behind her, arms folded, watching her work with
an expression that mixes respect and horror in equal measure.

## The Revivalist

**Neshë** is twenty-two and looks older. She's thin — not malnourished,
but the kind of thin that comes from forgetting the body. Her eyes
are ringed dark. Her hands are steady. She's been here for six weeks.

She's a language revivalist — one of a small number of young Revathi
who've dedicated themselves to recovering old Revathikh, the original
language that died when the cities fell. What survives in the villages
is Revashu, a creole that blends Revathikh roots with imperial grammar.
The old language exists only in fragments: carved inscriptions, a
handful of transcribed songs, loanwords that survived the pidginization.

Neshë has recovered more old Revathikh in six weeks than every other
revivalist combined has recovered in decades. She has verb
conjugations — full paradigms, regular and irregular. She has
possessive forms, plural markers, evidential suffixes that indicate
how the speaker knows what they're saying. She has a vocabulary of
over four hundred words, organized by semantic field, with usage
notes.

Her tablets are extraordinary. They represent a genuine linguistic
breakthrough. The grammar she's reconstructed is elegant, complex,
and internally consistent. It is, by any scholarly standard, real.

She's happy to show the PC her work. She's proud of it. She's also
vibrating with the particular energy of someone who hasn't slept
properly in weeks and has stopped being able to tell whether that's
a problem.

## The Figures

At dusk, the bioluminescence brightens. The water around the post
begins to move — not with current but with shape. Figures emerge.
Luminous, translucent, human-shaped. They stand in the water around
the post, and they move through a sequence the PC recognizes, after
a moment, as a re-enactment.

Hands are chained to rings. Water rises. Figures struggle, speak,
fall silent. The sequence takes about ten minutes. Then it resets
and begins again. The figures are not ghosts. They're Revënakh's
archive — composite reconstructions assembled from whatever
biological and sensory data the organism absorbed at the time.

They are speaking. The PC can hear it once the light steadies — low,
rhythmic, a language that sounds nothing like the Revashu spoken in
the stilt-villages. It's more musical, more inflected, with sounds
that don't exist in the creole. It's beautiful in the way that
something can be beautiful when you don't understand it.

Neshë understands it. She's been listening for six weeks. The words
are prayers, pleas, names called out, final messages to people who
aren't there. "Tell my daughter." "I didn't do what they said."
"The water is cold." The grammar is perfect. Every sentence is a
death.

## The Elder

**Revakh** is in his sixties, a village elder from one of the
upstream stilt-communities. He tracked Neshë here after her family
asked for help. He's been here for two days, trying to talk her
into leaving. She won't go.

Revakh is not unsympathetic to the revivalist cause. He speaks some
Revathikh himself — the songs, the inscriptions, the fragments that
survived. He understands what Neshë has accomplished. He calls it
remarkable. He also calls it poisoned.

He explains to the PC, away from Neshë, speaking quietly:

Every word she's recovered comes from someone dying. Not just any
death — drowning. Slow, terrified drowning, chained to a post by
an empire that used the punishment as entertainment. The vocabulary
is the vocabulary of extremity: water, cold, breath, chains, gods,
family, please. The verb forms are conjugated in the present tense
of crisis. The evidential suffixes — the markers that indicate how
the speaker knows something — appear almost exclusively in the form
that means "I am experiencing this right now."

The language Neshë has recovered is grammatically complete and
semantically devastated. It's a language in which the most complex
and fluent utterances are all about the same thing: the moment of
dying.

If she teaches this to the villages, the first generation of new
speakers will learn old Revathikh through its worst vocabulary.
They'll learn "water" as the thing that drowns you. They'll learn
"cold" as the sensation of dying. They'll learn to conjugate verbs
in the tense of desperation. The language will shape how they think,
because language always does, and this language thinks in agony.

Revakh asks: does the PC want to help him convince Neshë to stop?
Or failing that, to destroy the tablets?

## Neshë's Position

If the PC talks to Neshë about Revakh's concerns, she listens. She's
heard the argument. She has an answer.

Every language carries trauma. Imperial common was shaped by conquest
— half its vocabulary is military terminology repurposed for daily
life. The Revashu they speak in the villages is a creole born from
subjugation, its grammar a scar of colonization. No language is
clean. No language arrives without history.

Old Revathikh wasn't always a language of drowning. It was a language
of trade, of poetry, of law, of love. The drowning post captured
one moment — the worst moment — and Revënakh preserved it because
Revënakh preserves everything. The fact that the worst moment is the
most linguistically rich isn't evidence that the language is
poisoned. It's evidence that people are most articulate when they're
most desperate.

She can build from this. The grammar she's recovered is a scaffold.
Once the structure is established, new vocabulary can be generated —
words for eating, for building, for greeting, for joy. The old
words may carry the shadow of how they were recovered, but the new
words won't. A living language outgrows its sources.

She asks the PC: her people have been silent for generations. Is a
language of suffering worse than no language at all?

## The Choice

### Protect the tablets

The PC supports Neshë. Revakh is overruled — not by authority, but
by the fact that Neshë won't leave and the PC won't help force her.

Neshë continues her work. The tablets go back to the village with her
when she's ready — another few weeks, she says, to complete the
pronoun system and the numeral forms. She'll teach classes. She'll
train apprentices. The old language will return to the Revathi
villages for the first time in centuries.

It will return shaped by the post. The first words the new speakers
learn will be the words of the dying. The songs they compose will
be built on a grammar of extremity. Whether the language outgrows
its source — whether joy can be expressed in a tongue recovered from
agony — is a question that won't be answered for a generation.

Revakh returns to his village. He doesn't interfere. He doesn't
support the revival, either. When the first classes are held, he
doesn't attend. His granddaughter does.

### Destroy the tablets

The PC helps Revakh. This can happen two ways.

If Neshë is away from the tablets — bathing, sleeping, gathering
food — the PC and Revakh can break the wax tablets and scatter the
fragments in the bog. The work is gone. Six weeks of the most
significant linguistic recovery in Revathi history, dissolved into
the swamp that provided it.

If Neshë is present, this requires either a deception to get her
away from the tablets or a direct confrontation. Neshë will not
fight — she'll try to shield the tablets with her body. The PC must
physically move her. This is ugly and the PC knows it's ugly. Neshë
doesn't scream or cry. She goes quiet, which is worse.

Either way, the tablets are destroyed. The language returns to the
swamp. The figures at the post continue their loop — speaking,
drowning, resetting — to an empty clearing. The archive remains.
The transcription is gone.

Neshë goes home. She doesn't speak to the PC again. She may return
to the post. She may not. The grammar is in her head — she can't
un-learn what she's heard. But without the tablets, she can't teach
it systematically. The revival stalls. The language stays dead, or
nearly dead, preserved only in one young woman's memory and in the
swamp that archives everything and understands none of it.

### Convince Neshë to expand the source

The hardest path. The PC argues that the tablets are valuable but
incomplete — not because the grammar is wrong, but because the
vocabulary is drawn from a single context. The language needs more
sources. The blind-complex has other sites, other archives, other
moments preserved. If Neshë can find Revënakh recordings of daily
life — markets, craft work, conversation — she can balance the
drowning vocabulary with the vocabulary of living.

This requires a difficult persuasion check. Neshë is resistant —
she's spent six weeks here and the post is the richest source she's
found. The other sites in the blind-complex are fragmentary and
harder to interpret. The post is clear because extremity is clear.
Normal life is muddled.

If the PC succeeds: Neshë agrees to leave the post and spend time
at other reception sites. The work will be slower. The vocabulary
will be thinner. But the language she eventually teaches won't be
built exclusively on the words of the dying. It will be incomplete
in a different way — patchy, with gaps where the swamp's archive
is less vivid — but it won't carry the weight of the post in every
syllable.

Revakh accepts this compromise. He's not happy, but he can live
with a language recovered from fragments of daily life. It's the
language recovered from death that he can't stomach.

If the PC fails: Neshë won't leave. She's too close. The post is
too rich. The PC must choose between the first two options.
