# Inkwell Hollow (Forest Tier 2)

A quiet camp in the deep wood where a woman has been collecting every exile's
real name and story in a coded journal. She uses actual ink — a luxury out
here — and writes in a careful hand. She says she's preserving their history
so they won't be forgotten. The journal is also, if it ever leaves the
forest, a complete intelligence file.

**Deeper concept:** The desire to be remembered and the need to be invisible
are irreconcilable. The journal is simultaneously the most precious thing in
the exile camps and the most dangerous. Personhood is exactly what makes
people vulnerable.

**Hook:** The PC arrives at the camp to find the woman surrounded by an angry
group of exiles who have discovered what she's been writing.

**Clutch:** The PC must decide the fate of the journal — and of the woman
who wrote it.

# Scene Sketch

## The Hollow

The camp is tucked into a depression between the roots of several massive
trees, sheltered by a natural overhang of earth and root. It's small —
three lean-tos, a fire pit, drying racks. A quiet place, deliberately
hard to find. The sort of camp where people come to disappear completely.

The PC hears the argument before they see the camp. Raised voices, several
of them, and one calm voice underneath.

## The Confrontation

**Neve** is sitting on a stump with a leather satchel in her lap. She's a
thin woman with ink-stained fingers and a scholar's posture — straight-backed,
still, watching the people shouting at her with an expression that is closer
to sorrow than fear.

The group surrounding her is five exiles from nearby camps. Their leader is
**Breck**, a broad man with a deep scar across his chin. He's holding one
of Neve's journals — a hand-bound book, dense with small writing in a cipher
the exiles can't read.

Breck found the journal while Neve was away from camp. He can't read the
cipher, but he can read the index: real names, organized by camp, cross-
referenced by date of arrival. He recognized his own name. He recognized
everyone's name.

Breck is not violent by nature, but he is terrified. His wife and son are
in one of those camps. Their names are in that book.

## Neve's Explanation

If the PC engages Neve, she speaks plainly. She's been in the forest for
six years. She was a clerk in a trade house before she ran. She started
writing because she watched people arrive in the camps, live for a few
years, and vanish — dead, moved on, taken by a patrol — and nobody
remembered them. No record that they existed. The forest erases people.

She decided that was unacceptable. She began asking for stories. People
told her willingly — exiles are lonely, and someone who listens with real
attention is rare. She wrote everything in cipher. She keeps the key in
her head. Nobody else can read the journals.

She has fourteen journals spanning six years. Hundreds of names. Origins,
crimes (real and alleged), family connections, skills, stories. The most
complete record of the exile population ever assembled.

She knows what the journals are. She's not naive. She says: "If the
empire found these, they could empty the forest in a month." She pauses.
"If the empire never finds these, then these people never existed. I've
watched that happen. I won't let it happen again."

## Breck's Position

Breck is sympathetic to Neve's motives but unmoved by them. His argument
is simple: he didn't consent to being documented. Nobody asked him. Nobody
told him that the quiet woman with the ink was writing down his name and
his wife's name and the location of his camp. His family's safety was
traded for Neve's project, and she made that trade unilaterally.

He wants the journals burned. All of them. Now.

Another exile, **Callë**, backs Breck but goes further. She says Neve
herself is a threat — she has the cipher key in her head. Burning the books
doesn't solve the problem if the woman who wrote them is still walking
around. Callë doesn't say "kill her" directly. She says: "We need to be
sure this is finished."

A third exile, **Dorin**, is quiet and conflicted. He tells the PC
privately that Neve wrote down his daughter's name — his daughter who died
of fever two winters ago in one of the camps. Nobody else remembers her.
He wants the journals to survive, but he can't say that in front of Breck.

## The Clutch

### Side with Breck — Burn the Journals

The PC helps Breck build a fire and Neve's fourteen journals go into it.
Six years of work, hundreds of names, the only record of people the
forest swallowed without trace. Neve watches without speaking. The ink
burns blue.

Breck is visibly relieved. Callë is satisfied but still watches Neve.
Dorin stands apart, his face unreadable. His daughter's name is ash now.
Nobody will ever know she lived here.

Neve is told to leave the forest. She packs her satchel — empty now except
for a bottle of ink and a few blank pages — and walks. As she passes the
PC, she says quietly: "I'll start again somewhere. I have a good memory."
Breck hears this. He doesn't stop her. But his relief is gone.

### Side with Neve — Protect the Journals

The PC stands with Neve against the group. This requires either
intimidation or persuasion.

If the PC succeeds: Breck backs down, but the camps will never trust Neve
again. She'll have to move deeper into the forest, further from the people
she's documenting. The journals survive, but the project is over — nobody
will speak to her now. She'll preserve what she has and write no more.

Dorin finds the PC afterward and thanks them. He asks if the PC would
remember a name for him: **Asha**. His daughter. Just in case.

If the PC fails: the confrontation turns physical. Breck's people grab
the satchel. In the scuffle, Neve is hurt — not badly, but enough. The
journals are torn and scattered. Some pages are recoverable. Most aren't.
The camp fractures. Neve limps into the forest with what she can carry.

### Propose a Compromise — Neve Destroys the Index

The PC suggests Neve burn the index — the cross-reference of real names to
camps — but keep the stories. Without the index, the journals are a
collection of anonymous narratives. Rich, human, and useless as
intelligence.

Neve considers this for a long time. She says: "Anonymous stories are
literature. That's not what I was doing." But she tears out the index
pages and feeds them to the fire.

Breck watches the index burn and nods. It's not enough for Callë, but
Breck tells her it's done. The group disperses.

Neve sits in the hollow with her diminished journals. The stories are
intact. The names are gone. She tells the PC: "Someone will read these
in a hundred years and think they're folk tales." She picks up her pen.
She's not sure what to write next.
