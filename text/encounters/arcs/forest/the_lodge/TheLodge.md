# The Lodge (Forest Tier 3)

The hunter's dwelling, deep in tier 3. Austere, orderly, maintained with
quiet care. Good tools on pegs. A bed. Dried meat on a rack. And a wall
of small personal objects — a compass, a locket, a child's toy, a letter
never sent — each tagged with a date and a brief description in calm,
warm handwriting. "The one who sang." "The one who almost found the blind."
"The one who prayed."

**Deeper concept:** Total freedom from obligation allows perfect perception
of others — and perfect indifference to their fate. The hunter sees beauty
in people the way a naturalist sees beauty in a rare bird: with
appreciation, with attention, without kinship. The most free person in the
world is the one who can watch you clearly, like you, and let you die.

**Hook:** The PC finds the Lodge while lost in tier 3 — the first shelter
they've seen in days. The door is open. The hunter is not home. The journal
on the desk describes the PC.

**Clutch:** The hunter returns. The price of safe passage out is silence —
and becoming another entry in the journal.

# Scene Sketch

## Lost

The PC has been in deep forest for days. Tier 3 is different from the exile
territory — no camps, no trails, no signs of human passage. The canopy is
so dense that direction loses meaning. The undergrowth thins out in the
deep shade, leaving cathedral spaces between enormous trunks. It's
beautiful and it's empty and the PC is running low on provisions.

The Lodge appears between the trees like something that grew there. Timber
and stone, low-roofed, fitted so neatly into the terrain that the PC
nearly walks past it. Moss on the north wall. A thin column of old smoke
from the chimney — someone was here recently. The door is ajar.

## The Lodge

Inside is a single room, the most orderly living space the PC has ever
seen. Everything has a place and is in it. Woodworking tools on pegs,
arranged by size. A bow and a quiver on hooks. A knife with a wrapped
handle, well-used and well-kept. A cot with a wool blanket folded in
thirds. Dried meat on a rack. A clay jug of water. A small iron stove,
still warm.

The room smells like wood shavings and smoke and something faintly animal.
It's warm. It's safe. After days of sleeping in the wet forest, it feels
like sanctuary.

## The Wall

One wall is different. From floor to ceiling, small objects are mounted
on wooden pegs, each with a handwritten tag. Dozens of them.

A brass compass, dented: *"The cartographer. Sang to herself while she
worked. Three days."*

A child's carved horse, one leg broken: *"The boy from the commune. Braver
than he knew. One day."*

A locket with a pressed flower inside: *"The one who prayed at every
stream crossing. Spoke to someone I couldn't see. Five days."*

A belt buckle, imperial issue: *"The deserter. Wept on the second night.
Quick."*

A length of blue ribbon: *"The one who almost found the blind. Clever hands.
I was impressed. Two days."*

The tags are written in the same hand — steady, precise, warm. The
descriptions are affectionate. The writer noticed these people with real
attention: their habits, their small beauties, their particular ways of
being human.

The dates span years.

## The Journal

On the desk is a leather-bound journal. The handwriting matches the tags.
The entries are brief and observational, like a naturalist's field notes.

Recent entries describe a traveler in the deep forest. The description
matches the PC — their gear, their gait, their habits. The writer has been
watching for days. The observations are detailed and generous. The writer
notes things the PC does unconsciously: how they check the wind, how they
set their pack down, the specific way they scan the canopy. The tone is
the same warmth as the wall tags.

The most recent entry, from this morning: *"Heading toward the lodge now.
I've left the door open. Tired and hungry. They'll go in."*

The last line: *"I like this one."*

## The Hunter

The PC hears nothing. No footstep, no branch, no warning. **The Hunter**
is simply there in the doorway, leaning against the frame. A lean figure
in worn leathers, somewhere between forty and sixty, with the kind of
stillness that comes from years of being the thing in the forest that
doesn't need to move.

They smile. It reaches their eyes. They are genuinely glad to see the PC.

"I see you found the meat. Good. You needed it."

The Hunter enters and moves around the room with the ease of long habit.
They build up the fire, set water to heat, produce a second cup from
somewhere. They are unhurried and entirely comfortable. They do not touch
or reach for any weapon, though the PC is between them and the bow.

They talk. The Hunter is articulate, curious, and disarmingly warm. They
ask the PC questions — not interrogation, but the questions of someone who
finds people genuinely interesting. Where are you from? What brought you
this deep? What do you do when you're afraid?

They listen to the answers with their full attention. The PC has not been
listened to like this in a long time. Maybe ever.

## The Understanding

At some point — it's hard to say when — the nature of the conversation
shifts. The Hunter doesn't threaten. They don't explain. But the shape of
things becomes legible.

The PC looks at the wall of objects and understands that these are not
gifts. They are mementos. Every object belonged to someone who came through
this part of the forest. Every tag is an obituary written by the person who
watched them die.

The Hunter doesn't deny this if confronted. They don't justify it either.
They say, simply: "The forest is mine. I know every path, every water
source, every shelter. People come in and I watch them. Most of them I
never meet. I just... observe. Learn who they are. Some of them are
remarkable."

A pause.

"None of them find their way out."

The Hunter explains that the deep forest is genuinely lethal. The paths
loop. The water sources are miles apart. Without knowledge of the terrain,
no one survives more than a week. The Hunter has that knowledge. They
could guide anyone out in a day.

They have never done so.

## The Offer

The Hunter offers the PC food, rest, and a guided path out of tier 3 at
dawn. They know the way. They're the only one who does. The price is
unspoken but legible from the journal and the wall:

The PC leaves. The PC says nothing about the Lodge. The PC becomes another
entry — a living one, this time, with a warm description and a note about
how they checked the wind.

The Hunter is not lying. They will guide the PC out. They have no reason
to lie. They also have no reason to help, except that they like the PC,
and liking someone has never, in the Hunter's experience, implied any
obligation whatsoever.

## The Clutch

### Accept the Offer

The PC sleeps in the Lodge. The Hunter keeps watch. In the morning, they
walk together through the deep forest, and the Hunter points out the
landmarks — a split trunk, a moss pattern, a stone formation — that mark
the safe path. It would be impossible to find without a guide.

They walk in companionable silence. The Hunter is good company. At the
edge of tier 2, where the exile trails begin, the Hunter stops. They hand
the PC a small object — something from the PC's own pack, something
personal, something the PC didn't notice was missing.

"For the wall," the Hunter says. "I hope you don't mind. I'll write
something kind."

The PC walks out of the deep forest. Behind them, the Hunter is already
gone. The path they walked is already unreadable. The Lodge might as well
not exist.

Somewhere in the tier 3 forest, a wall has a new peg.

### Fight

The PC attacks the most competent person in the forest, alone, in their
home, in a room where every tool is within the Hunter's reach and the
only exit is behind them.

If the PC wins: it is the hardest fight of their life. The Hunter fights
without rage or desperation — with the same calm attention they bring to
everything. When they fall, they look surprised. Not at dying, but at the
PC. They didn't expect this one to fight. They say something quiet that
the PC doesn't quite catch.

The Lodge is the PC's now, along with the journal, the wall, and the
knowledge that the safe path out is described nowhere. The PC will have to
find their own way. They are alive in a dead person's home, surrounded by
the keepsakes of everyone the forest has swallowed, and the door is open.

If the PC loses: the Hunter disarms them efficiently and without malice.
They set the PC's weapon aside, help them sit, and tend the wound they
just inflicted. They are not angry. They say: "That was brave. I'll
remember it."

In the morning, the Hunter guides the PC out anyway. The wall gets a
new entry. The tag says something about courage. The PC walks into
tier 2 with a bandaged arm and the knowledge that they were released
by someone who did not have to release them, for reasons that had nothing
to do with mercy.
