# Grainway Station (Plains Tier 2)

A sealed supply depot from before the retreat, over-provisioned for an
advance that never came. Inside: enough food, weapons, and equipment
for a full company, inventoried and slowly rotting. Both the military
remnants and the scavenger bands claim it. The depot has become the
flashpoint for the entire tier 2 power dynamic.

**Deeper concept:** Abundance in a scarcity zone doesn't resolve
conflict — it detonates it. The depot was meant to supply progress;
instead it supplies a power struggle between people the empire forgot.
And the food is spoiling while they argue.

**Hook:** Both sides want the PC as a neutral arbiter. Both sides
separately approach the PC with reasonable proposals for dividing the
supplies. Both proposals are designed to shaft the other side. Meanwhile
the depot stinks of rot.

**Clutch:** The food is spoiling faster than either side admits. Broker
a compromise, pick a side by force, or crack it open and let both
groups rush in.

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The depot is a squat stone building at a crossroads, built to last.
Imperial engineering — thick walls, a heavy iron door with a regimental
seal, narrow ventilation slits near the roofline. The kind of structure
that was meant to supply a campaign for months. The campaign lasted
weeks. The depot has been sealed ever since.

Two camps face each other across the crossroads. On the north side,
military remnants — a dozen soldiers in patched imperial uniforms,
maintaining formation out of habit. On the south side, scavengers —
roughly the same number, in mixed clothing, organized around family
groups rather than rank. Both camps have cookfires. Both camps are
watching the depot and each other.

The smell hits the PC before the politics do. Something inside the
depot is rotting. The ventilation slits carry a sweet, heavy stink that
hangs in the still air. Whatever's in there, it's not going to wait
forever.

## The Military Remnants

**Captain Aldric** commands the remnant detachment. He's a career
officer who was stationed here when the retreat order came and simply
never received the follow-up. His unit has been "holding position" for
years. They maintain watches, keep equipment clean, observe protocol.
It's an elaborate performance of normalcy that keeps them from
acknowledging they've been abandoned.

Aldric's claim is legal and specific: the depot was provisioned for
the imperial advance. His unit is the last imperial presence in the
region. The supplies belong to the army. He has the seal codes. He can
open the depot at any time. He hasn't, because opening it means
distributing the supplies, which means admitting the advance isn't
coming, which means his unit has no mission and no reason to exist.

He approaches the PC with a proposal: a sixty-forty split, military
majority. Weapons and equipment stay with his unit. The scavengers get
food and tools. It's reasonable on paper. In practice, the weapons are
the only things in the depot that haven't degraded, and keeping them
means keeping the only source of military power in the region.

## The Scavengers

**Dalla** speaks for the scavenger families. She's not a leader in any
formal sense — she's the one who talks because she's the one who's
good at it. She's sharp, funny when she can afford to be, and
completely clear-eyed about the situation.

Dalla's claim is practical: the military remnants don't use the
supplies and can't use them. They're a dozen soldiers with no orders,
no chain of command, and no campaign. The scavenger families — four of
them, maybe thirty people total — are the civilian population of this
stretch of plain. The depot was built to supply an advance that was
supposed to benefit them. The advance failed. The supplies are the
only benefit left.

Her proposal: an even split by weight, everything included. She
presents this as fairness. In practice, an even split by weight gives
the scavengers the bulk of the food and provisions (heavy, perishable)
and the remnants a pile of rusted equipment they already have versions
of. The valuable items — intact weapons, tools, sealed medical
supplies — would be divided by lot. Dalla knows she has more people
to draw lots.

## The Rot

The PC can examine the depot from outside. The ventilation slits are
narrow but angled for airflow, and the smell is informative. Grain
has gone to mold. Meat stores have spoiled. The sealed provisions
might still be good, but the bulk food — the stuff that would actually
feed thirty people through a winter — is actively decomposing.

A scavenger who's been camped here longest (a quiet man named **Osk**)
tells the PC, if asked, that the smell has gotten worse every week.
When they first arrived two months ago, it was faint. Now it's constant.
He estimates half the food stores are gone. Maybe more. Neither side
will admit this publicly because admitting the food is spoiling
acknowledges that the standoff is destroying the thing they're fighting
over.

Every day the PC spends here, the depot loses value.

## The Proposals Unravel

If the PC shuttles between camps comparing proposals, the math stops
working quickly. Both sides have inflated their numbers and minimized
the other's needs. Both sides are also quietly aware that the food
is spoiling and the standoff is the problem, but admitting that means
being the first to blink.

Aldric, if pressed privately, admits his unit can't hold the region
even with the weapons. He has twelve soldiers and no resupply. The
weapons are symbolic — proof that the empire is still here. Without
them, he's a man in a costume.

Dalla, if pressed privately, admits the scavenger families could
survive the winter without the depot. It would be thin and ugly, but
they've done it before. The depot isn't survival — it's comfort and
security. She wants it because having it means not being desperate,
and not being desperate means negotiating from strength next season.

Neither side is facing starvation. Both sides are facing the
difference between hardship and stability. The stakes are real but
not mortal — which makes the standoff harder to break, because nobody
is desperate enough to concede.

## The Choice

### Broker a compromise

The PC negotiates a split. The details matter less than the fact of
agreement — any division means Aldric opens the depot, which means
admitting the advance is over, which means his unit's mission ends.

The compromise works if the PC can find a formula both sides accept.
This requires a difficult persuasion check. Success produces a
workable split: food to the families, sealed equipment to the
remnants, weapons divided by some face-saving mechanism.

Both sides comply. Neither side is satisfied. Aldric's soldiers begin
to drift away over the following weeks — without the mission, the
unit dissolves. Dalla's families get enough to make winter comfortable.
The power balance in the region shifts toward the scavengers, slowly
and permanently.

If the PC fails the check, one side rejects the terms. The standoff
continues. The PC can try another approach or leave.

### Pick a side and break the standoff

The PC sides openly with the remnants or the scavengers and helps
them take the depot by intimidation or force.

Siding with Aldric means helping a dozen armed soldiers secure the
depot against unarmed families. It's not a fight — it's a show of
force. Dalla pulls her people back. The remnants open the depot and
find what Osk predicted: half the food is spoiled. The sealed
equipment and weapons are intact. Aldric has supplies for a unit with
no mission. The scavenger families remember what happened.

Siding with Dalla means helping the families rush the depot before
Aldric's soldiers can organize a response. The remnants are
disciplined but outnumbered and demoralized. A fight check determines
whether violence breaks out. Success means the soldiers stand down.
Failure means a brief, ugly scuffle — injuries on both sides, no
deaths, but the kind of bad blood that lasts generations in a small
community. Either way, the families get the depot. Aldric's unit
collapses immediately.

### Crack the depot open and step back

The PC convinces Aldric to unseal the depot — or finds another way
in — and then steps back, letting both groups sort it out themselves.

This is the fastest resolution and the most dangerous. Both groups
rush the door. There is pushing, shouting, and a scramble for the
most valuable items. The perishable food gets trampled and scattered.
The weapons go to whoever grabs them first, which means the scavengers
(more people, more hands) end up armed and the soldiers end up
humiliated.

A fight check determines severity. Success means the rush is
chaotic but nonviolent — bruises, shouting, spilled grain, but
everyone walks away. Failure means someone draws a weapon and the
scramble turns bloody. One person is seriously hurt. The depot is
emptied either way, and what's left is damaged by the chaos.

The food spoilage is revealed to everyone at once. There's less than
either side thought. The scramble was for a prize that was already
half-gone. Both groups leave with armfuls of equipment and the
knowledge that they wasted two months guarding rot.
