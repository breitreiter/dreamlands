# The City — Scene Inventory

## Structure

Three acts. Acts 1 and 3 are chronological (on-rails). Act 2 is topographical (hub with explorable locations). The arc pivots on the ironic dissonance between what the reader understands, what the PC sees, and how Torben interprets — harmless at first, then increasingly strained, then catastrophic when the letter removes all ambiguity.

---

## ACT 1 — Approach & Entry (chronological)

### 1. The Perimeter
- **Location:** At the dome wall. The player has already traversed the surrounding tiles and fought through the outer district to get here.
- Meet Torben, sitting against the dome wall. He's been mapping the installation for three years, knows the outer district cold, but can't open the dome door.
- The dome door requires `grid_cipher` (from metal_beast arc). Torben has seen the Iron open it and deduced it's a signal. He mentions imperial engineers on the mid-plains — breadcrumb for players who don't have it yet.
- **Communicate:** Torben's professional competence and patience. He's been waiting for this. Keep it short — the player earned their way here, don't re-explain the setting.

### 2. The Gap
- **Location:** Inside the dome. The door opens with the cipher; the interior is the city proper.
- Torben narrates as they move through the entry corridor. His data on the interior security is accurate. His framework (military installation, defensive perimeter, kill zones) is professional and plausible.
- **Tac encounter:** Navigating a security corridor inside the dome. Clock-based, agility/perception. Torben's knowledge of patrol timing is genuinely useful — his observations are right, his interpretation is wrong, but in this moment the interpretation doesn't matter. Good data is good data.

## ACT 2 — The City Interior (topographical hub)

Hub scene. Player picks from available locations, any order. Torben interprets each location through his military framework. His reads are mostly plausible — the kind of thing you'd nod along with if you had no reason to doubt him. The details that don't fit are there, but quiet. None of these scenes are plot-critical; all are atmosphere. The reveal belongs to Act 3.

### 2.5 The Plaza
- **Location:** Open air atrium near the entrance, serves as a hub
- ornate glass tilework, strange geometric statue, long-dead planter beds. air is stale, dust on everything
- **Torben's read:** Looks orderly, maybe a progenitor of the Kesharat. Worship of geometric deities.
- **Communicate:** Should be ambiguous and foreign

### 3. The Depot
- **Location:** Warehouse/shipping facility near the entry corridor.
- Shelving units, inventory markings, desiccated packing material. Commercial scale.
- **Torben's read:** "Weapon racks." "Unit designations." He catalogs it without hesitation. And honestly? It's not an unreasonable read. Standardized shelving, coded labels, uniform containers — a quartermaster's depot looks a lot like a warehouse because it *is* one. Torben's interpretation is wrong but not stupid.
- **Communicate:** The details are there for attentive readers (packing material, commercial scale) but Torben's military frame is plausible. Don't underline the irony.

### 4. The Commons
- **Location:** Dining hall and adjacent residential block.
- Long tables, kitchen equipment (heating elements, prep surfaces, drainage), faded wall decoration. Small apartments: personal effects in drawers, decorative objects on shelves, clothing folded neatly.
- **Torben's read:** "Mess hall." "Barracks." He's mostly right about function — people ate here, people slept here. The distinction between garrison and neighborhood is in the details (decoration, personal effects, domestic scale), not the layout. Torben notes the decoration as "officer-class quarters" and moves on.
- **Communicate:** Orderly departure — nothing broken, nothing suggests evacuation. They packed what was worth packing and walked away. Let this observation sit without commentary.

### 5. The Exchange
- **Location:** Market or shop district.
- Display cases (empty), price markings in a denomination system, counters, back rooms with more shelving. Toy version of one of the massive Iron siege striders.
- **Torben's read:** PX. Toy striders are some sort of new miniaturized version. Or propaganda toys to indoctrinate children.
- **Communicate:** Torben has a read for everything. Some of his reads here are a stretch (the toy) but the PX interpretation is actually reasonable — military bases have shops. The denomination markings are the one detail that doesn't fit, but only if you're looking. Don't editorialize.

### 6. The Watchtower
- **Location:** Elevated structure at a junction, vantage point over the city layout.
- From up here the city's plan is visible: radial streets, central district, residential and commercial zones.
- Torben sketches it. He plots approach routes, choke points, fields of fire. He's planning how the empire could take this city with a proper campaign. His energy is infectious — he's good at this, it's clearly the work he was born to do.
- **Communicate:** Torben in his element. The sketch is interesting, the tactical analysis is sharp. Play this straight — a skilled professional doing what he does best. The reader doesn't need to know yet that his sketch is a weapon. That weight lands in Act 3.

## ACT 3 — The Archive & Confrontation (chronological)

### 7. The Archive
- **Location:** Administrative building, city center.
- Filing systems, record-keeping devices, screens that flicker when Torben leans on a console. Records in Grid script — incomprehensible, except for one section.
- A translation project. Someone in the city learned imperial language. The translated documents tell a simple story: regional trade outpost, seasonal population, resource processing and transport logistics. Parent civilization recalled personnel when the economy contracted. Standard procedure. Infrastructure left because decommissioning cost exceeded salvage. Security left on default protocol because the shutdown authorization belonged to an office that no longer existed.
- **Torben's read:** Strained but holding. Intelligence records. Bureaucratic cover for military operations. He's reaching, and for the first time it's visible effort rather than professional reflex.
- **Communicate:** The full mundane truth of the city, in terms even Torben can't easily reframe. But he's still trying.

### 8. The Envoy
- **Location:** Same building, deeper in. A chamber that looks ceremonial — or at least, someone's idea of ceremonial.
- Standing in the center of the room: a figure in golden armor, imperial in style but wrong in the details. The proportions are almost right. The crest is almost right. The stance is almost right. It's a composite, assembled from observation — the Grid studied the legion and built something that looked like what it saw. Beautiful craftsmanship. Uncanny result.
- The figure speaks. Halting, formal imperial. Grammatically strange. It addresses the PC (or Torben, or both) as though receiving a diplomatic delegation. It has been waiting to deliver its message.
- **Tac encounter (two approaches):**
  - **Combat (strength/agility):** The armor is a machine and it's sturdier than it looks. The golden plating is decorative but the frame underneath is Grid engineering. It doesn't fight like a soldier — it fights like a system responding to unauthorized interference with the delivery protocol. It's trying to complete its task, not win a battle.
  - **Diplomacy (cunning/negotiation):** Convince it you're the intended recipient. It wants to deliver its letter — it's been waiting for someone with authority to accept it. Its language processing is limited but functional. It asks questions to verify identity, and the "right" answers are about rank, command authority, willingness to receive correspondence on behalf of the expeditionary force. You're essentially impersonating a dead general to accept his mail.
- **Reward:** `golem_armor` (Heavy, Injured +5, Freezing +2). The envoy's golden armor. Combat: stripped from the frame after it's disabled. Diplomacy: the envoy removes it as part of completing its protocol — it was dressing for the occasion, and the occasion is over.
- On resolution (either path), the envoy produces the letter. A form letter in formal imperial language, translated from Grid administrative protocol. Addressed to the commanding general. Template-generated when the system detected an organized group rather than random wildlife.
- Apologizes for "the disruption." Explains the facility is unmanned, systems on automated protocol. Requests withdrawal to a safe distance. Provides boundary coordinates. Expresses hope that "future interactions may proceed with mutual awareness of operational parameters."
- The Grid figured out who was attacking it, learned their language, built a man out of metal and gold plate to look like them, taught it to speak, and sent it to deliver a polite letter asking them to stop. It has been standing in this room, waiting for someone to accept the message, ever since.
- Torben goes quiet.
- **Communicate:** The lie collapses — but it collapses under the weight of effort, not indifference. The Grid *tried*. It didn't just activate defense protocols. It built an envoy. It modeled the enemy. It learned the language. It did everything a civilization does when it wants to talk to another civilization, using the only tools it had. And nobody came to collect the letter. The envoy has been standing here in its beautiful wrong armor, holding a letter addressed to a dead man, for decades.

### 9. The Choice
- **Location:** The archive floor.
- Torben wants to destroy the archive, the letter, all evidence that the city was a trade outpost. Hand on his sword. He is not irrational — he can see the downstream consequences of the truth: stipends end, doctrine collapses, families learn their sons walked into a security fence.
- And he's holding a notebook full of sketches that could send another legion here.
- **Three paths:**
  - **Destroy the evidence.** Help Torben. The city stays a fortress. The dead stay heroes. Torben publishes his survey — the fortifications, the weapon emplacements, the invasion routes. The lie holds. The war that never was continues.
  - **Refuse.** Torben fights. Not a bluff — he's a man whose entire life is organized around this story. Combat check. Win: he yields, broken. Lose: he destroys the archive, leaves the PC alive. Either way, the truth exists only in memory, unprovable.
  - **Persuade him to carry it himself.** The hardest path. Torben is the only person the empire would believe. A stranger with an alien document is a rumor. A career cartographer with decades of service, standing in the proof, is testimony. Difficult persuasion check. Success: he takes the letter, doesn't know what he'll do with it yet, but won't destroy it. Failure: he can't. Destroys the archive quietly. Apologizes. Means it.
