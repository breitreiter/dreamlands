# The City (Plains Tier 3)

The big reveal. The evil Grid city was a normal mercantile settlement
from a futuristic culture. The inhabitants left ages ago — not because
of any exciting event, but because it became unprofitable. They left
their security systems running because turning them off costs more than
leaving them on. Landmine logic.

The defense Grid that laid waste to the imperial legions was a beefy
bug-zapper. It was just doing pest control.

**Deeper concept:** The stories of imperial valor and sacrifice holding
the line against monsters were a tragic misunderstanding. Imperial
explorers ventured too close, got killed, and the empire sent a legion
for revenge. The Grid killed them all. It wasn't halted by bravery — it
decided the pest situation was resolved and went back to standby.

**Hook:** The PC meets a hardened imperial cartographer outside the city.
He's found a way in and is happy to have company.

**Clutch:** Deep in the city, the PC and the cartographer find evidence
that the Grid had figured out the imperial language and delivered a
message to the legion's general, apologizing and asking them to keep
their distance. The cartographer wants to destroy the evidence — and
possibly the PC. It contradicts official history. It means brave men
died like vermin for no reason.

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The city announces itself by what it does to the landscape. The grass
stops in a clean line — not grazed or burned, just absent, as if the
ground ahead operates under different rules. Beyond the line, the
structures begin. They don't look like buildings. They look like
geometry that forgot to be architecture: smooth surfaces, no seams,
no windows at human height, proportions that suggest use by something
that didn't think about space the way people do.

A man is sitting against a boulder on the safe side of the line,
studying the structures through a battered spyglass. He's weathered,
mid-fifties, with the bearing of someone who's spent decades outdoors
and the eyes of someone who's spent those decades looking for
something specific.

This is **Torben**. Imperial cartographer, self-funded since the
empire stopped paying. He's been mapping the Grid perimeter for years.
Last week, he found a gap — a section where the defensive systems
don't respond. A maintenance fault, maybe, or a zone the Grid doesn't
consider worth protecting. He's been waiting for someone to walk
through it with him.

## The Approach

Torben's gap is real. A corridor between two structures where the
ground-level sensors have failed. The PC and Torben move through it
carefully. Torben narrates as they go, pointing out the things he's
cataloged from years of perimeter observation: patrol routes,
sensor ranges, the rhythm of the automated sweeps. He's professional,
thorough, and entirely wrong about what all of it means.

He calls the structures "fortifications." They're not. The first
building they enter through a ground-level opening is a warehouse —
shelving units, inventory markings, the desiccated remains of packing
material. Everything is labeled in the Grid script. The scale is
commercial, not military. This was a shipping depot.

Torben doesn't see it. He catalogs the shelving as "weapon racks"
and the inventory marks as "unit designations" in his notebook.

## The Interior

The city is eerily mundane once the PC adjusts to the alien
architecture. The buildings are functional spaces built for daily
life by people who had different technology but recognizable needs.

The PC and Torben explore several areas:

**A dining hall.** Long tables, bench seating, a kitchen area with
equipment the PC doesn't recognize but whose purpose is obvious —
heating elements, preparation surfaces, drainage. Torben calls it a
"mess hall" and isn't wrong, exactly, but the decoration on the walls
— faded patterns, what might be art — suggests something more
civilian. People ate here because they worked nearby, not because
they were garrisoned.

**A market or shop.** Display cases, empty now, with price markings
still visible. A counter. A back room with more shelving. The prices
are in a denomination system, not a rationing system. This was
commerce. Torben frowns at this but doesn't comment.

**A residential block.** Small, efficient living spaces. Personal
effects left behind: containers, what might be clothing folded in
drawers, decorative objects on shelves. Not barracks. Apartments.
The people who lived here had possessions, preferences, routines.
They left in an orderly fashion. Nothing is broken. Nothing suggests
evacuation. They packed what was worth packing and walked away.

Throughout the exploration, the PC encounters Grid security systems.
These are genuinely dangerous — autonomous mechanisms that scan for
unauthorized presence and respond with force. But their placement is
revealing. They're concentrated at the perimeter and at certain
internal junctions. They're not guarding military assets. They're
guarding infrastructure — the equivalent of locks on doors, fences
around equipment, "keep out" signs backed by lethal force. Property
security scaled up to a civilization that considered lethal force a
reasonable default.

Each encounter with a security mechanism requires an agility or
perception check to avoid or disable. Failure means damage.

## The Record Office

Deep in the city center, the PC and Torben find a building that
contains what can only be described as an administrative archive.
Filing systems. Record-keeping devices. Screens that still flicker
with data when Torben accidentally activates them by leaning on a
console.

The records are in Grid script, incomprehensible — except for one
section. A later addition, clearly appended to the existing files.
A translation project. Someone — something — in the city learned
the imperial language and used it.

The translated documents tell a simple story:

The city was a regional trade outpost. Population fluctuated
seasonally. Primary industries: resource processing, transport
logistics, commodity exchange. When the trade routes shifted and the
regional economy contracted, the parent civilization recalled its
personnel. Standard procedure. The infrastructure was left in place
because decommissioning costs exceeded the value of the salvage.
The security grid was left active on a default protocol because
turning it off required authorization from an office that no longer
existed.

The legion that attacked the city was, from the Grid's perspective,
a pest incursion. Wildlife tripping the perimeter sensors. The
defensive response was automatic, proportional by the Grid's
standards, and decisive. It was pest control.

## Torben's Discovery

Torben finds the critical document before the PC does. Or at the same
time. It doesn't matter — the moment he reads it, the air changes.

It's a letter. Composed in formal imperial language, clearly
translated from Grid administrative protocol. Addressed to the
commanding general of the imperial expeditionary force. It reads like
a form letter — because it is one. A template the Grid's systems
generated when they detected a sustained incursion by an organized
group, as opposed to random wildlife.

The letter apologizes for the "disruption" (the word is precisely
chosen). It explains that the facility is unmanned and the defensive
systems are operating on automated protocol. It requests that the
imperial forces withdraw to a safe distance and confirms that the
Grid's defensive perimeter will not pursue beyond its designated
boundary. It provides coordinates for the boundary. It expresses, in
polite bureaucratic language, the hope that "future interactions may
proceed with mutual awareness of operational parameters."

The Grid figured out who was attacking it, learned their language,
wrote them a letter, and asked them to stop. The empire's response
is not in the file. The legion's continued assault is.

Torben is very quiet.

## The Confrontation

Torben sets the letter down and speaks carefully. He tells the PC
what this means.

His father served in the second expedition. The one that was supposed
to avenge the first. Two thousand men walked toward this city and did
not come back. The empire declared them heroes who fell holding the
line against an existential threat. Monuments were built. Veterans'
families receive stipends. An entire military doctrine was built
around containing the Grid menace.

The letter says the Grid tried to tell them to leave. The letter says
the Grid had no interest in the empire whatsoever. The letter says
two thousand men died because they attacked a security fence and the
fence won.

Torben wants to destroy the letter. And the archive. And any evidence
that the city was a trade outpost and not a military installation. He
is not irrational. He is not a villain. He is a man whose entire life
has been organized around a story that turns out to be a
misunderstanding, and he can see what happens if that story breaks.

The veterans' stipends end. The military doctrine collapses. The
families who buried empty coffins and called their sons heroes learn
that their sons walked into a bug-zapper because nobody on their side
could read the warning sign. The empire's only unifying military
narrative — the one thing that justifies the expense, the garrisons,
the continued presence on the plains — evaporates.

He asks the PC, hand on his sword: what do you want to do?

## The Choice

### Destroy the evidence

The PC helps Torben smash the archive. The screens go dark. The
translated documents are burned. The letter is ash. The city returns
to being the enemy fortress the empire needs it to be.

Torben is relieved and diminished. He thanks the PC. He will publish
his cartographic survey of the city's exterior — the fortifications,
the weapon emplacements, the defensive grid — without mentioning what
he found inside. The empire's story holds. The dead stay heroes. The
Grid stays a monster. The war that never was continues to justify
everything built on top of it.

The PC leaves the city knowing the truth and unable to prove it.

### Refuse to destroy the evidence

The PC tells Torben the letter survives.

Torben draws his sword. This is not a bluff. He is a man who has
spent his life in service of a story, and the story is the only
thing holding together a military, a pension system, a national
identity, and his memory of his father. He will fight the PC to
protect it. He is not confident he'll win. He fights anyway.

If the PC wins, Torben yields. He is not a killer, just a man
breaking apart. He sits on the floor of the archive and asks the PC
what they intend to do with the letter. The PC can take it. Whether
anyone in the empire will believe it — a document in an alien city,
carried by a stranger — is another question.

If the PC loses, Torben destroys the archive and leaves the PC
injured but alive in the ruins. He is not cruel enough to kill
someone over a disagreement, even this one. He takes the letter
and burns it outside. The PC wakes in a dead city with the truth
in their memory and nothing to prove it.

### Persuade Torben to carry the truth himself

The hardest path. The PC argues that Torben is the only person who
can make the empire listen — a career cartographer with decades of
service, standing in the proof. The letter has weight because he
found it. It means something because of who he is. If the PC
carries it out, it's a rumor. If Torben carries it out, it's
testimony.

This requires a difficult persuasion check. Torben's resistance is
not stupidity — it's the reasonable fear that the truth will destroy
things that matter. The PC must acknowledge what's at stake (the
stipends, the doctrine, the families) and still argue that the
lie is worse.

Success: Torben takes the letter. He folds it carefully and puts it
in his map case. He tells the PC he doesn't know what he'll do with
it yet. He might publish. He might not. But he won't destroy it.
The truth exists, in the hands of the one person positioned to do
something with it, and neither the PC nor Torben knows what happens
next.

Failure: Torben shakes his head. He can't. He destroys the archive
himself, quietly, without violence toward the PC. He apologizes. He
means it.
