# Wrenbury (Plains Tier 2)

Miles of zigzag campaign trenches, now a mass grave and a salvage economy.
The empire advanced over the dead and retreated back over them. Scavengers
mine the earthworks for weapons, buckles, insignia. A merchant buys the
effects and sells them back to families in the heartland. Some of the
"identified" possessions are fabricated, because demand exceeds supply.

**Deeper concept:** Commerce fills every vacuum, including grief. The
merchant isn't a ghoul — he's performing a service the state refuses to.
But the service creates a market, and the market creates incentives, and
some families are mourning over a stranger's belt buckle and calling it
closure.

**Hook:** The PC finds the merchant doing brisk business at the trench
edge. A heartland woman is here trying to match her brother's description
to the dead. A drunk scavenger tells the PC that the buckle she just
bought came from a different body entirely.

**Clutch:** The merchant isn't defensive — he's tired. There's no way to
identify which remains are which. The buckle fits the description. The lie
lets the woman stop searching. The scavenger wants to lead her into the
unstable trenches to find the real body. The woman asks the PC what she
should do.

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The earthworks go on for miles. Zigzag trenches cut into the dry plain,
chest-deep in places, collapsed in others, the walls crumbling where rain
has softened the packed dirt. You can see where the line advanced — fresher
timber, deeper cuts — and where it fell back, the same ground churned
twice. Equipment lies half-buried in the trench walls: a canteen, a
splintered pike haft, leather straps bleached white by the sun.

At the trench edge, a canvas awning has been staked out over a folding
table. A merchant sits behind it, his goods laid out on cloth: buckles,
insignia, rings, dog-eared letters in faded ink. A small queue of people
waits in the heat. Most are scavengers selling. One woman stands apart,
holding a bundle of letters, studying the merchant's table.

## The Merchant's Trade

Examining the merchant's display, the items are organized with care. Each
piece has a tag: a unit number, a name, a brief physical description.
"Third Company, Harrogate — fair hair, tall, scar on left hand." The
handwriting is the merchant's own. Some tags are more detailed than others.

The merchant is patient with his customers. He buys without haggling,
pays fairly, and handles the effects with a kind of professional
gentleness. He is not a con artist. He is a man who has built a small,
necessary business in a place no one else will work.

## The Woman

The woman is Sera. She's been traveling for weeks with a bundle of her
grandfather's letters — the ones he sent home before the campaign collapsed.
She has his physical description, his unit assignment, and a sketch of a
family belt buckle he took with him when he shipped out.

She's already bought a buckle from the merchant. She holds it carefully,
wrapped in cloth, comparing it to the sketch. The match is good. Not
perfect, but good. She looks relieved and uncertain in equal measure.

## The Scavenger

A scavenger named Druce sits nearby, half-drunk on rough spirits. If the
PC talks to him, he's bitter and talkative. He's been working these
trenches for years. He knows every section, every collapse, every body
that's been pulled or left in place.

He tells the PC — quietly, or not quietly enough — that the buckle Sera
bought didn't come from Third Company's section. It came from a cavalry
officer's kit two trenches south. Wrong unit, wrong rank, wrong man. The
merchant matched the description, not the provenance.

Druce isn't doing this out of principle. He's doing it because the
merchant refused to buy a batch of corroded insignia he dug up last week,
and he's sore about it.

## Confronting the Merchant

If the PC brings the discrepancy to the merchant, he doesn't deny it. He
pulls the PC aside and speaks plainly.

There are four thousand dead in these trenches. No grave markers. No
registry that survived the retreat. The units were mixed together in the
final push and the bodies were left where they fell. He has matched
thirty-seven families to effects this season alone. Maybe a third of
those matches are accurate. Maybe less.

He asks the PC: what does accurate mean here? The buckle fits the
description. Her grandfather is in the trench. The buckle is from the trench.
She has something to take home and bury. She stops searching.

Because the alternative is what Druce is offering — leading her into the
earthworks. The section where Third Company fell is three hundred yards
in, past two partial collapses. Two scavengers died there this season
when a wall gave way. Even if she reaches the section, the bodies are
skeletal, commingled, stripped of anything identifying by years of weather
and prior scavengers. She will find bones. She will not find her grandfather.
She will find the place where her grandfather became indistinguishable from
everyone else who died there. And then she has to walk back out.

## Druce's Offer

If the PC talks to Druce about leading Sera into the trenches, he sobers
up slightly. He knows the way. He's worked that section. He can get her
to where Third Company's line was — the position is identifiable by the
trench architecture, the timber, the direction of the earthworks.

He's honest about the danger. The walls are bad. The floor is uneven and
you can't always tell where the ground is solid and where it's fill over
a collapsed lower section. He's seen people fall through.

He's also honest about what's there. Bones. Equipment fragments. Nothing
with a name on it. Nothing that says "this one was Sera's grandfather and
that one wasn't." She'll see where he died. She won't see him.

If pressed on why he's offering, he shrugs. Says everyone deserves to
know the truth about their dead. Then admits the merchant cut him off and
he'd like to see the man's business disrupted. Both things are true.

## Sera's Question

However the PC arrives at this point — through the merchant's confession
or Druce's accusation or both — Sera asks the PC directly what she should
do. She's not stupid. She can see the buckle might not be her grandfather's.
She can also see the trenches and what going in there means.

She says: her mother is dying. She promised to bring something back.
Something real. She doesn't know if "real" means "actually his" or
"from the place where he was." She doesn't know if there's a difference.
She's asking the PC because she's been traveling alone for weeks and she
can't think clearly anymore.

---

## The Choice

### Tell Sera to keep the buckle

The merchant is right. The buckle is from the trench. Her grandfather is in
the trench. The connection is as real as anything that can be recovered
from this place. She takes it home. Her mother has something to hold.
The merchant continues his work.

The PC knows the buckle belonged to someone else. The closure is built
on a fiction — but it's a fiction that ends the searching, and the
searching was going to kill her or break her.

### Help Druce lead Sera into the trenches

Druce guides them in. The walk is dangerous and grim. The trench walls
press close. They pass exposed remains — some half-buried, some scattered
by prior collapses. Druce points out where units were positioned by the
construction of the fortifications.

They reach Third Company's section. It's what the merchant said it would
be: bones and dirt and rusted metal, indistinguishable from every other
section. Sera kneels in the trench for a long time. She doesn't find her
grandfather. She finds the place.

She takes a handful of earth and a corroded button — not identified, not
identifiable, but from the right section. She is quiet on the walk out.
Something has changed in her face. It might be peace. It might be the
beginning of something worse.

If the PC fails a survival check during the walk, a wall section
collapses. The PC is injured and Druce pulls them clear. Sera is unhurt
but shaken.

### Convince Sera to go home with nothing

The hardest option. The PC tells Sera the truth: the buckle probably
isn't her grandfather's, the trenches will give her bones but not answers,
and neither option is what her mother asked for. Her grandfather is here,
in the ground, with four thousand others. That's what happened. There's
nothing to take home that isn't a lie or a guess.

Sera is quiet. She asks the PC to walk with her back to the road. At
the crossroads she thanks the PC — stiffly, not warmly — and turns
south toward the heartland. She leaves the buckle on the merchant's
table on her way out.

This requires a difficult persuasion check. If the PC fails, Sera
isn't convinced and insists on going into the trenches with or without
Druce. The PC can either accompany her (as above, but without a guide —
higher danger) or let her go alone (she does not return on screen; her
fate is unresolved).
