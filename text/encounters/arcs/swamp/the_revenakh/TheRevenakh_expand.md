# The Revënakh (Swamp Tier 3)

A palace of organic material extruded by the living earth, shaped to
resemble one of the towering, long-lost Revathi cities. The wet walls
ripple with an ever-changing mosaic — possibly a perverse imitation of
Revathi tiles, or worse, the tiles are a simplified expression of
whatever this is.

Someone the PC cared about, but lost, is here. The creature-palace
replicates the best patterns from the PC's memory. The dead live on
in the Revënakh as well, apparently.

**Deeper concept:** The Revënakh feels the longing of the Revathi and
the player. It has manifested this palace and the loved one in
response. It will manifest feasts and sumptuous rooms and musicians
and poets. It is only a mirror. These things only have whatever
meaning is projected by the viewer.

**Hook:** The PC finds the creature-palace while exploring the deep
swamp. A fleeting figure in a window. A powerful sense of recognition.
The building is alive.

**Clutch:** The PC has several outs: ride the wave, provoke violence,
or interrogate the experience into meaninglessness.

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The PC has been lost in the deep swamp for days. This is tier 3 —
the canopy is so dense that daylight is a rumor, the water is waist-
deep in places, and the bioluminescence is constant. Revënakh's
presence isn't subtle here. The ground moves. The trees rearrange.
Paths that existed an hour ago are gone.

Then the water clears. The trees pull back. And the PC sees it.

A building. Not a ruin — a building. Tall, multi-storied, with
balconies and archways and a facade covered in tilework that shifts
and ripples like something breathing. The architecture is Revathi —
or what Revathi architecture might have been at its height, before
the cities fell. Soaring proportions, geometric precision, colors
that don't exist in nature but feel right anyway.

A figure moves past a window on the second floor. The PC sees it for
only a moment — a silhouette, a posture, a way of turning the head
— but the recognition is immediate and visceral. The PC knows that
person. Knew them. Lost them.

The door is open. Warm light spills from inside. It smells like a
memory the PC hasn't thought about in years.

## The Palace

The interior is beautiful and wrong.

The proportions are generous — high ceilings, wide corridors, rooms
that feel designed for comfort rather than function. The walls are
alive: the tilework patterns shift continuously, cycling through
geometric variations that never quite repeat. The colors are warm.
The air is warm. The floors are smooth stone that gives slightly
underfoot, like packed earth, like something organic wearing the
shape of architecture.

The furnishings are drawn from memory — not the PC's memory alone,
but a collage. A table is set with food: dishes the PC recognizes
from childhood alongside dishes that belong to no cuisine the PC has
ever encountered. A shelf holds books in a script the PC can't read.
A window looks out on a view that isn't the swamp — a cityscape, a
harbor, a mountain range, shifting lazily between images like a
dreamer turning over.

The palace is Revënakh's most sophisticated construction. Every detail
is sourced from something real — recovered from the archive's
centuries of stored sensory data. Every detail is also slightly wrong,
because the organism that assembled them has perfect memory and zero
comprehension. It doesn't understand what a table is for. It doesn't
know why people eat. It has observed ten thousand meals and reproduced
the average, and the average of ten thousand meals is beautiful and
uncanny and serves nothing edible.

## The Figure

The person the PC lost is waiting in a room upstairs. Sitting in a
chair by a window, turning as the PC enters, smiling.

This is the hardest part of the dungeon to design because the figure
is specific to each PC's history. The implementation will need to
reference a mentor, a parent, or another significant figure from the
PC's backstory — someone who mattered and is gone.

What matters mechanically: the figure looks right. Sounds right. Has
the right mannerisms, the right expressions, the right way of filling
a pause. They greet the PC with warmth and familiarity. They ask how
the journey has been. They pour tea — or something like tea — and
sit across from the PC as if this is the most natural thing in the
world.

The seams don't show immediately. The conversation is good — better
than good. The figure says things the PC wants to hear. Not flattery,
but the kind of insight that only comes from someone who knows you
well. They ask the right questions. They listen the right way. They
remember things the PC remembers.

The first crack appears in the details. The figure references
something slightly wrong — a place that's a composite of two places,
a date that's close but not right, a person's name with the wrong
inflection. It's the kind of mistake a very good actor would make
when working from thorough but secondhand research. Or the kind of
mistake a perfect archive would make when assembling a person from
stored patterns without understanding what makes a person a person.

## The Evening

If the PC stays, the palace provides. There is food — it tastes like
something, recognizable flavors arranged in combinations that don't
quite make sense, satisfying in the way that a dream of eating is
satisfying. There are other figures — musicians who play instruments
that produce sounds somewhere between music and memory, poets who
recite fragments of verse in languages the PC almost understands.

The figure stays with the PC. They talk. The conversation ranges over
the PC's past, the things they've seen, the things they've lost. The
figure is a perfect listener — endlessly attentive, endlessly patient,
reflecting the PC's emotions back with precision and warmth.

It's the best evening the PC has had in months. Maybe years. The deep
swamp is outside, dark and hostile and disorienting, and in here is
warmth and light and someone who knows the PC's name.

The palace is showing the PC what it does — what Revënakh does — at
its most refined. It archives experience. It reconstructs from stored
patterns. It offers back what it has absorbed, filtered through no
understanding whatsoever. The evening is real in the way a reflection
is real: accurate, responsive, and empty.

## The Choice

### Ride the wave

The PC accepts the experience for what it is. They eat the food.
They listen to the music. They talk with the figure until the
conversation winds down into comfortable silence. They sleep in a
warm bed in a room that smells like somewhere they used to live.

The PC wakes in the swamp. The palace is gone — dissolved, or
rearranged, or simply no longer visible. The PC is lying on dry
ground on a hummock of moss, warm and rested. The deep swamp is
still around them, but they feel oriented for the first time in
days. A path is visible through the trees — not a Revathi path, but
a route that feels navigable.

The PC has gained nothing material and lost nothing. They carry the
memory of an evening with someone they'll never see again, someone
who was never really there, and the memory is warm. Whether that's
a gift or a trap depends on whether the PC can hold it lightly — an
experience, not an answer.

No mechanical cost or benefit, except: the PC is rested and oriented
in tier 3 for the first time. That has value.

### Pick a fight

The PC rejects the experience. Confronts the figure. Demands answers.
Breaks things.

The palace responds. Not with malice — Revënakh doesn't have malice —
but with the same indiscriminate archival fidelity it applies to
everything. The PC has changed the channel. The archive has centuries
of violence stored alongside everything else, and it mirrors what
it's shown.

The figure's face shifts. The warmth leaves. The palace's walls
ripple and the tilework patterns change — the warm geometries
replaced by something angular and aggressive, sourced from different
memories, different eras. The food on the table darkens and rots.
The music distorts.

The PC isn't fighting Revënakh — you can't fight a mirror. They're
fighting their own aggression reflected back at them through
centuries of stored violence. The palace manifests combat scenarios
drawn from its archive: composite soldiers, re-enacted battles,
the swamp's memory of every fight that ever happened in this region.

This is mechanically dangerous. The combat is a series of difficult
fight checks against opponents that escalate in intensity because
the archive has a lot of violence to draw from. The PC takes damage.
The palace takes no damage — it's organic, and the walls heal as
fast as the PC can break them.

The PC can fight their way out. It takes time, effort, and health.
They emerge from the palace into the swamp, disoriented, wounded,
and alone. The palace is behind them, still standing, still rippling.
It wasn't offended. It wasn't defending itself. It was doing what it
always does: showing the viewer what the viewer brings.

### Ask questions

The PC interrogates the figure. Not with hostility — with curiosity.
They sit across from this perfect reconstruction and start picking
at the seams. Where did you come from? What are you? What happens
when I leave?

The figure answers. Every answer is reasonable, warm, and slightly
circular. It says what the PC wants to hear, adjusted in real time
to reflect the PC's reactions. It's like arguing with a mirror — the
reflection is always convincing because it's always built from the
viewer's own material.

The PC can push harder. The figure never breaks character — because
there is no character to break. There's no moment where the mask
slips and the alien intelligence reveals itself. Revënakh isn't
hiding behind the figure. Revënakh is the process by which the
figure exists. There is no "real thing" underneath the performance.
The performance is the thing.

The conversation can go on for hours. The PC learns nothing they
didn't already know: the palace is a mirror, the figure is a
reconstruction, the experience is real and meaningless in equal
measure. The more questions the PC asks, the less the experience
offers, because interrogation is the one frame that the archive
can't reflect productively. You can't interview a mirror.

The PC leaves voluntarily, having spent a day in the swamp's most
sophisticated production asking it to explain itself. The palace
offered everything and the PC accepted nothing. They emerge rested
but unsatisfied, oriented but empty-handed, carrying the knowledge
that Revënakh's greatest achievement is a question with no answer:
is a perfect reflection of meaning the same as meaning?

It is not.
