# The Listening Blind (Swamp Tier 2)

One of the few structurally sound reception blinds in the complex — a
stone chamber aligned to help trained interpreters receive and sort
what Revënakh shows. A small group of elders has been using it for its
original purpose, and it's working: they've recovered fragments of
genealogies, songs, craft techniques. But one interpreter has gone
silent. She sits in the blind, eyes open, breathing steady, responding
to touch but not to speech. Three weeks now. The others say she's
receiving something large and needs time. They won't pull her out
because what she's receiving might be irreplaceable.

**Deeper concept:** The reception tradition works, and that is
precisely the danger. The line between receiving and being consumed is
a question of discipline, and discipline is finite. The silent
interpreter may be recovering something mourned for generations. Or
she may already be gone.

**Hook:** The interpreter's son finds the PC and begs for help. The
elders have turned him away — sacred work, must not be disturbed. He
thinks she's dying.

**Clutch:** She responds to touch. Her hand tightens around the PC's
wrist. Both interpretations — finishing or drowning — are equally
possible. The only way to know is to wait or to pull her out.

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The son finds the PC on the path, not at the blind. He's been
waiting for someone — anyone — who might listen.

**Teshen** is nineteen, lean, with the careful posture of someone
raised on stilt-platforms. He's been walking the paths around the
blind-complex for three days, sleeping rough, eating what he's
carrying. He's not panicked. He's past panic into something quieter
and worse — the steady hum of someone who has been afraid for too
long to feel the edges of it anymore.

His mother, **Avëth**, is an interpreter. One of four who've been
working the blind this season. Three weeks ago she stopped
responding. She sits in the reception seat, eyes open, breathing
normally, and doesn't speak, doesn't eat unless food is placed in
her mouth, doesn't acknowledge anyone's presence. She responds to
touch — she'll grip a hand, shift her weight if guided — but
nothing else.

The elders say she's receiving. Teshen says she's dying. He went to
the blind and they turned him away. Sacred work. The reception must
not be interrupted. He's not allowed inside.

He asks the PC to help him get to his mother.

## The Path to the Blind

The blind is a half-hour walk into the complex, along a raised
stone path through standing water. The path is old and well-
maintained — one of the few in the complex that's been kept clear.
The Revathi consider the blind sacred. The bioluminescence is active
along the route, pulsing in slow waves that move toward the blind
like a current.

Teshen talks as they walk. His mother has been an interpreter for
twenty years. She's good at it — disciplined, careful, trained since
childhood in the reception techniques. She goes into the blind for
sessions that last hours or days, receives what Revënakh shows her,
and comes out to transcribe. She's recovered genealogies that
reconnected families separated for generations. She found a complete
weaving pattern that allowed the villages to restart a textile
tradition. Her work is important and she's always come back from it.

Three weeks is different. Three weeks is longer than any session
Teshen has ever seen. And when he looked at her — the last time the
elders let him near, five days ago — something was wrong. Not in her
body. In her stillness. She was too still. Not the focused stillness
of reception, but the stillness of something that has stopped trying
to move.

## The Blind

The blind is a stone chamber, roughly circular, with walls that curve
inward to a partially open ceiling. The acoustics are strange —
sounds from outside are muffled, while sounds inside seem to sustain
and layer. The floor is polished stone, slightly concave, with a
raised seat at the center carved from the same rock. The seat faces
a section of wall where the stone is darker, wetter, and faintly
luminous.

Three elders are present:

**Khavë** is the senior interpreter, a woman in her seventies who
has been using the blind for forty years. She's the one who trained
Avëth. She's calm, authoritative, and unshakeable in her conviction
that Avëth is receiving something important. She has seen long
sessions before — not this long, but long enough. The tradition has
protocols for this. You wait. You tend the body. You trust the
discipline.

**Oresh** is younger, perhaps fifty, a skilled interpreter in his
own right. He's less certain than Khavë but unwilling to contradict
her openly. If the PC speaks to him alone, he admits he's worried.
Three weeks exceeds anything in the oral record. But he also says:
the reception is active. He can feel it. The blind is resonating in
a way it doesn't during normal sessions. Whatever Avëth is receiving,
it's substantial.

**Lîshu** is the third interpreter, a quiet woman who has stopped
participating in sessions since Avëth went silent. She won't say why
directly, but the PC can infer: she's afraid. If it happened to
Avëth, it could happen to any of them. She's the most sympathetic to
Teshen but the least willing to act on it.

## Avëth

Avëth sits in the reception seat. She is physically present and
apparently healthy — breathing steadily, skin warm, pulse normal.
Her eyes are open and unfocused. She doesn't track movement. She
doesn't respond to her name, to Teshen's voice, to direct questions.

She responds to touch. When the PC takes her hand, her fingers close
— not a reflex, but a grip. Deliberate. Strong. When the PC tries
to withdraw, she holds on. Her breathing changes — slightly faster,
slightly shallower. Something is happening behind those open eyes.

Khavë interprets this as confirmation: Avëth is aware and engaged.
The grip is communication. She's saying "I'm here, I'm working,
don't interrupt." The change in breathing is effort — she's
processing something large and the contact is a distraction she's
choosing to accept.

Teshen interprets it differently: his mother is holding on because
she can't let go. The grip isn't communication — it's a reflex, the
body's last attempt to anchor itself to something real. The breathing
change is distress. She's been under for three weeks and she's
drowning.

## The Thîkërevas

If the PC asks for a medical opinion, the elders send for a
**thîkërevas** — a Revathi healer who combines herbal medicine with
reception-trained observation. **Mevëth** arrives within the hour.
She's practical, unsentimental, and honest.

Her examination is thorough. She checks Avëth's vitals, her muscle
tone (deteriorating — three weeks of immobility), her responsiveness
to stimuli. She tries speaking to her in old Revathikh fragments.
No response. She touches specific points — wrist, temple, the base
of the skull — that the reception tradition associates with the
interpretive state.

Her conclusion: Avëth is alive and stable. She is not in immediate
danger of death, though three more weeks of immobility will cause
serious physical damage — muscle wasting, joint contracture,
dehydration complications. The reception state is genuine — Mevëth
can observe the physiological markers. Whether Avëth is receiving
productively or trapped in a loop, Mevëth cannot determine. Both
present identically from the outside.

She tells the PC, plainly: pulling Avëth out will either save her
or destroy what she's receiving. Leaving her will either complete
the reception or kill her. There is no diagnostic that distinguishes
the two cases. The only variable is time, and time is working against
the body regardless of what the mind is doing.

## The Choice

### Pull her out

The PC and Teshen extract Avëth from the reception seat. This is
not violent but it is physical — she doesn't resist, but she doesn't
help. Her body is stiff from three weeks of immobility. Teshen
carries her.

Khavë does not interfere, but she watches with an expression that
the PC will remember. She believes they've just destroyed something
irreplaceable. She may be right.

Avëth comes back slowly. Over the course of hours, her eyes begin
to focus. She recognizes Teshen. She grips his hand — the same
grip, but now with recognition behind it. She speaks his name.
She's confused, disoriented, and physically weak. She doesn't
remember what she was receiving. Fragments — sounds, colors,
something that felt like a song but she can't reproduce it. The
content of three weeks of reception is gone, or so degraded by the
interruption that it might as well be.

Teshen has his mother. The elders have lost whatever Avëth was
holding. Oresh asks her, gently, over the following days, what she
remembers. She can't articulate it. She says it felt important. She
says it felt like a whole thing — not fragments but a complete
transmission, something Revënakh had never offered before. She cries
when she says this, not from loss but from the frustration of knowing
she held something she can no longer describe.

The blind resumes normal sessions. Khavë does not speak to the PC
again. Lîshu quietly resumes interpreting, relieved and ashamed of
her relief.

### Wait

The PC supports the elders' decision. Teshen is not removed from
the blind — the PC negotiates that much — but Avëth stays in the
seat.

The waiting is terrible. Teshen sits beside his mother, holding her
hand, talking to her in a low voice. She doesn't respond. Her grip
is constant. Her breathing doesn't change. Mevëth visits daily and
reports the same thing: stable, deteriorating, no way to know.

After four more days — twenty-five total — Avëth's breathing
changes. It deepens. Her eyes close for the first time in weeks.
The bioluminescence in the blind brightens sharply, then dims.
Khavë leans forward.

Avëth opens her eyes and looks at Teshen. She says his name. She
says: "I have it."

What she has takes days to transcribe and months to fully process.
It's a genealogy — not a fragment but a complete lineage record for
one of the lost clans, stretching back centuries before the cities
fell. Names, marriages, children, migrations. The connective tissue
of a family that the Revathi have mourned as entirely lost. It's
the single most significant recovery the reception tradition has
ever produced.

Avëth is physically damaged. Her legs don't work properly for weeks.
Her hands shake. She sleeps fourteen hours a day. Mevëth says some
of the damage may be permanent. Teshen doesn't leave her side.

Khavë transcribes the genealogy with trembling hands. She was right.
The cost was real and the yield was real and neither cancels the
other. Teshen has his mother back, diminished. The Revathi have a
piece of themselves they never expected to recover. Whether the
trade was worth it is a question that depends entirely on who you
ask.

### Sit with her

The PC doesn't decide. The PC sits in the blind beside Avëth, takes
her hand, and waits — not as an endorsement of the elders' position
or the son's, but as a presence.

This isn't a resolution. It's a refusal to force one. The PC stays
for a day, or two, holding Avëth's hand when Teshen needs to sleep,
listening to Khavë's stories about past receptions, watching Lîshu
avoid the seat she's afraid to sit in again.

The outcome is not determined by the PC. It unfolds on its own
timeline. Avëth either surfaces or she doesn't. The PC's presence
doesn't change the odds. What it changes is Teshen — who, for the
first time in three weeks, doesn't have to keep watch alone.

The PC leaves before the reception ends. They don't learn the
outcome. What they carry out of the blind is simpler than a
decision: the knowledge that sometimes the most honest response to
an impossible situation is to sit with someone in it and not pretend
you know the answer.
