# The Ledgerhaus (Mountains Tier 2)

A scholar on the upper slopes has dedicated his life to documenting
every injustice visited upon the miners. Decades of research,
meticulously organized — foremen cheating miners, unsafe shafts,
forged inspection reports. He's preparing a case he's certain will
upend the mining mercantile class. He just needs a few more years
to make it ironclad before traveling to the Stift to argue it. A
life's work in the shadows.

A miner's widow has climbed the mountain looking for closure on her
husband's death. She's followed a spider web of rumors about the
shadowy scholar who documents the suffering of the miners but does
nothing.

**Deeper concept:** Documentation is not the same as action, and
understanding is not the same as remedy. The world's most complete
record of suffering has helped nobody, because it was built to
comprehend injustice in the abstract, not to help the woman standing
in the doorway.

**Hook:** The PC finds the widow forlorn outside the Ledgerhaus. She's
been told to leave by the acolytes. She insists she'll remain until
she's allowed to speak to the scholar. The acolytes insist this is
hearsay and the woman is deranged — but privately, they know exactly
what's going on and are struggling with it.

**Clutch:** The scholar offers a verbal confirmation that her husband's
death was preventable. No paper leaves the building. The widow says
words are worthless — the same superintendent runs the mine, and her
son works there now. The PC decides: support the compromise, slip her
the documents, or confront the scholar publicly.

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The path up the upper slope narrows to a track cut into the rock face.
The Ledgerhaus sits at the end of it — a stone building wedged into a
shelf in the mountainside, unremarkable from below, invisible from the
valleys. No sign. No banner. The kind of place you only find if someone
tells you where to look, and the kind of people who know don't talk
about it freely.

A woman sits on a rock outside the door. She's been here for at least a
day — her pack is open, her food laid out in careful portions, her
bedroll unfurled against the wall. She's not leaving. She's not making a
scene. She's just sitting, watching the door, waiting with the
particular patience of someone who has traveled a very long way and is
not going back empty-handed.

Two young scholars stand inside the doorway, visibly uncomfortable.
They've barred the entrance but haven't had the nerve to physically
remove her.

## The Widow

**Emra** is a miner's wife from the Tarnach valley — the valley with
the worst safety record in the range. Her husband Pol died in a shaft
collapse eighteen months ago. The company inspector ruled it an act of
providence. The foreman expressed regret. The shaft reopened three
weeks later. Her son Daven, sixteen, works the same mine.

She didn't come here for grief. She came because a retired miner told
her, over drinks at a wake, that somewhere on the upper slopes there's
a man who has the real records. The ones that show which collapses were
preventable, which inspections were fabricated, which foremen knew and
signed off anyway. She followed the rumor through six contacts over
four months, each one more reluctant to talk than the last, until she
got a location.

She tells the PC all of this plainly. She doesn't need the PC to feel
sorry for her. She needs someone to knock on the door and be taken
seriously, because she's a miner's wife and the scholars don't take
miners' wives seriously.

## The Acolytes

The two young scholars — **Brin** and **Maret** — are research
assistants. They are mortified. They've been told to turn Emra away,
and they've done it, and they're ashamed.

If the PC speaks to them separately from Emra, the truth comes out
quickly. They know exactly what the Ledgerhaus contains. They've spent
years helping organize it. The archive documents every mining death in
the range for the past forty years, cross-referenced with inspection
records, company filings, foreman assignments, and geological surveys.
It is the most complete record of industrial negligence ever assembled.
Pol's death is in there. Of course it is.

They're torn. Brin is the more idealistic — he thinks the whole point
of the archive is to help people like Emra, and turning her away
contradicts everything they're supposed to believe in. Maret is more
cautious — she understands the master's reasoning, even if she doesn't
like it. The archive is a weapon being forged. Drawing it too early
dulls the blade.

If the PC pushes, they'll admit the master to the Ledgerhaus. They
won't hand over documents on their own — but they won't stop the PC
from making the case directly.

## The Scholar

**Aldren** is not what the rumors suggest. He's not a recluse or a
zealot. He's a tired man in his sixties with meticulous handwriting
and a back that hurts from decades of bending over documents. His
study is a controlled disaster of paper — stacked, indexed, color-
coded, cross-referenced. The system makes sense only to him, but it
works.

He invites the PC in with a sigh. He's been expecting this. Not Emra
specifically, but someone like her. He's surprised it took this long.

He explains his work without defensiveness. Forty years of
documentation. Every mining death, every forged inspection, every
chain of culpability traced from the pit face to the company board to
the guild council. He has names, dates, documents, witness
statements. He has the complete case against the Tarnach Mining
Company and six others. When he presents it to the Stift, it will be
unanswerable.

When. Not if. When.

He estimates three more years. Perhaps four. There are gaps in the
evidence chain for the earliest cases. The oldest witnesses are dying.
He needs their testimony formalized before he can proceed. The case
must be complete. If he brings an incomplete case to the Stift and
loses, the precedent makes it harder to bring a second one. He gets
one shot.

He knows about Pol. He has the file. The shaft was flagged as
structurally compromised in an internal geological survey the company
never disclosed. The foreman who signed the inspection report knew.
The superintendent who approved the continued operation knew. The
documentation is precise and damning.

He will not show it to Emra.

## The Argument

Aldren's reasoning is sincere and maddening. If he releases
individual records, it sets a precedent. Every widow, every orphan,
every maimed miner who hears about the Ledgerhaus will come here
looking for their file. Each individual release risks exposure —
the mining companies don't know the archive exists, and if they learn
of it, they will destroy it. They have the money and the connections.
The archive survives because it's secret.

One file for Emra means the secret starts to leak. And even if it
doesn't — even if she's discreet — the file alone won't change
anything. She can wave it at the Tarnach foreman and he'll call it a
forgery. She has no institutional backing. One woman with one
document against a mining company is a nuisance. The complete archive,
presented to the Stift with forty years of evidence, is a revolution.

He asks the PC: which matters more? Emra's closure, or the case that
could save every miner in the range?

Emra, if she hears this — or when the PC relays it — has one answer:
her son goes into that shaft tomorrow. The case might save miners in
three years. The file might save her son this month. She doesn't care
about the revolution. She cares about Daven.

## The Choice

### Support the scholar's compromise

Aldren speaks to Emra. He confirms, verbally, that Pol's death was
preventable and documented as such. He names the foreman. He names the
superintendent. He tells her the shaft was known to be unsafe. He says
all of this with the door closed and no witnesses other than the PC.

Emra has the truth. She does not have proof. She can tell Daven not
to go back into the mine, and Daven will ask why, and she'll say "a
scholar on the mountain told me," and Daven will say "that's not a
reason to lose our income."

Aldren continues his work. The case proceeds. Emra walks back down
the mountain with words and no paper. The archive remains secret.
Three years from now, maybe four, Aldren brings the completed case
to the Stift. Maybe it changes everything. Maybe Daven is still alive
to see it.

### Slip Emra the documents

Brin has already copied the relevant pages. He's been waiting for
someone to make the decision he can't make himself. If the PC signals
agreement, Brin passes the copies to Emra when Aldren isn't watching.

Emra has the file. Names, dates, the geological survey, the foreman's
signature. Enough to take to the guild council in the valley. Enough
to pull Daven from the mine with proof his father was killed by
negligence, not providence. Enough to make noise.

Making noise is the problem. The file traces back to information only
the Ledgerhaus could have compiled. The mining companies will
investigate. The archive's secrecy — the thing that has kept it safe
for forty years — cracks. Aldren's case isn't destroyed, but it's
compromised. The companies now know someone is watching. They'll
start covering their tracks. The revolution gets harder, maybe
impossible.

Aldren will discover the breach. He won't blame Emra — he'll blame
himself for letting the PC through the door. The acolytes fracture:
Brin leaves, relieved and guilty. Maret stays, furious.

One son might be saved. The case that could save all of them takes
a wound it may not survive.

### Confront Aldren publicly

The PC forces the question into the open. In front of the acolytes,
in front of Emra: the archive exists, Pol's file is in it, and the
choice to withhold it is a choice with consequences.

Aldren doesn't crumble. He argues. The archive's purpose is
structural change, not individual remedy. Every exception weakens
the whole. He's not wrong. But saying it out loud, to Emra's face,
with Brin and Maret watching, transforms an institutional policy
into a personal cruelty. The abstraction collapses.

The acolytes have to choose. This is the institutional crisis — not
the archive's integrity, but its soul. Is the Ledgerhaus a weapon
to be wielded at the right moment, or a record that belongs to the
people it documents?

Brin sides with Emra. Maret sides with Aldren. The Ledgerhaus splits.
If the PC backs Emra, Aldren releases the file — not willingly, but
because the alternative is a public rupture that exposes the archive
anyway. The file goes to Emra. The archive's secrecy is preserved —
barely — but the precedent is set, and the next widow who climbs the
mountain will be harder to turn away.

If the PC backs Aldren, Emra leaves empty-handed, but the
confrontation has changed something. Aldren is shaken. He may
accelerate his timeline. The case might come to the Stift sooner,
rougher, less perfect. Whether that helps or hurts is unknowable.
