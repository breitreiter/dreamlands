# The Stift (Mountains Tier 3)

The Stift is a magical Hague — a court of final appeal whose judgments
are metaphysically binding. The magistrates are cautious, aware that
mortals work hard to trick them into favorable rulings. This makes the
system slow and the rulings complex and tepid.

The PC comes as a curious tourist and gets wrapped up in a plot to
destroy the Stift.

**Deeper concept:** The Stift isn't corrupt and it isn't broken. It's
working exactly as designed, and the design includes no mechanism for
acknowledging its own failure. Radical change can come from outside,
but radical change is inherently destructive. Exploding the Stift will
end the injustice, but will not, in itself, create justice.

**Hook:** The PC does fun tourist things — cafes, libraries, views from
the mountaintop. They're recruited into a conspiracy, possibly by
accident, subterfuge, or direct pitch.

**Clutch:** If the PC goes along, they succeed or are captured. If they
inform the guard, they're recruited into counter-espionage leading to
a confrontation at the conspirators' hideout.

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The Stift is the most beautiful place the PC has ever been. The
buildings are white stone and dark timber, set into the peak like
they grew there. The air is thin and clean. Libraries with tall
windows look out over a hundred miles of mountain range. Cafes serve
hot drinks on terraces above the clouds. Scholars and petitioners and
functionaries walk cobbled streets at a pace that suggests no one has
ever been in a hurry here.

It's easy to forget what the place is for. The courtrooms are quiet.
The proceedings are measured. The magistrates — seven of them, robed,
unhurried — speak in careful sentences that account for every possible
interpretation. Judgment here is permanent and metaphysically
binding. A ruling of the Stift cannot be appealed, reversed, or
ignored. The words become truth in a way that goes beyond legal
authority. Reality reshapes itself, gently, to accommodate the
court's decision.

This power is why the magistrates are so careful. And their care is
why the dockets stretch for decades.

## The Tourist

The PC arrives with no agenda and no appointment. The Stift is open
to visitors — it's a point of pride, part of the institution's claim
to transparency. The PC can spend time exploring.

**The libraries.** Extraordinary. Centuries of case law, philosophical
treatises, mathematical proofs. The PC can browse freely. The
librarians are helpful and proud of the collection.

**The cafes.** Good coffee, mountain pastries, conversation with
travelers and petitioners. The atmosphere is collegiate — people
debating fine points of jurisprudence over breakfast, not because
they're pretentious but because this is where the conversations
happen.

**The view.** From the Stift's highest terrace, the PC can see the
mining valleys, the scholar institutions on the upper slopes, the
trade roads, the distant plains. The entire world the mountains
contain, laid out in miniature. It's breathtaking and clarifying.
From up here, the suffering in the valleys is invisible.

**The waiting petitioners.** In a long hall near the main courthouse,
people sit on benches. Some have been here for weeks. Some for
months. A few, the regulars say, have been here for years. Their
cases are in the docket. Their turn will come. The system is fair.
The system is very, very slow.

## The Recruit

How the PC gets pulled in depends on circumstance, but the shape is
the same: someone asks the PC to carry a package across the Stift.

The most natural version: a friendly scholar the PC meets in a cafe —
**Aldis** — mentions that she needs a sealed case of documents
delivered to a colleague on the far side of the compound. She's
expected in a hearing and can't do it herself. The PC is a Trader's
Guild member; carrying things is practically professional.

The package is heavy. Sealed. Aldis is specific about the route: not
through the main courtyard, but around the back, through the service
corridors. She says it's because the courtyard is crowded during
session. It's because the courtyard has guards.

If the PC carries the package without opening it, they deliver it to
a ground-floor room in a residential building on the Stift's western
edge. The door is opened by a man named **Ferren**, who thanks the
PC, takes the package, and invites them in for tea.

If the PC opens the package, they find blasting powder. A lot of it.

## The Conspirators

Ferren's apartment is a meeting room. Three people are present:
Ferren, Aldis (who arrives shortly after), and a third — **Kael**,
a former petitioner who has been waiting for a hearing for seven
years.

They are not wild-eyed radicals. They are exhausted, principled
people who have tried everything else.

**Ferren** is a structural engineer who was hired to maintain the
Stift's buildings. He knows the architecture intimately — load-
bearing walls, foundation points, the specific column in the high
court chamber whose failure would bring down the roof. He's not
angry. He's clinical. He maps the building's vulnerabilities the
way he'd map a renovation project.

**Aldis** is a scholar of jurisprudence who spent fifteen years
studying the Stift's legal framework, looking for a mechanism to
reform the docket system from within. She found nothing. The system
is self-sealing — every rule that could be changed requires a ruling
to change it, and every ruling requires a hearing, and every hearing
requires a place in the docket. The Stift cannot reform itself
because reform would require the very speed the system lacks.

**Kael** is the emotional center. His village's water rights case
has been in the docket for seven years. In that time, the mine
company that diverted the water has expanded operations twice. His
daughter is sick. He came to the Stift believing in justice. He still
believes in justice. He's just stopped believing the Stift dispenses
it.

Their plan: place blasting charges at Ferren's identified structural
points in the high court chamber. Detonate them during a night
session when the chamber is empty. Destroy the court. Not the
magistrates — the building, the records, the physical institution.
Force the system to rebuild from scratch, with the chance to design
something that actually works.

They know this is monstrous. They've talked about it for months.
Aldis has written a legal analysis of why it's necessary — it reads
like a court brief, because she can't stop being a scholar even when
planning to blow up a courthouse. Kael has written letters to his
family. Ferren has calculated the blast radius to the inch.

They tell the PC everything because they need one more person to
place charges at a point Ferren can't reach during his normal
maintenance rounds. And because, Aldis admits, they want someone
from outside the mountain to understand why.

## The Weight

The PC has time to sit with the conspirators and interrogate the
plan. Key pressure points:

**The records.** The Stift's case archive is in the building. Centuries
of rulings, precedents, petitions. If the building falls, the archive
is lost. Aldis has thought about this. She says the archive is the
chain. Every precedent in it is a reason the court moves slower. Burn
the precedents and the new court starts clean. Ferren says he can't
guarantee the archive survives even a targeted demolition.

**The magistrates.** They're not in the building at night. Probably.
Ferren says the night sessions are administrative — clerks, not
judges. But magistrates sometimes work late. "Sometimes" is doing a
lot of work in that sentence. Kael says he doesn't want anyone to die.
Ferren says he can't promise that.

**The consequence.** If the Stift falls, its metaphysical authority
doesn't transfer. There is no backup court, no secondary institution,
no mechanism for continuity. Every pending case is annihilated. Every
petitioner who climbed the mountain — the ones who've been waiting
months, years, decades — loses everything. Not just Kael's case. All
of them.

And the new system that replaces it? Aldis is honest: she doesn't
know what it would look like. She knows what's wrong with the current
one. She doesn't have a blueprint for something better. The
destruction is the plan. What comes after is hope.

## The Choice

### Help the conspirators

The PC places the charges. The demolition is set for midnight. The
PC and the conspirators retreat to a safe distance.

A fight check or agility check determines whether the PC is spotted
by a night patrol while placing the final charge. If spotted, the PC
must subdue or evade the guard — a clerk, not a soldier, frightened
and out of their depth.

The detonation is precise. Ferren's engineering is good. The high
court chamber collapses inward. The roof falls. Dust and stone
cascade down the mountainside. The Stift's central building — the
seat of metaphysical justice for centuries — is rubble.

The PC watches from the terrace where they admired the view that
morning. The petitioners' hall is intact. The libraries are intact.
The cafes are intact. The court is gone.

In the morning, the petitioners emerge and stand in the courtyard,
looking at the ruin. Some weep. Some are silent. One old man, who
has been waiting eleven years, sits down on a bench and doesn't move.
His case is gone. Not denied — erased. As if it never existed.

Kael stands among them and has nothing to say. The injustice he came
to fight is still there. The institution that failed to address it
is gone. The space between those two facts is where he'll live now.

### Inform the guard

The PC goes to the Stift's constabulary — a small, mostly ceremonial
force that handles pickpockets and drunks. They are not equipped for
this.

The captain, **Renate**, is a serious woman who takes the report
seriously. She doesn't panic. She asks the PC to help — they need
someone the conspirators trust to lead the guard to the hideout.

The PC returns to Ferren's apartment with the guard positioned
outside. The approach requires a deception check — the conspirators
are cautious. Success means the door opens and the guard enters.
Failure means Kael spots something wrong and the conspirators
scatter; the guard catches Ferren but Aldis and Kael escape into
the Stift's residential quarters.

If the guard takes the apartment: the arrest is quiet. Ferren
surrenders immediately. Aldis argues — not resisting, but making her
case to the arresting officers as if they're a court. Kael fights.
Not well — he's not a fighter — but desperately. A scuffle. Someone
gets hurt. Kael is restrained. He looks at the PC and doesn't say
anything, which is worse than if he did.

The blasting powder is confiscated. The Stift is saved. The system
continues. Kael's water rights case remains in the docket, now
accompanied by a criminal case that will take precedence. His
daughter is still sick. The mine company is still operating.

The magistrates issue a formal statement thanking the constabulary
for their diligence. They do not address the conditions that produced
the conspiracy. They announce no reforms. The docket advances one
case. It is not Kael's.

### Walk away

The PC leaves the Stift. Doesn't help. Doesn't inform. Just goes.

This isn't a non-choice — it's the recognition that neither outcome
is the PC's to decide. The conspirators will act or they won't. The
Stift will stand or it won't. The PC is a traveler who stopped for
coffee and saw something impossible: a place where justice is real
and binding and beautiful and completely, structurally incapable of
arriving in time.

The PC descends the mountain. Behind them, the Stift gleams in the
afternoon light. Whether it's still there tomorrow is someone else's
question.
