# The Zahlenhaus (Mountains Tier 2)

One of the great scholarly houses, focused on mathematics and
statistics. The headmaster has retired to complete his masterwork,
and there's a lively succession debate between three candidates:
- A firebrand who wants the haus to build political theories from
  materialist market analysis
- A former Trade Guild member who thinks the haus should identify
  market inefficiencies to enrich everyone
- An old-fashioned theorist who resents the politicization and wants
  the haus to focus purely on theory

**Deeper concept:** Illustrates the three competing identities for
the scholarly class: revolutionary instrument, practical partner to
commerce, or ivory tower.

**Hook:** The PC is seeking warmth and shelter. As a member of the
Trader's Guild, the PC is immediately feted by the former guild
member. Other scholars notice and the PC becomes enmeshed in
electoral drama.

**Clutch:** The PC's support decides the election and the fate of
the haus.

# Scene Sketch

## Opening

The Zahlenhaus is unmistakable — a broad stone hall perched on a
ledge with windows cut wide for light, every sill stacked with
books. Smoke from three chimneys. The sound of argument carries
through the door before the PC opens it.

Inside: a common room dominated by a massive blackboard covered in
equations, half-erased and rewritten in three different hands. Long
tables are buried under papers, slates, instruments of measurement.
A fire is burning and the room is warm. A dozen scholars in various
states of agitation are clustered in groups of two and three, talking
over each other.

The PC has walked into an election season. The atmosphere is somewhere
between a faculty meeting and a blood feud.

## The Situation

The headmaster, **Aldric**, is eighty and has formally stepped down
to finish his final treatise — a unified theory of market cycles
that he's been working on for thirty years. He's not dead, just done.
He refuses to endorse a successor, saying the haus must choose for
itself. This is either principled or cowardly, depending on who the
PC asks.

Three candidates have emerged. Each represents a fundamentally
different answer to the question: what are scholars for?

The election is decided by a vote of the full haus — twelve scholars
plus any visiting fellows. The PC, as a credentialed member of the
Trader's Guild, technically qualifies as a visiting fellow. This was
an obscure procedural detail until the former guild member pointed it
out, loudly, in the common room this morning.

## The Candidates

### Voss (The Firebrand)

**Voss** is young, intense, and furious. She grew up in a mining
village and climbed the mountain on scholarship. She's brilliant — her
work on labor extraction ratios is cited across every haus in the
range — and she has no patience for scholarship that doesn't serve
the people who suffer.

Her platform: the Zahlenhaus should use its mathematical expertise
to build rigorous, evidence-based cases against the mining companies
and present them to the Stift. Statistical proof of systematic
exploitation. The numbers that show, beyond argument, that the
current system kills miners at a predictable rate for a calculable
profit. She wants the haus to be a weapon.

She's magnetic and frightening. The junior scholars adore her. The
senior scholars are wary. If the PC talks to her, she's blunt: the
haus has spent decades proving theorems that no one reads while people
die in shafts a day's walk below. She wants to burn the ivory tower
and build a courthouse.

She doesn't approach the PC directly — she considers a Trader's
Guild member to be part of the problem. But she's not stupid. If the
PC seeks her out, she'll make her case without flattery. She doesn't
need the PC to like her. She needs the PC to look at the numbers.

### Dael (The Pragmatist)

**Dael** is the former Trader's Guild member — mid-fifties, well-
dressed by mountain standards, with the easy confidence of someone
who's made money and chosen to stop. He left the guild ten years ago
to pursue the mathematics he'd always loved, and his work on pricing
theory is genuinely good.

His platform: the Zahlenhaus should partner with the Trade Guild to
identify market inefficiencies. Better pricing models, fairer
exchange rates, optimized trade routes. Mathematics in service of
commerce, which is mathematics in service of prosperity, which lifts
everyone. He points to specific examples: his pricing model for tin
futures saved three mining villages from a speculative crash two
years ago. Real people, real money, real help.

He's the one who fetes the PC on arrival — drinks, a warm seat by the
fire, genuine interest in the PC's travels. He's charming without
being oily. He believes what he's saying. He also knows that a
Trader's Guild vote in his column changes the math of the election,
and he's not above hospitality as politics.

If the PC presses him on whether enriching the guild enriches the
miners, he's honest: not directly. The benefits trickle. But they
trickle reliably, and reliably is more than the miners have ever
gotten from revolutionary politics.

### Hesse (The Theorist)

**Hesse** is old, dry, and uninterested in making his case attractive.
He's been at the Zahlenhaus for forty years. He was Aldric's first
student. His work on prime distribution is respected across the
continent, by the eleven people who understand it.

His platform: the Zahlenhaus should do mathematics. Not politics, not
commerce, not advocacy. Mathematics. The theorems they prove today
will matter in ways no one can predict. Voss wants to weaponize the
haus for a fight that will be over in a decade. Dael wants to yoke
it to a guild that will extract its value and discard it. Both
positions subordinate knowledge to power. The haus exists to know
things. That's enough.

He doesn't campaign. He doesn't seek the PC out. If the PC finds him,
he's in his study, working. He's polite but unpersuasive — not because
he lacks arguments, but because he genuinely doesn't believe the
election should be decided by persuasion. The best candidate is the one
who does the best work. He assumes this is self-evident.

He's also the only candidate who mentions the headmaster's unfinished
treatise with real understanding. Aldric's work on market cycles isn't
advocacy or commerce — it's pattern recognition at a scale that
transcends both. Hesse is the only person in the haus who sees what
Aldric was actually trying to do, and the only one who might finish it.

## The Common Room

The election plays out in the common room over the course of the
evening. Scholars argue at tables, on the blackboard, over drinks.
The PC can move between groups, listen, ask questions, and be lobbied.

Each candidate has supporters who make their case in different ways:

Voss's supporters show the PC their work — statistical breakdowns of
mining deaths, injury rates, profit margins. The numbers are
devastating and irrefutable. They ask the PC: what is mathematics
for, if not this?

Dael's supporters pour the PC another drink and talk about practical
outcomes. The pricing model that prevented the crash. The trade route
optimization that cut transport costs for mountain villages by a
third. They ask the PC: what good is a theorem that no one applies?

Hesse's supporters are mostly absent — working in their studies. The
one the PC can find, a quiet woman named Lise, says simply: Voss will
burn the haus out in ten years. Dael will sell it in five. Hesse will
keep it alive long enough for the next generation to decide what it's
for. She's not passionate about this. She's just doing the math.

## The Choice

The PC votes. The haus is split — four votes for each candidate among
the residents. The PC's vote, plus a handful of swing scholars who
follow the PC's reasoning, tips the balance.

### Vote for Voss

The Zahlenhaus becomes a weapon. Within a year, Voss has assembled
the most rigorous statistical case against the mining companies ever
constructed. The numbers are airtight. She presents them to the Stift.
The case grinds through the courts.

The haus's reputation shifts. It's no longer a place of learning —
it's an advocacy institution. Funding from neutral sources dries up.
The mining companies begin a campaign of discreditation. Some scholars
leave, unwilling to work in a politicized environment. The work that
remains is focused, fierce, and narrow.

Whether the case succeeds is beyond the scope of the dungeon. What's
certain is that the Zahlenhaus will never again be what it was.

### Vote for Dael

The Zahlenhaus partners with the Trade Guild. The collaboration is
productive — better models, real applications, measurable improvements
in trade efficiency across the mountain communities. Dael's pricing
work expands. The haus gains funding and prestige.

The partnership also means the haus's research agenda is increasingly
shaped by what the guild needs. Pure mathematics is defunded gradually,
not by malice but by priorities. Hesse retires. His work on prime
distribution is shelved. Aldric's unfinished treatise is filed in the
archive. The haus becomes useful and diminished.

The miners continue to die at the same rate. The trade routes are
more efficient. The prices are fairer. The shafts are still unsafe.
Dael's model doesn't touch the thing that kills people — it only
makes the economy that profits from their deaths run more smoothly.

### Vote for Hesse

Nothing changes. The Zahlenhaus continues to do mathematics. The
scholars who wanted revolution or partnership are disappointed.
Some leave. The haus contracts slightly. Hesse works quietly and
expects others to do the same.

Aldric's treatise advances. Hesse is the only person with the
mathematical background and the institutional patience to continue
it. Whether the treatise, when finished, changes anything — whether
pure knowledge of market cycles can disrupt the systems those cycles
sustain — is a question that won't be answered for decades.

The miners continue to die. The trade routes remain inefficient. The
haus endures. The PC leaves knowing they voted for patience in a
place where people are suffering now, on the faith that understanding
the system is a prerequisite to changing it. It might be true. It
might be the thing scholars tell themselves so they can keep working
while the mountain burns below them.
