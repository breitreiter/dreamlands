# The Occupied Mesa

The Kesharat Administration has pushed into Tashkari territory. Rail lines cut across ancestral passes, depots squat on land that was clan-held for generations, and patrols walk the roads in deliberate formation. The comedy of the borderlands is gone. Here the Kesharat are clearly an occupying force, and they justify it with something they call "alignment with the Lattice"—a phrase delivered with bureaucratic patience, as if expansion into clan country were a public works project that everyone should already understand the wisdom of.

The Tashkari are not strangers to the Kesharat. They are the Kesharat's own breakaway cousins—descendants of Prince Tashkar, who fled the empire generations ago before the domestication took hold. Every charged encounter on this mesa runs on that recognition. The Kesharat look at the Tashkari and see what their grandfathers were: brutal, vital, mortal at thirty in a pointless raid. The Tashkari look at the Kesharat and see what they would have become: polite, productive, glassy-eyed, organizing pencils on a desk in compulsive symmetry. Neither side has the vocabulary for what they are seeing in the other. It comes out as contempt, pity, condescension, fury—never as the thing it actually is.

Water is the flashpoint. Wells and springs that were clan property for generations are now claimed by administrative decree—a piece of paper signed somewhere far away, by someone who has never tasted the water. The rail brings labor crews from elsewhere: people the Tashkari don't recognize and can't negotiate with, working under conditions that are deeply wrong in ways the clans struggle to put into words. Construction is always visible. Every time you pass through, the track is a little farther along. Signal towers go up on hilltops where the clans used to light bonfires for the old gods.

The terrain is broken mesa country—plateaus, dry washes, boulder fields, salt flats, dust. Wind is constant. Days are hot and exposed, nights cold. Navigation is by landmark, and the landmarks are sparse, and some of them belong to someone now. The land produces spices, salt, goat hides, and handcrafted metalwork that both sides want, but the real contest is over infrastructure and control. Two systems of authority overlap here and neither recognizes the other: Tashkari honor-law (oral, kin-based, settled by elders or by violence) and Kesharat procedure (written, jurisdictional, settled by quotas and forms). When they collide there is no court of appeal.

The Tashkari themselves are not unified. They are clans, not a movement. Some have taken Kesharat work—postmen, laborers, translators—because the pay is decent and the horse is free, and they don't think of themselves as collaborators because they aren't, really; they're just delivering letters. Others are isolationists, keeping their heads down and praying the rail line goes somewhere else. A few are guerrillas, fighting Kesharat expansion with ambush and arson, and their tragedy is that they cannot articulate what they are fighting *for*. "Go back to raiding and blood feuds" is not a platform. They know something is wrong but the only alternative they can name is genuinely worse.

The Lattice in tier 2 still mostly sounds like procedure. Civil priests explain it with evangelical patience. Functionaries cite it the way a clerk cites a regulation. The phrase "alignment with the Lattice" gets used to justify a rerouted road, a confiscated well, the relocation of a family for "census correction." Everything they say is internally coherent. None of it quite tracks. Listen long enough and the priest will start to make sense—and that is the warning sign, not the relief.

## Encounters

Territorial tension between Tashkari clans and the Kesharat Administration drives most encounters. Water rights, passage through contested ground, rail construction encroaching on ancestral land, "census correction" disappearances, hospitality used as a loyalty test, coded warnings exchanged between Tashkari who can't be openly seen agreeing with each other. Not every encounter is hostile—some are transactional, some are territorial warnings, some are quiet conversations between people on the wrong side of their own family—but nobody is relaxed.

Encounters should feel tense, exposed, and jurisdictionally contested. They should also, where it can be slipped in sideways, carry the unspoken weight of the Mirror: a Kesharat patrol officer's flicker of unease when a Tashkari woman uses an old salute correctly; a Tashkari elder's contempt when a Kesharat clerk pronounces an ancestral name in the smoothed, balanced modern way. The horror of tier 2 is not yet body horror. It is the horror of watching your own cousin become a stranger in slow motion, and being unable to say what is actually wrong with him.

## Scene Palette

**Characters:** a Tashkari outrider watching from a ridge and deciding whether to challenge you, a Kesharat toll collector who is polite in a way that isn't reassuring, a herder whose well was claimed by administrative decree and who hasn't decided what to do about it, a rail construction overseer tracking progress on a clipboard, a clan elder negotiating passage terms with a face like carved stone, a merchant caught between a Tashkari checkpoint and a Kesharat patrol, a civil priest explaining the Lattice with evangelical patience to a hostile audience, a water-seller with an armed escort and non-negotiable prices, a Kesharat deserter heading the wrong direction with nothing but the clothes on her back, a laborer on the rail line who won't make eye contact, a Tashkari postman in Kesharat colors who insists he is not a collaborator, a guerrilla who can't say what he is fighting *for*, a clan grandmother who still curses the old gods by name and means it, a prefect who pronounces Tashkari names in the smoothed modern way and does not understand why this is an insult, a translator working both sides who has stopped sleeping

**Objects:** a toll cairn with a painted stone meaning "you owe someone," a Kesharat census form in triplicate, a clan banner staked at a territorial boundary, rail spikes and graded earth where track is being laid, a confiscated weapon with an administrative tag, a water skin that's lighter than you'd like, a brass teapot battered enough to have crossed the barrens fifty times, a map scratched into a flat stone at a crossroads, a bundle of dried spice plants tied with cord, signal tower scaffolding half-built on a hilltop where bonfires used to burn, a small wooden idol of one of the old gods, kept hidden, a Kesharat-issued horse that a Tashkari rider is trying very hard not to look proud of, a freshly painted boundary marker where no boundary used to be, a "relocation order" written in the smooth balanced script no clansman is taught to read, a knife with an heirloom hilt and a brand-new administrative serial number filed into the blade

**Sensory:** heat shimmer erasing the horizon, the crunch of mineral crust underfoot, wind that scours exposed skin, the rhythmic clang of rail construction carrying for miles across the plateau, the absolute silence of midday when everything alive is hiding from the sun, the sudden cold when a cloud crosses the sun in high country, the taste of alkaline water, dust that works into everything, the low whistle of wind through a slot canyon, distant voices arguing in two registers of the same language, the wrong-feeling perfect symmetry of a freshly built Kesharat depot, a civil priest's voice that is somehow both patient and tireless, the smell of a goat-fat lamp inside a clan compound that hasn't changed in three generations

**Activities:** negotiating passage through land that belongs to someone, sheltering from midday heat under an overhang, evaluating whether an approaching patrol is clan or Kesharat, rationing water with grim arithmetic, navigating by a landmark that doesn't match the description you were given, sitting through a hospitality ritual that is also a loyalty test, watching rail construction advance across land that was open range last season, wrapping cloth around your face against blowing dust, studying a scratched-stone map and disagreeing about what it means, listening to a civil priest explain the Lattice and feeling it start to make sense, watching a Tashkari postman flinch when an elder calls him by his father's name, refusing a cup of tea in a way that doesn't start a feud, refusing a census form in a way that doesn't start an arrest, helping a clan family bury an idol before a patrol arrives, pretending not to notice that the prefect across the table has the exact same eyes as the woman at the checkpoint who hates him

Tone reference (do not reuse these scenarios): a Tashkari checkpoint refuses passage because the Kesharat have claimed the road ahead and the elder will not say the new name out loud; a civil priest offers to explain the Lattice and seems genuinely hurt when refused, as if you had declined medicine; a Kesharat clerk and a clan grandmother argue over a well, and partway through you realize they are second cousins and neither has acknowledged it
