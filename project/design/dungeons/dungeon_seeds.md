# Dungeon Seeds

Proposals for 4 tier 2 and 1 tier 3 dungeon per biome (25 total).
Each entry has a name, a pitch, and the deeper concept the dungeon is exploring.

Bride's Cave is the quality reference. These seeds should feel grounded in
each biome's specific culture and contradictions — horror that emerges from
how people actually live, not from monsters dropped into a landscape.

---

## Forest

The forest is about freedom scaling with inhumanity. Tier 2 is the exile
zone: displaced people from every biome hiding in the deep wood, trading
paranoia for autonomy. Tier 3 is the hunter — one disciplined person who
has passed all the way through freedom into something post-human.

### Briar Commons (Tier 2)

Three exile camps tried to form a commune in a hidden clearing. They
pooled labor, built shared structures, started a storehouse. It collapsed
within a season — not from violence, but from the realization that anyone
who left the commune knew where everyone else slept. The half-finished
buildings are still standing. So are the people, trapped by mutual
knowledge, unable to leave and unable to trust.

**Deeper concept:** Community requires vulnerability, and vulnerability is
what exile exists to prevent. The commune didn't fail because the exiles
were bad people. It failed because the conditions of their freedom made
cooperation into a hostage situation.

**Hook:** The PC encounters the village leader on the road. She is returning home after a long trip and invites the PC to join her in her commune.

**Clutch:** After the PC witnesses the devastation, they must decide whether to encourage or discourage the village leader from attempting to establish a new commune.

**Asset:** 

### The Warrant Oak (Tier 2)

A massive oak in a clearing where every exile who arrives nails up their
own wanted poster — a bounty notice, a warrant, a letter of disownment —
as proof they're "one of us." The tree is papered with decades of crimes
and accusations. It's become the exile zone's ritual of belonging: you
show what you're running from. Someone has been copying the warrants and
carrying them out.

**Deeper concept:** Identity in exile is defined by the thing that
expelled you. Displaying your crime to prove you belong also makes you
findable. The thing that creates community among exiles — shared fugitive
status — is the same thing that can destroy them, and the ritual
makes it easy.

**Hook:** The PC investigates the tree after hearing rumors about it. When they turn to leave, their path is blocked by a loose semi-circle of ruffians. They press the PC to confess to whatever crime brought them into the forest.

**Clutch:** The PC must decide whether to put themselves at risk to stop this group who preys on vulnerable folks mislead by false rumors. Or go along to get away.

**Asset:** 

### The Forester's Post (Tier 2)

A well-maintained ranger station at the edge of the exile territory,
stocked with supplies, maps, and a coded logbook. The ranger who lives
here has been the exiles' secret benefactor for years — misdirecting
search parties, leaving medicine at dead drops, falsifying reports to the
settled forest. She's just been recalled. The camps don't know yet.

**Deeper concept:** Sustained individual compassion can keep a desperate
situation stable, and stability prevents self-sufficiency. The camps never
organized because one person's secret kindness made it unnecessary. Her
departure will be a crisis — and possibly the first real chance the exiles
have to become a community rather than a collection of dependents.

**Hook:** The PC encounters a group of exiles laying in wait near the station. They say imperial soldiers have come to arrest the ranger, who is a friend of the locals. They mean to murder the soldiers, but are open to the PC negotiating a peaceful outcome.

**Clutch:** Does the PC talk their way out of the situation, help murder the soldiers (to the ranger's horror), or betray the exiles.

**Asset:** 

### Inkwell Hollow (Tier 2)

A quiet camp in the deep wood where a woman has been collecting every
exile's real name and story in a coded journal. She uses actual ink — a
luxury out here — and writes in a careful hand. She says she's preserving
their history so they won't be forgotten. The journal is also, if it ever
leaves the forest, a complete intelligence file: real names, origins,
crimes, locations. She knows this. She keeps writing.

**Deeper concept:** The desire to be remembered and the need to be
invisible are irreconcilable. The journal is simultaneously the most
precious thing in the camps and the most dangerous. The Namekeeper is
giving the exiles back their personhood, and personhood is exactly what
makes them vulnerable.

**Hook:** The camp is in disarray. The woman is trying to explain to a group of angry exiles that she is a scholar. The leader of the group insists she decode her notes. Another member of the group thinks it best the woman be killed and her books burned.

**Clutch:** Who does the PC side with?

**Asset:** 

### The Lodge (Tier 3)

The hunter's dwelling, deep in tier 3. Austere, orderly, maintained with
quiet care. Good tools on pegs. A bed. Dried meat on a rack. And a wall
of small personal objects — a compass, a locket, a child's toy, a letter
never sent — each tagged with a date and a brief description in calm,
warm handwriting. "The one who sang." "The one who almost found the
blind." "The one who prayed." The hunter admired these people. Genuinely.
It just didn't change anything.

**Deeper concept:** Total freedom from obligation allows perfect
perception of others — and perfect indifference to their fate. The hunter
sees beauty in people the way a naturalist sees beauty in a rare bird:
with appreciation, with attention, without kinship. This is the forest's
thesis taken to its conclusion. The most free person in the world is the
one who can watch you clearly, like you, and let you die.

**Hook:** The PC finds the Lodge while lost in tier 3 — the first shelter they've seen in days. The door is open. Inside is warmth, dried meat, a bed. The trophies are on the wall. The journal is on the desk. The hunter is not home. As the PC reads, it becomes clear the hunter has been tracking them since they entered the territory. The most recent journal entry describes the PC.

**Clutch:** The hunter returns. They are calm, unarmed, and unsettlingly warm. They offer the PC food and directions out — the only person who actually knows the safe path. The price is unspoken but legible: the PC leaves, says nothing about the Lodge's location, and becomes another entry in the journal. Or the PC can fight the most competent person in the forest, alone, on their home ground.

**Asset:**

## Plains

The plains are about the violence of scale and systemic indifference. Tier
2 is the failed campaign zone: skeletonized imperial infrastructure,
scavenger guilds, and military remnants guarding nothing. Tier 3 is the
abandoned city and its automated defense grid — machines enforcing
boundaries around a home whose owners quietly left.

### The Metal Beast (Tier 2)

There is a massive metal monster here, frozen in place. Perhaps dead? One of the few imperial kills in their war against the Grid. There is a makeshift rope lander ascending to a hatch on the side of the thing.

**Deeper concept:** The only thing the empire learned from their defeat at the hands of the technologically-superior Grid was that the Grid had cooler toys.

**Hook:** The PC climbs the ladder to investigate. Inside they face various automated security systems before finding a group of imperial engineers, hard at work trying to get the monster running again. They tell the PC about a key part they need and ask the PC to go fetch it. If they can get the thing running, they can push back the Kesharat.

**Clutch:** The PC must decide whether to help these engineers resurrect this cursed robot (granting the empire untold power), openly sabotage their efforts (and risk the anger of the engineers), or try to rig the whole robot to explode.

**Asset:** 

### The Glassway (Tier 2)

A stretch of plain where the ground has been fused to glass by the Grid's
defensive weapons. Beneath the surface, an entire imperial company is
visible: soldiers, horses, supply wagons, preserved mid-stride. Scavengers
have been chipping in from the edges to reach equipment. Deeper in, the
glass reveals something worse — the soldiers are moving. Walking at a pace
measured in years per step. The campaign isn't over for them.

**Deeper concept:** The Grid's weapons didn't kill this army. They removed
it from time. The campaign the heartland mourns as history is still being
fought, in glass, slower than erosion. The soldiers will arrive at their
objective long after everyone who sent them is dust. This is what
"indifference" looks like as a physical force — not cruelty, just a frame
of reference so alien that human urgency becomes geological.

**Hook:** A scavenger crew is working the edge of the glass, chisels set. A woman in scholar's clothes is trying to stop them — she's been studying the field and insists the soldiers beneath the surface are alive, trapped in dilated time. The scavengers are about to crack into a section she's mapped as a high time-differential zone. She has proof: a test crack she made the day before, where exposed grass aged and died in minutes. The crew foreman says his people are starving and the equipment under the glass is the difference between surviving winter and not.

**Clutch:** The equipment the scavengers need — weapons, tools, a supply wagon's worth of metal — sits in the worst part of the differential zone. Cracking the glass there will release centuries of compressed time on the soldiers inside, killing them instantly. The foreman says they've been dead in every way that matters since the weapon fired. The PC decides: help the scavengers crack the glass (feeding the living, killing the frozen), back the scholar and turn the crew away (preserving soldiers no one can ever free, while the crew starves), or help the crew work the low-differential edges for scraps (less equipment, nobody dies, probably not enough to last the winter).

**Asset:** 

### The Harrow Line (Tier 2)

Miles of zigzag campaign trenches, now a mass grave and a salvage economy.
Bodies were left where they fell; the empire advanced over them and then
retreated back over them. Scavengers mine the earthworks for weapons, belt
buckles, insignia. A merchant has set up shop buying the dead soldiers'
effects and selling them back to families in the heartland. Some of the
"identified" possessions are fabricated, because demand exceeds supply.

**Deeper concept:** The empire won't retrieve its dead, but it will buy
them back at retail. The merchant isn't a ghoul — he's performing a
service the state refuses to. But the service creates a market, the market
creates incentives, and the incentives mean some families are mourning over
a stranger's belt buckle and calling it closure. Commerce fills every
vacuum, including grief.

**Hook:** The PC finds the merchant's stall set up at the trench edge, doing brisk business. A woman from the heartland is here with a bundle of letters, trying to match her brother's description to the dead. The merchant has already sold her a belt buckle he claims was her brother's. A scavenger, drunk and bitter, tells the PC the buckle came from a different body entirely. The merchant notices the conversation.

**Clutch:** The merchant pulls the PC aside. He's not defensive — he's tired. The woman's brother is almost certainly in the trench, but there's no way to identify which remains are his. The buckle fits the description. It gives her something to bury. The lie lets her stop searching the earthworks, which are collapsing and have killed two scavengers this season. The scavenger who spoke up wants to lead the woman into the trenches to find the real body — he knows roughly where her brother's unit fell, but that section is structurally unsound. The merchant says the search will likely kill her. The scavenger says she deserves the truth. The woman is asking the PC what she should do.

**Asset:** 

### Grainway Station (Tier 2)

A sealed supply depot from before the retreat, over-provisioned for an
advance that never came. Inside: enough food, weapons, and equipment for a
full company, inventoried and slowly rotting. Both the military remnants
and the scavenger bands claim it. The depot has become the flashpoint for
the entire tier 2 power dynamic. The player arrives during a standoff
where everyone is armed, everyone is hungry, and everyone has a speech
about rightful ownership.

**Deeper concept:** Abundance in a scarcity zone doesn't resolve conflict —
it detonates it. The depot was meant to supply progress; instead it
supplies a power struggle between people the empire forgot. And the food is
spoiling while they argue, because the standoff has lasted longer than
anyone planned for, and admitting that means admitting nobody is in charge.

**Hook:** Both sides want the PC as a neutral arbiter — someone with no prior loyalty to either the military remnants or the scavenger bands. Both sides separately approach the PC with reasonable proposals for dividing the supplies. Both proposals are designed to shaft the other side. Meanwhile the depot stinks of rot.

**Clutch:** The food is spoiling faster than either side admits. The PC can broker a compromise (knowing someone will feel cheated and may retaliate later), pick a side and break the standoff by force, or crack the depot open and let both groups rush in — which will probably turn violent, but at least the food gets eaten before it's gone.

**Asset:** 

### The City (Tier 3)

This is the big reveal, where we show that the evil Grid city was just a normal mercantile city, except from a futuristic culture. We also reveal that the inhabitants left ages ago, not because of any exciting situation. It just became unprofitable to keep the settlement going. They left all their security doo-dads running because turning them off costs more money; landmine type situation. 

The evil defense Grid that laid waste to the imperial legions was a really beefy bug-zapper. It was just doing pest control.

**Deeper concept:** All the stories of imperial valor and their losses holding the line against monsters was a tragic misunderstanding. Some imperial explorers ventured too close to The City, got killed, and the empire sent a legion to take revenge. The Grid killed them all. It ultimately wasn't halted by the legion's bravery, it just decided the pest situation was resolved and headed home.

**Hook:** The PC meets a hardened imperial cartographer outside the city. he's found a way in and is happy to have help infiltrating the enemy base.

The PC and the cartograph go on adventures, discovering strange futuristic things through their renaissance-era eyes. Fighting robots (i mean "golems"). Eating weird canned food. Maybe visiting a shopping mall. Normal stuff.

**Clutch:** Deep in the facility, the PC and the cartographer find evidence that the Grid had figured out the imperial language and bureaucracy. It delivered a message to the general in charge of the legion, apologizing about the whole war situation. The Grid explained it was pulling back its war machines, asking only that the empire keep their distance from The City. The cartographer wants to destroy this evidence, and possibly the PC as well. It contradicts the official imperial history. It means brave men died like vermin for no reason.

**Asset:**

## Mountains

The mountains are about justice and the distance between suffering and the
institutions built to address it. Tier 2 is the scholar zone: remote
institutions on the upper slopes where people who see the whole system
clearly are willing to spend individual lives cheaply in service of
structural change. Tier 3 is the Stift — the cosmic court where justice
becomes comfortable bureaucratic stasis.

### The Ledgerhaus (Tier 2)

A scholar on the upper slopes has dedicated his life to meticulously documenting every injustice visited upon the miners. He has a veritable spy network of informants and contacts who supply him with unexpurgated reports. He has decades of resarch, meticulously organized; foreman cheating miners, shafts known to be unsafe, dubious and forged inspection reports. He is preparing a case which he is certain will entirely upend the mining mercantile class. He just needs a few more years to make sure it is ironclad before travelling to the Stift to argue it. It is a life's work in the shadows.

A miner's widow has climbed the mountain looking for closure on her husband's death. she has followed a veritable spider web of rumors and legends about the shadowy scholar who meticulously documents the suffering of the miners but does nothing. she just wants the honest truth about what happened.

**Deeper concept:** Documentation is not the same as action, and
understanding is not the same as remedy. The world's most complete record
of suffering has helped nobody, because it was built to comprehend
injustice in the abstract, not to help the woman standing in the doorway.
Knowledge that serves only itself is indistinguishable from complicity.

**Hook:** The PC arrives at the Ledgerhaus to find the woman forlorn outside. she has travelled all this way, got into a shouting match with the acolytes, and told to leave. she insists she will remain here until she is allowed to speak to the shadowy scholar. the acolytes insist this is scurrilous hearsay and the woman is deranged.

privately, they are absolutely aware of what's going on and are struggling to come to grips with it. this whole mission is supposed to help the miners, and yet here's a real one and we're treating her like a pariah.

**Clutch:** The archivist offers a compromise: he'll confirm to the widow, verbally, that her husband's death was preventable and documented as such. But no paper leaves the building. The widow says a verbal confirmation is worthless — the same superintendent still runs the mine, and her son works there now. The PC decides: support the archivist's compromise (the widow leaves with words and no proof), slip her the documents (the document gets out, the archivist's case is at risk), or confront the archivist publicly (forcing an institutional crisis that might change the archive's policy, or destroy it).

**Asset:** 

### The Halfway House (Tier 2)

A hostel on the switchback between the mining valleys and the scholar
institutions, run by a woman who was once a miner's wife and is now a
scholar's assistant. She carries petitions uphill and explanations
downhill. Both sides trust her completely. She's been editing the messages —
softening the miners' rage into language the scholars will read, flattening
the scholars' abstractions into advice the miners can use. She's the only
bridge between two kinds of justice, and the bridge is built entirely on
lies.

**Deeper concept:** Translation between incompatible moral frameworks
requires choosing what survives the crossing. The most honest version of
the miners' grief would be illegible to the scholars; the most honest
version of the scholars' analysis would be insulting to the miners.
Diplomacy is the art of productive dishonesty, and the woman who
understands both sides can only serve them by betraying each.

**Hook:** The PC stops at the hostel and finds both a miner delegation and a scholar delegation present. The hostess is engaging in an elaborate ruse to keep the two groups separate. Both groups describe her differently. If confronted, she insists that the groups would quarrel if allowed to meet.

**Clutch:** Does the PC collapse the ruse or allow it to continue?

**Asset:** 

### The Zahlenhaus (Tier 2)

One of the great scholarly houses, this one focused on the study of mathematics and statistics. The headmaster has recently retired to focus on completing his master work and there is lively debate about who will replace him.
- a firebrand who believes the haus should focus on building political theories based on a materialist analysis of markets
- a former member of the Trade Guild, who thinks the haus should focus on identifying market inefficiencies in order to enrich everyone
- an old-fashioned theorist who resents the politicization of the scholars and believes the haus should focus purely on theory

**Deeper concept:** Illustrates the three competing identities for the scholarly class.

**Hook:** The PC is seeking warmth and shelter. The PC is a member of the Trader's Guild and will be immediately feted by the former member upon arrival. Other scholars will see this and the PC will find themselves enmeshed in haus electoral drama whether they like it or not.

**Clutch:** The PC's support ultimately decides the election and the fate of the haus.

**Asset:** 

### The Stift (Tier 3)

Like all tier 3 areas, this is a capstone and we should give it some space to breathe.

The Stift is like a magical Hague. It is a court of final appeal, and the judgments made by the courts are metaphysically binding. However, the wheels of justice grind exceedingly slow. the magistrates are very cautious about using their power and are aware that many mortals will work very hard to trick them into a favorable ruling. This nets out to making the system very slow and the rulings very complex and tepid.

The PC should come to the Stift as a curious tourist and then find themselves wrapped up in a bizarre Gunpowder Plot thing to destroy the Stift.

**Deeper concept:** The Stift isn't corrupt and it isn't broken. It's
working exactly as designed, and the design includes no mechanism for
acknowledging its own failure. Radical change can come from outside, but radical change is inherently destructive. Exploding the Stift will end the injustice, but will not, in itself, create justice.

**Hook:** The PC is doing fun tourist things; visiting the cafes, enjoying the libaries, admiring the striking views from the moutain top. 

They are recruited into a plot. This could be by accident or mix-up. Possibly by subterfuge (the PC is a member of the Trader's Guild, so it's reasonable they could be asked to transport a very important package discretely within the city). It could be an explicit pitch from a conspirator who thinks the PC, as an outsider, would be sympathetic to the cause.

**Clutch:** If the PC decides to go along with the plan, they're either captured by the local guard or succeed in toppling the high court building. if they do succeed, they get to witness the awful consequences of doing so. if they inform the guard, they're recruited into an elaborate counter-espionage thing to lead the guard to to the conspirator's hideout, where a fight ensues.

**Asset:**

## Swamp

The swamp is about memory without narrative. Revënakh — the archiving
organism — preserves everything with perfect fidelity and zero
comprehension. The Revathi, the displaced people who live in the stilt-
villages, are the human-scale version of the same problem: fragments of a
culture without the connective tissue. Tier 2 is the blind-complex, where
the boundary between human memory and inhuman memory blurs. Tier 3 is the
deep swamp, where the entity reconstructs composite figures from
centuries of stored data.

### The Tile House (Tier 2)

A structure in the blind-complex where a Revathi woman has spent years
assembling salvaged tilework fragments, trying to reconstruct a complete
decorative panel from the old cities. Hundreds of pieces laid out on the
floor in overlapping arrangements, each attempt beautiful and wrong. The
swamp has started showing her visions of panels she's never seen — and
her latest arrangements include patterns from those visions, woven in
seamlessly. She can no longer tell which compositions come from her own
knowledge and which Revënakh is feeding her.

**Deeper concept:** Cultural recovery becomes cultural invention when the
original is gone. The swamp's indiscriminate memory offers everything and
authenticates nothing. The woman may be restoring something real, or she
may be hallucinating with borrowed data, and there is no remaining
authority that can tell the difference. Restoration and fabrication are
identical when the standard of truth has been lost.

**Hook:** The PC finds the Tile House and the woman inside, working intently. She's expecting visitors — village elders are coming to evaluate her latest panel, and if they accept it as authentic, it will be installed in the village communal hall as recovered Revathi heritage. She confides in the PC: at least a third of the patterns in the panel came from visions Revënakh showed her. She doesn't know which third. She's frightened, but she can't stop — the work is the most beautiful thing she's ever made.

**Clutch:** The elders arrive and are moved to tears. The panel is extraordinary — it contains motifs no living Revathi has seen, and they feel right in a way the elders can't articulate but recognize in their bones. They declare it authentic. The woman looks at the PC. She hasn't told them. If the PC stays silent, the panel goes to the village and a piece of Revënakh's undiscriminating archive becomes part of Revathi identity — living culture absorbing the swamp's memory, unable to tell the difference. If the PC speaks, the panel is rejected, the woman is discredited, and the most complete piece of Revathi visual culture assembled in generations is abandoned in a shack in the blind-complex because its provenance is impure. The woman whispers: does it matter where beauty comes from, if the people it belongs to recognize it as theirs?

**Asset:** 

### The Drowning Post (Tier 2)

A colonial-era punishment post, half-submerged in the bog. The empire
chained prisoners here at low tide. The Revathi avoid the place. But
Revënakh doesn't — the post is ringed with bioluminescent activity, and at
night, composite figures appear around it, re-enacting the drownings in a
continuous loop. A young revivalist has been coming here to listen, and she
claims the figures are speaking fluent old Revathikh. They are. The words
are the last words of the dying, preserved and replayed without context or
mercy.

**Deeper concept:** The swamp preserves suffering with the same fidelity it
preserves everything else — which is to say, perfectly and without
comprehension. You can recover a dead language from the mouths of the dead,
and the grammar will be impeccable, and the content will be agony. The
revivalist wants the language. The language comes packaged in the worst
moments of the people who spoke it. You can't separate the two.

**Hook:** The PC comes across the Drowning Post at dusk. The bioluminescent figures are active, and a young revivalist is seated among them with a wax tablet, transcribing. She's been here for weeks and her work is extraordinary — she's reconstructed verb conjugations, possessive forms, a vocabulary of several hundred words of old Revathikh. She's also visibly unwell: thin, pale, not sleeping. A Revathi elder has tracked her here and is trying to convince her to leave. She refuses. She says she's close to recovering enough of the language to teach it.

**Clutch:** The elder pulls the PC aside. The language is real — but it's drawn entirely from the last words of people who were chained to the post and drowned. The grammar is perfect. Every sentence is a death. If the revivalist teaches this to the villages, the first generation of new speakers will be speaking a language whose entire recovered vocabulary is composed of begging, prayer, and despair. The elder says a language of suffering will shape how the speakers think and who they become. The revivalist says a language of suffering is still a language, and her people have been silent long enough. The elder asks the PC to destroy the transcription tablets. The revivalist asks the PC to protect them. The figures at the post keep speaking, indifferent to what anyone decides.

**Asset:** 

### The Listening Blind (Tier 2)

One of the few structurally sound reception blinds in the complex — a
stone chamber aligned to help trained interpreters sit, listen, and sort
what Revënakh shows. A small group of Revathi elders has been using it for
its original purpose, and it's working: they've recovered fragments of
genealogies, songs, craft techniques. But one interpreter has gone silent.
She sits in the blind, eyes open, breathing steady, responding to touch
but not to speech. Three weeks now. The others say she's receiving
something large and needs time. They won't pull her out because what she's
receiving might be irreplaceable.

**Deeper concept:** The reception tradition works, and that is precisely
the danger. The line between receiving the swamp's memory and being
consumed by it is a question of discipline, and discipline is finite. The
silent interpreter may be on the edge of recovering something the Revathi
have mourned for generations. Or she may already be gone, and the others
are letting her die because the possibility of what she holds is worth
more to them than the certainty of who she is.

**Hook:** The silent interpreter's son finds the PC and begs for help. The elders at the blind have turned him away — they say his mother is doing sacred work and must not be disturbed. He doesn't believe them. He thinks she's dying. He asks the PC to help him get past the elders and reach her.

**Clutch:** The PC enters the blind and reaches the interpreter. She responds to touch — her hand tightens around the PC's wrist. She's trying to communicate something. The elders insist this means she's close to finishing and must not be moved. The son insists she's asking for help. A thîkërevas examines her and says honestly: both interpretations are equally possible, and the only way to know is to wait or to pull her out. Pulling her out will either save her life or destroy what she was receiving. Waiting will either complete the recovery or let her die in the chair. The son is looking at the PC.

**Asset:** 

### The Revënakh (Tier 3)

A palace of organic material extruded by the living earth and shaped to resemble one of the towering, mighty, and long-long lost Revathi cities. The wet walls ripple with an every-changing mosaic. Possibly a perverse imitation of the Revathi tiles. Or worse, the Revathi tiles are a simplified expression of whatever this is.

Someone the PC cared about, but lost, is here. Perhaps a parent or mentor. This thing is clearly a monstrosity, but it replicates all the best patterns from the PCs memory. It is a living thing, in a very literal sense of "the dead live on in our memories." They live on in the Revënakh as well, apparently.

**Deeper concept:** The Revënakh feels the longing of the Revathi and the player. It has manifested this palace and the loved one in response. It will manifest feasts and sumptuous rooms and musicians and poets. It is only a mirror, though. These things only have whatever meaning is projected by the viewer.

**Hook:** The PC finds the creature-palace while exploring the swamp. There is a fleeting figure in a window and they feel a powerful sense of recognition. At first they think the building is just overgrown, but come to realize it's alive.

The challenge here will be explaining the weird nature of the Revënakh (it's a living mega-organism which has perfect memory but no understanding of time or chronology. it's intelligent in a way we can't understand. we think it's communicating because it's clearly very powerful, but it's just reflecting a melange of historical impressions, driven largely by the observer)

**Clutch:** The PC has a few available outs:
- Just ride the wave. Embrace Revënakh thought. Just enjoy the experience for what it is, don't overthink it, fall asleep in a warm bed, awake tomorrow lost in the swamp but otherwise feeling pretty great.
- Be a little bitch. If the PC is quarrelsome or cruel to the doppleganger or insists on trying to smash things, the Revënakh just blindly reponds with violence. You've tuned the TV to the violence channel. I hope you like violence, because this thing can remember a WHOLE LOT of it.
- Ask more questions. And more. The doppleganger sounds really, really reasonable and smart and just like the person you remember, except they talk in circles and you can't pin them down on anything. You waste a perfectly nice day being a nerd.

**Asset:**

## Scrub (High Mesa Corridor)

The scrub is about kinship versus administration under an alien cosmic
order. Plateau clans run on honor, hospitality, and blood. The Kesharat
Administration runs on census, rail, and alignment with the Lattice — an
extra-dimensional intelligence that colonizes minds and reshapes
institutions into instruments of its expansion. Tier 2 is where the
Kesharat is an occupying force and the comedy of tier 1 has dropped away.
Tier 3 is where the Lattice is overt and the Kesharat become a little
sympathetic again, because they're victims too.

### Wellhead Station (Tier 2)

A Kesharat-built water distribution facility constructed over a spring
that a local clan controlled for generations. The new infrastructure is
objectively better: pumps, pipes, a schedule that ensures every settlement
in the area receives water. More efficient and more equitable than the old
arrangement. But the clan that held the spring has lost its primary source
of sovereignty and identity. During a drought, the schedule doesn't
change — and the question of who gets cut first reveals what "equitable"
means when the Lattice's priorities are the ones being served.

**Deeper concept:** Efficiency and justice aren't the same thing, and a
system can be objectively better at distributing a resource while being
objectively worse at everything that resource used to mean — sovereignty,
obligation, the binding power of hospitality. The Kesharat improved the
water and destroyed the well. When scarcity returns, "fair" serves
expansion, not the people the well once belonged to.

**Hook:** The PC arrives at the station during a drought. A clan delegation is demanding the Kesharat functionary change the water schedule to prioritize their settlements — they're closer to the spring and held it for generations. The functionary shows them the schedule: it's equitable, it's rational, and it allocates a share to a new Kesharat construction site downriver that nobody in the room knew existed.

**Clutch:** The construction site is a rail extension — the water is being used to mix concrete for foundations that will push Kesharat infrastructure deeper into clan territory. The schedule is "fair" only if you count the rail project as a legitimate recipient. Does the PC sabotage the station (returning the water to the clan but destroying the only equitable distribution infrastructure in the region), expose the hidden allocation (guaranteeing a violent confrontation), or broker a compromise that legitimizes the rail extension in exchange for clan concessions they'll regret in a year?

### The Relay Post (Tier 2)

A Kesharat communications station where a dutiful operator transcribes
decrees that arrive from the capital. Or from somewhere. The messages
used to be normal administrative traffic — border adjustments, resource
quotas, personnel transfers. Lately they've been strange: instructions
for buildings that serve no obvious purpose, requests for materials that
don't seem to exist, coordinates pointing to empty desert.

There is also an elderly bureaucrat here who is part priest, part functionary. He makes a case for The Lattice.

**Deeper concept:** The Lattice communicates by hijacking human systems.
It doesn't issue divine pronouncements; it sends memos. The horror isn't
that an alien intelligence is giving orders. The horror is that the orders
look exactly like normal paperwork, and you cannot tell — and may never
be able to tell — where human administration ends and alien will begins.
The operator's realization isn't that the system is corrupted. It's that
she can't determine when the corruption started, or whether it was ever
absent.

**Hook:** The PC shelters at the relay post during a dust storm. It's likely to last the night.

Also present is the relay operator an the old bureaucrat. Things start on a light note, with the operator mentioning how absurd recent requests down the wire have been. She points out a few recent, truly bizarre requests. 

the bureaucrat counters, explaining the political philosophy of the lattice and the way that all things are interconnected and how the people in charge understand the great lattice in a deep way. we should not expect to understand them, and the fact that we can't is actually evidence for how correct they are.

The banter goes on a bit but starts to take a sharp tone. the bureaucrat is clearly trying to not embarass his culture in front of the outlander. the relay operator is frustrated at the gaslighting. Everyone agrees to amicably break for the evening.

**Clutch:** Things take a dark turn at night. The operator privately confides in the PC that she thinks the people who are giving orders have gone insane. She wants to leave at first light and make for Aldgate. She asks the PC to write and seal a letter of introduction to the Trader's Guild.
The old man privately finds the PC and explains that he thinks the operator is plotting something. He is too old to confront the woman, but with the PCs help, he could arrest her and keep her until soldiers come. He's smart enough to keep a lid on the lattice talk and just presents this in a very secular way. She's a state employee who handles sensitive information and she's clearly not loyal.

### The Census House (Tier 2)

A Kesharat administrative office where a young functionary has compiled
the most thorough record of clan genealogies ever assembled. She's polite,
diligent, and genuinely fascinated by kinship structures. Her census maps
every family, every alliance, every marriage debt, every vulnerability.
It's a masterpiece of ethnography. It's also, to the Lattice, a targeting
document — every relationship charted, every pressure point identified.
She doesn't know this. She thinks she's doing scholarship.

**Deeper concept:** Knowledge gathered in good faith serves whoever holds
it, and the collector doesn't get to choose the use. The functionary is
a scholar, not a soldier. The work she does out of sincere curiosity will
be used for systematic subjugation by an intelligence that thinks in
networks and sees kinship as a topology to be optimized. Good intentions
are perfectly irrelevant when the institution you serve is parasitic.

**Hook:** The PC is in the area when a clan raid hits the Census House. The raiders want to burn the records. The functionary is hiding in a back room. The raiders aren't killers — they're a clan elder and his sons, frightened of what the records represent. They're willing to let the functionary go unharmed if the books burn.

**Clutch:** The functionary begs the PC to save the census — it's her life's work and it's irreplaceable scholarship. The clan elder explains, calmly, that every name in those books is a throat the Kesharat can cut whenever they choose. He's not wrong. The records are both things at once: the most beautiful ethnography of a living culture and a precision instrument for its destruction. Does the PC let them burn?

### The Foundry (Tier 3)

A Kesharat industrial facility deep in tier 3 territory where something
unprecedented is being manufactured. Workers feed raw materials into
machinery — stone, metal, glass — and what comes out is none of those
things: crystalline structures that pulse with the Colors, that grow
when unobserved, that hum at frequencies the body feels before the ear
hears. The overseers say the structures are "nodes" — components of a
network. When enough nodes are placed across the mesa, the Lattice will
be able to perceive the entire region directly, without human
intermediaries. The foundry is building the infrastructure for the
Lattice to stop needing people.

**Deeper concept:** The Lattice isn't trying to rule humanity — it's
trying to outgrow it. The foundry is where the endgame becomes legible:
a world of perfectly aligned geometry with no requirement for the messy
biological substrate that currently hosts the entity's will. The true
believers think they're building paradise. They're building their own
obsolescence. And the workers, bodies faintly glowing with that
indescribable Color, are already closer to nodes than to people.

**Hook:** The facility is operating openly. Workers don't resist. Overseers are polite. The PC is welcomed as a visitor and offered a tour. Everything is explained clearly and transparently. The horror is that it all makes perfect sense in the Lattice's terms.

**Clutch:** Deep in the foundry, the PC finds someone they recognize — or someone connected to an earlier quest — among the workers. They're not chained. They volunteered. They explain, with calm conviction and genuine warmth, that the work is important and they understand now what the Lattice is trying to build. They ask the PC to join them. Their skin is faintly luminous with the Colors. They are happy. Does the PC try to extract them by force (against their stated will), accept their choice and leave, or destroy the foundry (killing everyone inside, including the willing)?
