# Traverse Encounter Model

The stalker-in-the-zone fantasy: you're looking at a field of hazards, you can see a path through, you plan your route — but you might not be able to afford the path you planned, and then you're improvising.

## What carries over from combat

- Momentum (+1/turn), Spirits (non-renewable), Resistance (terrain remaining)
- Openings with cost/effect pairings
- Press the Advantage (-2 Momentum) and Force an Opening (-2 Spirits) to fish for better options
- One action per turn
- Timers as recurring environmental threats
- Same three endings: Resistance kill (made it through), Control kill (all hazards neutralized), Spirits loss

## The big difference: the visible queue

In combat, you see one opening and decide blind. In traverse, you can see ahead.

At the start of the encounter, generate a **queue** of upcoming openings (5-6 deep). The player can see the full queue. Each turn, the front opening is your current position — take it or spend the turn on Press the Advantage/Force an Opening.

After you take the front opening, the queue advances. A new opening is generated and added to the back to maintain depth. No skipping — if you don't want the front opening, you pay for Press the Advantage or Force an Opening.

No approach selection. Everyone plans to the best of their ability before crossing hazardous terrain. Starting Momentum, timer count, and queue depth are set by the encounter.

### What the queue gives you

**Planning.** You can see that three turns from now there's a free stop-timer opening. But the two turns before it both cost Momentum you don't have. Do you Force an Opening now to rewrite the front of the queue, or take a bad opening to conserve Spirits and hope Momentum builds fast enough?

**Legibility.** The queue IS the anomaly field. You're scanning the terrain, picking your footing. The openings ahead are the landmarks you're navigating toward.

### Where it goes sideways

The plan breaks down not because the queue changes, but because **you can't afford what's in it**. Timers drain your Spirits. An expensive opening depletes your Momentum. Now the beautiful path you planned is sitting right there, and you can't pay for it.

This is the uber-driver-who-can't-make-car-payments tension. You can see the good opening. It's right there, two turns away. But you're short 2 Momentum, so you Press the Advantage — which burns your turn, ticks the timers, and next turn you get 3 ephemeral openings to pick from. If one of them sets you up, great. But the queue hasn't moved — the opening you were planning around is still sitting there, one turn further away, and that timer just ticked.

Or you ignore the planned path entirely and yolo: take whatever the front opening is, even if it's suboptimal, because at least you're making progress and the timers are ticking.

## Press the Advantage/Force an Opening in traverse

Works identically to combat: spend 2 Momentum or 2 Spirits, end your turn, receive 3 openings next turn. Pick one of the three, then the queue resumes normally.

The queue is stable — it never changes. Press/Force gives you a detour with better options, then you come back to the same path. This means the queue is always a reliable planning surface. You know exactly what's waiting for you.

## Timers in traverse

Environmental hazards rather than enemy actions:

- **Spirits drain** — "Crumbling ledge", "Rising water", "Biting cold" — the terrain is physically punishing. Drains Spirits when it fires, resets.
- **Resistance recovery** — "Shifting terrain", "Landslide" — the path gets longer. Adds Resistance when it fires, resets.

No queue corruption mechanic. The queue is stable — it's the terrain, it doesn't move. What changes is your ability to afford the path through it. Timer pressure is on your resources, which makes the planned path unaffordable. That's the disruption.

## Difficulty levers

No approach means the encounter itself sets the parameters:

| Parameter | Easy | Medium | Hard |
|---|---|---|---|
| Starting Momentum | 3 | 2 | 0 |
| Spirits | 10 | 10 | 10 |
| Resistance | 6 | 8 | 10 |
| Timer count | 2 | 3 | 4 |
| Queue depth | 6 | 5 | 4 |

Queue depth is the big one. Longer queue = more planning = easier to find a route. Shorter queue = more blind spots = more improvisation.

## Opening flavor in traverse

Same mechanical structure (cost + effect), different flavor:

**Costs** read as "what the terrain demands":
- Momentum cost: requires quick timing, momentum
- Spirits cost: physically exhausting to attempt
- Tick a timer: the detour costs time, a hazard advances
- Free: the path is clear here

**Effects** read as "what you gain from this stretch":
- Damage Resistance: make progress through the terrain
- Stop Timer: neutralize an environmental hazard permanently
- Gain Momentum: find good footing, build momentum

## Key difference from combat

Combat is reactive — you're in the moment, reading your opponent, fishing for opportunities. Traverse is proactive — you can see the path, you have a plan, and the drama is in watching that plan become unaffordable and deciding how to adapt.

The mechanical difference is minimal (queue visibility). The experiential difference is large.
