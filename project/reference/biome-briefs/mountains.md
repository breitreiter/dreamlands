# Mountains — Trade Brief

## Culture
Three-tier society organized around justice and distance from suffering.

**Lowlands**: Salt-of-the-earth mining folk — trappers, miners, charcoal burners. Appalachian-coded. Their moral world is relational: loyalty, kinship, community. They measure a person by whether you show up when someone's roof collapses. They don't trust law; they trust memory. Independent ridge families vs. company towns in the valleys — debt paid in scrip, strikes simmering.

**Upper slopes**: Remote scholarly institutions — monasteries, academies, observatories. Swiss-German institutional culture. They see the whole system clearly and get upset about abstract injustice. They view individual suffering with stoic detachment. Miners think scholars are heartlessly detached; scholars think miners are heartbreakingly short-sighted. Both are correct.

**Peak**: A court of cosmic authority — basically magic Hague on a mountain. Endless bureaucratic procedure for resolving grievances. Petitioners arrive with fury and are gradually domesticated into long-term residents who socialize with their adversaries. Tea houses, bakeries, a small market square. Everything is hauled up at great expense.

## Material Culture
- Weapons: tools that serve as weapons — picks, axes, knives. Nothing ornate.
- Armor: leather, quilted, minimal. Nothing military or noble.
- Foods: game, preserved meat, root vegetables, grain where the land allows. Company towns: provisions controlled, scrip economy. Peak: everything hauled up — tea, bread, paper, cloth.

## Trade Profile
- Produces: mineral wealth (ore, coal), timber, charcoal, labor.
- Hungers for: metal goods (tools, nails), salt, luxuries from below, books, paper, ink, supplies for scholarly life.

## People & Tone
- Lowlands: Appalachian naming (Arlen, Boone, Delia, Cora). Soft but clipped speech. Slate gray, weathered wood, coal black, autumn red.
- Scholars: Swiss-German naming (Urs, Margrit, Kaspar). Austere, functional, serious.
- Peak: bureaucratic, procedural, comfortable. The system works by never quite finishing.
