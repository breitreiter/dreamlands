VOICE AND TONE GUIDE
====================================

This guide describes the prose style for Dreamlands encounters. It
exists to keep writing consistent across arcs and to prevent common
LLM failure modes (over-description, bizarre metaphors, emotional
narration). Read this before writing or editing encounter prose.


THE VOICE
------------------------------------

The narrator is a competent professional observing unfamiliar places.
They notice what things look like, how they're built, what they might
be for. They do not narrate their own emotions. They do not reach for
poetic language. They describe what is in front of them with the
precision of someone writing a field report that happens to be
well-written.

This is not sparse or minimalist writing. It is *specific* writing.
There is plenty of detail — but every detail is something the
narrator could actually see, touch, or hear. No abstractions, no
atmosphere-for-atmosphere's-sake.

    Good:  "A sleeping surface built into the wall like a shelf,
            wide enough for one person. A recess below it that
            serves as storage."

    Bad:   "The room whispered of centuries of solitary rest,
            its alcove bed a testament to the austere devotion
            of its former occupant."

The first version tells you the shape, size, and function. The second
tells you nothing and sounds like a museum placard.


DESCRIPTION THROUGH FUNCTION
------------------------------------

Describe what things are *for*, not what they look like in the
abstract. The reader builds the image from the function.

    Good:  "Flat surfaces at working height, recessed basins with
            drainage channels cut into the floor beneath them."

    Bad:   "Smooth countertops gleamed dully in the lantern light,
            their surfaces marred by deep grooves that channeled
            some unknown substance toward mysterious drains."

The first version: you're in a kitchen. The second version: you're
in a gothic novel.

When the narrator encounters something alien, they describe it by
the closest functional analogue they know, then note where the
analogy breaks down:

    "A row of dark panels set into the wall that might be heating
     elements or might be something else. It reads as a kitchen,
     and everything is where you'd expect it. Nothing is a size
     or shape you'd expect it to be."

This is how real people process unfamiliar environments: pattern
match, then notice the gaps.


WHAT THE NARRATOR DOES NOT DO
------------------------------------

**No emotional narration.** The narrator does not tell the reader
what they feel. If a moment is sad, the reader should feel it from
the situation, not from the prose telling them it's sad.

    Good:  "Torben is very quiet. When he speaks, his voice is
            level and careful."

    Bad:   "A heavy silence fell between you, weighted with the
            enormity of what you'd just learned."

**No metaphor unless it earns its place.** A simile that clarifies
a physical detail is fine ("curves away like a shelf"). A metaphor
that exists to sound literary is not. If you catch yourself reaching
for a metaphor, ask: does this help the reader see the thing? If
not, cut it.

    Fine:  "The dome wall curves away in both directions, close
            enough now to see the surface clearly. Not glass —
            something layered, something that catches the light
            wrong."

    Cut:   "The dome loomed like a frozen wave, its surface a
            kaleidoscope of refracted memories."

**No dramatic narration of action.** Combat and physical action are
described briefly, in concrete terms. No slow motion. No blow-by-blow
choreography. No "time seemed to slow."

    Good:  "His sword finds a joint in the frame, between the
            chased golden plates, and something inside the figure
            sparks and grinds."

    Bad:   "With lightning reflexes, Torben's blade sang through
            the air, finding the hairline gap between two plates
            of ancient armor. Sparks erupted like tiny stars."

**No thesaurus words.** Use the plain word. "Said" not "intoned."
"Walked" not "strode." "Looked" not "regarded." The narrator is
literate but not performatively so.

**No weather and atmosphere padding.** Don't open scenes with wind,
clouds, temperature, or light conditions unless they're mechanically
relevant or establish something the player needs to know. "The air
changes as you rise, drier, thinner" tells you about the building.
"A chill wind swept across the desolate plains" tells you nothing.


DIALOG
------------------------------------

Characters speak the way professionals speak: short, direct,
informed by what they know. Nobody monologues. Nobody explains
things the other person already knows.

**Voice comes from expertise.** Torben sees a garrison because he's
a military cartographer. The player sees a trading floor because
they're Guild. Sev is curt because he's old and has no patience for
ceremony. Every character's dialog should reveal what they're good
at and how they see the world.

    Torben:  "Nightmare to assault. Tight corridors, no sight
              lines, a hundred places to put an ambush."

    Player:  "The desks face the counter. The counter faces the
              waiting area. There's a loading bay in the back
              with inventory controls and crate storage."

Both characters are describing the same environment. The difference
is the lens.

**Emotion in dialog is understated.** When Torben processes that the
war was a misunderstanding, he says "That much death has to mean
something." One line. Then he asks for the letter. Characters don't
deliver speeches about their feelings. They say one true thing and
move on.

    Good:  "I can't promise anything. You know that, right?"

    Bad:   "I want you to understand that this weighs heavily on
            me. The burden of truth is not one I take lightly,
            but I owe it to the memory of those who served."

**No accents or dialect spelling.** Don't write "ye" or "aye" or
phonetic speech. Differentiate characters through vocabulary,
sentence length, and what they choose to talk about. Dalla is rough
because she uses short blunt sentences about practical things, not
because she drops her g's.


CHARACTERS AND MOTIVATION
------------------------------------

Every NPC exists because they want something. Before writing a
character's dialog or actions, know:

1. What do they want right now? (immediate goal)
2. What do they want long-term? (what drives them)
3. What are they afraid of? (what constrains them)

Torben wants to map the city (immediate), wants to serve the empire
(long-term), and is afraid the truth will unmake the meaning of his
father's death (constraint). Every line he speaks is consistent with
all three.

The Envoy wants to deliver a letter (immediate), was built to serve
a diplomatic function (long-term), and has no fear because it's a
machine following protocol. It fights like "a door that's been told
not to open" — mechanical, procedural, escalating. Its last words
are about the undelivered correspondence, not about self-preservation.

**NPCs are not quest dispensers.** They don't exist to give the
player things to do. They exist because they were already doing
something when the player showed up. The player is interrupting
their life, not the other way around.

**No villains, no heroes.** Characters act according to their
interests and beliefs. Torben wanting to destroy the letter isn't
villainy — it's a man protecting the meaning of a thousand deaths
including his father's. Write characters whose positions you can
defend even when the player opposes them.


THE PLAYER CHARACTER
------------------------------------

The player character is a Trade Guild merchant. They are competent,
observant, and practical. They notice things through the lens of
commerce, logistics, and negotiation. They have traveled widely and
seen a lot. They are not naive, not a chosen one, not a blank
cipher.

When the player character has an insight, ground it in their
professional experience:

    "You've stood at a desk like this. Not this desk, not in this
     city, not built by whatever built this place — but a desk that
     faced a counter that faced a floor where people waited."

The player character can be wrong. They can miss things that a
soldier or a scholar would catch. Their Guild perspective is a lens,
not omniscience — and other characters may reasonably dismiss it as
bias.

Don't give the player character knowledge they wouldn't have. They
know trade, travel, people, and practical survival. They don't know
military tactics, ancient history, or engineering unless they've
learned it in-arc.

**IMPORTANT** Unless stated explicitly otherwise:
- The PC has never met any NPCs previously
- The PC has never visited a location previously
- The PC has no pack animals, companions, or vehicles
- The PC will not promise to perform an action in a distant location. This includes retrieving an item, delivering an item, relaying a message, summoning help, etc.


PACING AND DENSITY
------------------------------------

**One idea per paragraph.** Each paragraph describes one thing, one
action, or one exchange. If you're combining the room description
with a character's reaction with a flashback, break it up.

**Front-load the concrete, end with the implication.** Describe what
the player sees first, then (optionally) what it suggests.

    "The containers that remain are marked to match. It's a system
     for knowing what's where and how much of it you have. It is,
     in every respect that matters, an inventory."

The physical detail comes first. The interpretation comes last. The
reader has already built the image before the narrator names it.

**Short scenes hit harder.** The Defeated by the Envoy scene is
eight lines of narration and one choice. The Letter scene is the
emotional climax of the entire arc and it's a page. Don't pad
scenes to feel substantial. Let them be as long as they need to be
and no longer.

**Choice body text scales with significance.** A self-linking dialog
choice might be 3-4 paragraphs. A choice that resolves the arc
might be 6-8. A hub navigation choice might be 1-2. The length
should match what's happening, not a target word count.


HANDLING MYSTERY AND THE UNKNOWN
------------------------------------

The world contains things that are genuinely alien. The narrator
does not understand them and does not pretend to. The technique is
to describe precisely what is observed and let the mystery emerge
from the gap between what is seen and what is understood.

    "Panels line the walls floor to ceiling, dark surfaces broken
     by faint lines of light that pulse at intervals too slow to
     be mechanical and too regular to be random."

The narrator doesn't say "alien technology hummed with unknowable
purpose." They say: it pulses, here's the timing, here's why that
timing is weird. The reader does the rest.

**Never explain the mystery.** Don't tell the reader what the
ancient builders intended, what the technology does, or what the
symbols mean. Describe what it looks like and how people react to
it. Let characters offer *interpretations* that may be wrong.

**Characters disagree about the unknown.** Torben sees a military
installation. The player sees a trading post. Neither is confirmed.
This is how real people process the unfamiliar — through the
frameworks they already have — and it's more interesting than a
narrator who knows the answer.


AUTHORIAL TOUCHSTONES
------------------------------------

These are the sensibilities to channel, not styles to imitate.

**Jack Vance** — The travelogue quality. An observant outsider
moving through cultures that operate on their own logic, describing
what he sees with wry precision. The narrator is always slightly
outside the thing he's observing, and the prose is richer for the
distance.

**Arkady and Boris Strugatsky (Roadside Picnic)** — Encountering
artifacts built by minds that didn't think like yours. The Zone
isn't explained. People interact with it through their own needs
and limitations. The alien stays alien.

**Gene Wolfe (Book of the New Sun)** — Describing advanced or
unfamiliar things through a narrator who lacks the vocabulary for
them. A "sleeping surface built into the wall like a shelf" is a
Wolfe move — the thing is described by analogy to something simpler
because the narrator doesn't have the right word for it.

**Patrick O'Brian** — Professional competence as characterization.
People reveal themselves through how they do their jobs, not through
introspection. Dialog is clipped and informed by expertise. Emotion
is expressed through restraint.

**Cormac McCarthy (The Road, Suttree)** — Concrete, sensory detail
without sentimentality. The prose trusts the situation to carry the
emotional weight. "He looked at the scorched bolt on the ground,
and the door, and the man who's spent three years trying to open
it" — no commentary, just the image.

What these have in common: the prose respects the reader's
intelligence, the narrator observes more than they explain, and
emotional weight comes from situation and restraint rather than
from the language itself.


COMMON LLM FAILURE MODES TO AVOID
------------------------------------

These are specific patterns that language models fall into. Watch
for them in generated text and cut them ruthlessly.

**Purple atmosphere.** "The ancient corridors seemed to breathe with
forgotten memories, each stone whispering secrets of a civilization
lost to the relentless march of time." Delete all of this. Describe
the corridor.

**Emotional stage directions.** "A wave of sadness washed over you."
"You felt a chill that had nothing to do with the temperature."
"Something stirred deep within you." The reader's emotions are not
yours to direct. Describe the situation and trust it.

**Redundant metaphor pairs.** "The silence was deafening, a void
that swallowed all sound." Pick one or pick neither. Usually neither.

**Faux-profound closing lines.** "And in that moment, you understood
that some doors, once opened, can never be closed." "The city had
given you its answer — but the question had changed." These are
empty. End scenes on concrete action or concrete image.

**Thesaurus escalation.** Using increasingly exotic words for the
same thing across a passage. The door is a "portal" then an
"aperture" then a "threshold." It's a door. Call it a door.

**Invented sensory details.** "The metallic tang of ancient air."
"A subsonic vibration you felt in your teeth." "The smell of ozone
and something older." If you can't explain what it actually smells
or sounds like, don't invent a sensation. Silence is fine. No smell
is fine.

**Narrating the obvious.** "You realize this must be important." If
the player is choosing to examine something, they already know it's
important. Don't narrate the act of paying attention.

**Restating choice outcomes.** After a skill check, don't write
"Your combat training pays off" or "Your skills prove insufficient."
Describe what happens. The reader knows whether they succeeded.
